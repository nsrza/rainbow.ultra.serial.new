# PART ONE: THE THREADS

---

## CHAPTER 1: THE SEEING

The threads were always there.

That's what Lifan would realize later, looking back. They'd been there when he was born, probably. There in the hospital room, connecting his mother to the nurses, to his father, to him. Invisible spider silk holding the world together.

But he didn't *see* them until he was six.

It was autumn. He remembers because of the persimmons. His mother had bought a whole bag from the Asian market, and they sat on the kitchen counter in their plastic mesh, glowing orange in the afternoon light. She was showing him how to check if they were ripe—the Fuyu kind, not Hachiya, she said, you could eat these ones hard or soft—when the kitchen did something weird.

Everything got brighter. Sharper. Like someone had turned up the contrast on the whole world.

And then he saw them.

Golden threads, streaming from his mother's chest. Thousands of them. Millions. They spread out in every direction, disappearing through walls, through the ceiling, through the floor. Some were thick as rope. Others were gossamer-thin, barely visible even now that he could see them. They pulsed with light, breathing in and out, alive.

"Māma?" His voice came out small.

She looked at him. Really looked. And her face did this thing—surprise, then understanding, then something that looked almost like grief.

"Oh, bǎobèi," she said softly. "You can see them now."

He nodded. He couldn't look away from the threads. They were beautiful and terrible and so, so bright.

His mother set down the persimmon. She knelt in front of him, and the threads swayed with her movement like seaweed in a current.

"Lifan. Look at me. Not at the threads. At me."

It was hard. The threads wanted his attention. But he focused on her face—her dark eyes, the small mole beside her left eyebrow, the silver starting to show in her black hair.

"Good," she said. "Do you know what you're seeing?"

He shook his head.

"In Chinese, we call them *yuánfèn*. It means... destiny's thread. The connections between people. Most people can't see them. But you can. Like I can."

"You see them too?" Relief flooded through him. He wasn't going crazy.

"Yes. My mother could see them. Her mother before her. It runs in our family, sometimes. A gift."

"What are they for?"

His mother smiled, but it was sad. "They show us how people are connected. See?" She pointed to a thick golden cord that ran from her chest straight to his. "This is us. Mother and son. This thread is strong. It will never break."

Lifan looked down at his own chest. Sure enough, threads emerged from somewhere behind his ribs. Fewer than his mother's—he was only six, he didn't know as many people—but they were there. The one connecting him to his mother blazed brighter than all the others.

"Do they go to everyone?" he asked.

"Everyone you've ever touched, in some way. Everyone who's touched you. Some connections are stronger than others. Some are brief—you help someone pick up dropped groceries, there's a little thread, thin as spider silk. It fades quickly. Others last forever."

She pointed to another thick thread, this one running toward the front of the house. "That goes to your father, at work. See how it's still strong, even though he's far away?"

Lifan squinted. He could see it, sort of. The thread disappeared through the wall, heading east toward downtown Sacramento where his father worked at the bank.

"What about thin threads?" he asked. "Like... like those?"

He pointed to a cluster of gossamer-thin strands trailing from his mother's heart. They looked different from the others. Faded. Some of them seemed to be... floating? Rising upward?

His mother's expression shifted. "Those are connections to people who've died."

"They don't break?"

"No, bǎobèi. Threads don't break. They change. When someone dies, their thread rises. It goes... somewhere else. But it never breaks. Love doesn't break."

Lifan stared at the rising threads. There were more than he'd expected. His mother knew dead people. A lot of them.

"Can I touch them?" he asked.

"You can try."

He reached out toward one of the threads connecting his mother to him. His fingers passed right through it. No sensation at all. Just light.

"They're not physical," his mother said. "They're *qì*—energy. Life force. You can see them because you're sensitive to that kind of energy. But you can't touch them. Not with your hands."

"How do I touch them, then?"

His mother paused. She looked at him for a long moment, and he could see her thinking, deciding something.

"There are ways," she said finally. "Ways to interact with threads. To strengthen them, sometimes. To... to do other things. But Lifan, you have to promise me something first."

"Okay."

"You can't tell people about this. Not yet. Not until you're older, until you understand better. People who can't see threads... they won't understand. They'll think you're lying, or confused, or sick. Do you understand?"

He nodded. He understood about not telling. He'd learned about that in kindergarten when Tyler Chang said he could fly and all the other kids laughed at him.

"Good. Now, let me teach you the first thing you need to know about threads."

She stood and held out her hand. He took it.

"Feel that?" she asked.

"Feel what?"

"Close your eyes. Don't look at the threads. Just feel."

Lifan closed his eyes. His mother's hand was warm and dry. But there was something else, underneath. A kind of buzzing. Like when he held his hand near the TV screen and felt the static.

"I feel it," he said.

"That's the thread. The connection between us. Physical touch makes threads stronger, Lifan. Especially if you do it with intention. If you're paying attention. Like this—"

The buzzing got stronger. Warmer. It spread up his arm and into his chest, and he felt something there, right behind his ribs, glow brighter. The thread connecting them. He could feel it now, without even looking.

"Whoa," he breathed.

"This is how you touch threads," his mother said. "Not with your hands. With your *xīn*—your heart-mind. The place inside you where the threads live. Does that make sense?"

It did, sort of. Or maybe it didn't, but he understood it anyway, in a way that wasn't about words.

They practiced for hours that afternoon. His mother taught him how to find his own center, the place where all his threads originated. How to feel the difference between strong connections and weak ones. How to trace a thread outward and get a sense of the person on the other end—not reading their mind exactly, but feeling their... essence. Their emotional color.

His father came home and found them sitting cross-legged on the kitchen floor, holding hands, eyes closed.

"Meditation?" he asked, amused.

"Something like that," Lifan's mother said, and winked at Lifan.

Over the next few months, she taught him everything she knew. They had code words for it. "Thread work" was what they called it when they practiced. She showed him how to dim his awareness of the threads so he could function normally—otherwise they were too distracting, all that light and movement. She taught him to spot different types of connections: family bonds, friendships, romantic love, the weird sticky threads of resentment or anger.

But the most important lesson came one evening when the autumn light slanted golden through the kitchen windows.

"The threads have colors, Lifan. Six frequencies." She held up her fingers, counting them off. "White is Ground—the foundation, the carrier wave. Deep blue is Recognition—seeing what you're actually looking at. Green is Resonance—the connection itself, empathy made visible. Gold is Transmission—the power to change someone, to wake them up. Red-orange is Integration—making the change stick. And the last one..." She paused. "Rainbow light dissolving to clear. That's Dissolution. The sixth frequency. The one that takes everything you are and lets it go."

"Why would anyone want that?"

"Because sometimes letting go is the only way forward." Her eyes were wet. "Remember the frequencies, bǎobèi. White, blue, green, gold, red, rainbow. One day you'll need to know them."

He memorized them like his multiplication tables. Like something important he'd be tested on later.

"Those are the worst," she said, pointing to a muddy-colored thread that emerged from her chest and disappeared toward the neighbor's house. "Mrs. Chen still hasn't forgiven me for letting our tree drop leaves in her yard three years ago. See how the thread looks sick? That's unresolved anger. It hurts both of us, but she won't let it go."

"Can you cut it?" Lifan asked.

His mother shook her head. "No. Only she can release it. I've apologized. I've offered to rake her yard. But she holds onto it. That's her choice."

"That's stupid."

"Yes," his mother agreed. "But people are often stupid about forgiveness."

---

It was spring when his mother got sick.

At first it was just tiredness. She napped more. She stopped going to her book club on Thursdays. Then came the doctor appointments. A lot of them.

Lifan watched her threads and tried not to panic.

They were changing. The golden color was still there, but new threads were appearing—dark purple ones, almost black, that didn't reach out to other people. They curled back into her body, spiraling inward. Wrong.

"Māma, what are those?" he asked one night.

She looked down at her own chest. She could see them too.

"Sickness," she said quietly. "Illness makes its own kind of thread. Internal connections, breaking down instead of building up."

"Can you fix them?"

"No, bǎobèi. I can't."

"Can the doctors?"

She pulled him close. "They're trying."

But the purple threads got thicker. They multiplied. By summer, they were choking out the golden ones.

Lifan's father started making a lot of phone calls. His voice would go tight and angry, arguing with insurance companies, with the hospital, with people Lifan couldn't see. The thread connecting his parents got brighter, more intense, shot through with red—desperation, fear, love all tangled together.

In August, his mother went to the hospital and didn't come home.

Lifan visited her there. The room was white and smelled like bleach and something else, something sweet and rotten underneath. Machines beeped. His mother looked small in the bed, her skin gray-pale.

Her threads were rising.

Not all of them. Not yet. But some of the strong ones—the ones to people who'd died, her own mother, her grandmother—those were definitely rising now, pulling upward, connecting to something Lifan couldn't see.

"Come here," she whispered.

He climbed onto the bed carefully. The thread between them blazed gold.

"Listen to me," she said. "I don't have much time, so I need you to remember this, okay?"

He nodded. Tears were already blurring his vision.

"The threads are a gift, Lifan. Not a curse. Even when they hurt. Even when you see things you wish you couldn't see. Do you understand?"

"I don't want this gift," he choked out. "I want you to stay."

"I know, bǎobèi. I know. But that's not how it works." She touched his face with a hand that trembled. "The threads show us the truth. People are connected. Love is real. It survives death. That's what you need to remember."

"Your thread to me isn't rising," Lifan said desperately. "Look. It's still here. You're not dying."

His mother smiled. "Not that thread. That one never rises, Lifan. Mother and son—that's forever. Even when I'm gone, you'll still feel it. Different, but there."

"I don't want different. I want you."

"I know." Her eyes were wet. "But you're going to be okay. You're strong. Stronger than you know. And one day, you're going to do something important with this gift. Something that matters."

"What?"

"I don't know. But I feel it, in our thread. Your destiny is bright, Lifan. So bright."

She pulled him close. He buried his face in her shoulder and sobbed.

"Threads don't break," she whispered into his hair. "They rise. Remember that. When I'm gone, when you can't see me anymore—the thread doesn't break. It rises. I rise. But I'm still here. Always here."

She died three days later.

Lifan wasn't there. He was home with his father, asleep, when it happened. He woke up at 2:47 AM with a gasp, his hand clutching his chest.

The thread had changed.

It was still there. Still bright. But it was... vertical now. Rising straight up, disappearing into something vast above him. And when he touched it with his heart-mind, he felt her, distant but real, like a star.

His father came home at dawn and told him the news.

Lifan just nodded.

He already knew.

---

At the funeral, Lifan watched the threads.

All the mourners had connections to his mother. Some were bright and strong—family, close friends. Others were thin and formal—work colleagues, distant cousins. They formed a web around the casket, a net of light and grief.

And all of them, every single one, was hurting. He could feel it through the threads. Sadness, regret, love, anger. Mrs. Chen was there, crying, and the muddy thread of resentment had finally dissolved into pure golden grief.

His father stood next to him, hollow-eyed. The thread connecting them was different now. Before, it had been medium-strong, the normal connection of a busy father to a young son. Now it was thicker, red-gold, laced with desperation.

His father was terrified.

Lifan understood. He was terrified too.

After the funeral, people came to the house. They brought food. So much food. Casseroles and dumplings and fruit baskets and Tupperware containers full of things Lifan didn't want to eat.

Everyone wanted to touch him. Pat his head. Hug him. Tell him it would be okay.

The threads blazed with every touch. New connections formed, thin sympathetic strands. They hurt. All of them hurt.

He hid in his room eventually, overwhelmed.

His father found him there, lying in bed, staring at the ceiling.

"Lifan?" His father's voice was rough. "You okay?"

"Yeah."

A lie. His first real lie to his father.

His father sat on the edge of the bed. The thread between them pulsed. "Listen, buddy. I know this is hard. It's hard for me too. But we're going to get through this, okay? You and me."

Lifan wanted to believe him.

He tried.

But when he looked at his father's threads, he saw something that scared him. A lot of them were fading. Connections his father had maintained while Lifan's mother was alive—friends, extended family, his own parents—they were going dim. Fraying.

His father was collapsing inward.

And Lifan couldn't tell him. Couldn't explain what he saw.

His mother had made him promise. Not until you're older.

So he just nodded and said, "Okay, Dad."

His father hugged him. Lifan felt the thread between them strain with the weight of everything unsaid.

Outside, the sun was setting. Golden light streamed through the window, and for a moment, it looked almost like threads.

Lifan closed his eyes.

*Threads don't break,* his mother had said. *They rise.*

He tried to believe her.

He really, really tried.

---

## CHAPTER 2: THE SYSTEM

The Hendersons were first.

"Nice family," the social worker said, like she was narrating a brochure. "Two kids, a dog. They specifically requested a child who'd experienced loss. They want to help."

Lifan sat in the back of her Prius and watched the threads. The social worker—Ms. Rodriguez—had a thin connection to him, professional and pitying. It felt like sandpaper.

His father was gone. Not dead. Worse.

Three months after Mom died, Dad stopped getting out of bed. Stopped going to work. Stopped doing anything except stare at the ceiling and occasionally cry. The threads connecting him to the world had dimmed to almost nothing. When Lifan tried to feel him through their connection, all he got was static. White noise.

Someone called Child Protective Services. Lifan never found out who.

Now here he was, seven years old, driving to his first foster home with everything he owned in a garbage bag.

The Hendersons lived in a cul-de-sac in Roseville. Big house, bigger lawn, American flag by the front door. Mrs. Henderson came out smiling, blonde hair perfect, and opened her arms for a hug.

Lifan stepped into it automatically.

The thread formed instantly. Thin, yellow-gold, laced with something that felt like... performance. She was hugging him because that's what you did with foster kids. Because it looked good.

"Welcome home, sweetie!" she said, too loud.

Home. Right.

Mr. Henderson shook his hand. "Good to meet you, Lifan. We're happy you're here."

Another thin thread. This one was more genuine—Mr. Henderson actually meant it—but it was still surface-level. Polite. Distant.

The Henderson kids were twins, both ten. Bryce and Brittany. They stared at Lifan like he was a science project.

"Is he Chinese?" Bryce asked.

"Half," Lifan said. "My mom was Chinese. My dad's white."

"Cool," Bryce said, in a tone that meant the opposite.

Brittany didn't say anything. Her thread was the thinnest of all—she didn't want him here. He could feel it. Resentment already forming, sticky and dark.

Great.

---

The first two weeks were fine. Awkward, but fine.

Lifan got his own room. Small, painted beige, with furniture that was obviously from IKEA. Mrs. Henderson had put up a poster of a kitten hanging from a tree branch. "Hang in there!" it said.

He took it down the first night.

School was the worst part. Middle of second grade, and everyone already had friend groups. Lifan was the new kid, the foster kid, the quiet Asian kid who sometimes stared at nothing.

Because he did stare. At the threads.

He couldn't help it. The classroom was a web of connections—kids who'd known each other since kindergarten, clusters of best friends, the complicated tangle of crushes and rivalries. It was beautiful and overwhelming and so, so distracting.

"Lifan? Are you paying attention?"

He blinked. Mrs. Kowalski, his teacher, was looking at him with concern. Her thread to him was thin but warm—she cared about her students, genuinely.

"Yes," he lied.

"Then what's the answer to number seven?"

He had no idea what number seven was.

"Um..."

The class giggled. A few threads lit up with amusement, some with secondhand embarrassment.

Mrs. Kowalski sighed. "It's okay. Just try to focus, please."

He tried. God, he tried.

But the threads wouldn't stop.

---

The incident happened three weeks in.

Lifan was eating breakfast. Cereal—the Hendersons bought the cheap stuff, big generic bags of wheat squares. He was staring at his bowl, watching the milk swirl, when he felt it.

A thread. Strengthening.

He looked up automatically, searching for the source.

Mrs. Henderson was on the phone in the next room. Her voice was low, but he could hear snippets.

"...harder than we thought... doesn't really engage... I don't know, maybe we're not the right fit..."

The thread between her and someone else—another adult, someone Lifan had never met—blazed bright. They were talking about him. Deciding his fate.

Fear shot through him. Not the Hendersons too. Please not already.

He must have made a noise, because Bryce looked at him.

"Why are you staring at Mom?"

Lifan jerked his gaze away. "I'm not."

"Yeah you are. You do that a lot. Stare at people. It's weird."

"I wasn't staring."

"Yeah you were. You stare at nothing, too. Like you're seeing stuff that's not there. Are you, like, mental or something?"

"Bryce!" Mrs. Henderson had appeared in the doorway, phone still in hand. "We don't use that word."

"But he is weird, Mom. He just sits there and stares and—"

"Enough." She looked at Lifan. Her thread pulsed with discomfort. "Lifan, honey, is everything okay?"

"Yes," he said quietly.

"Because if you're having trouble adjusting, we can talk to your social worker, get you some help—"

"I'm fine."

But he wasn't. And she knew it. And three days later, Ms. Rodriguez came to pick him up.

"It's not your fault," she said in the car. "The Hendersons just... they weren't equipped for your needs."

What needs? Lifan wanted to scream. I just need people to stop giving up on me.

But he didn't say that. He just watched the threads and stayed quiet.

---

The Patels were better.

They were younger, early thirties, no kids of their own yet. Mr. Patel—"Call me Rohan"—was a software engineer. Mrs. Patel—"I'm Priya, please"—was a nurse. They lived in a townhouse in Elk Grove and seemed genuinely excited to foster.

"We've been wanting to do this for years," Priya said, smiling. "We just needed to get settled first."

Their threads were strong. Not to Lifan, not yet, but to each other—bright red-gold, the kind of connection that meant real partnership. And when they looked at him, he felt something that had been missing at the Hendersons.

Hope.

They wanted this to work.

So did Lifan.

He tried hard. Really hard. He did his homework without being asked. He cleaned his room. He smiled at dinner and answered questions about school and didn't stare at the threads.

Except he did. A little. Because they were there and he couldn't not see them.

He just got better at hiding it.

For two months, it worked.

Then Priya's nephew came to visit.

Devon was twelve, loud, and loved video games. He and Lifan actually got along okay at first. Devon taught him how to play Apex Legends (the 2032 remake, since the original servers had finally shut down), and they stayed up late eating pizza and yelling at the screen.

It was the most normal Lifan had felt since his mom died.

Then Devon caught him talking to her.

It was an accident. Lifan was in his room, door closed, lying on his bed and reaching out through his thread to his mother. It was something he did every night. Just... checking in. Feeling her presence on the other end.

And sometimes, he talked to her.

Not out loud. Usually. But that night he was tired and forgot to be careful.

"I think this one might work, Māma," he whispered to the ceiling, where her thread rose. "The Patels are nice. Priya reminds me of you, kind of. She makes chai the right way."

The door opened.

Devon stood there, staring.

"Dude," he said. "Who are you talking to?"

Lifan's heart stopped. "No one."

"You were definitely talking to someone. I heard you say 'mama.'"

"I was just... thinking out loud."

Devon's expression shifted from confused to something worse. Pity. "Are you talking to your dead mom?"

"No."

"It's okay if you are. My therapist says it's normal to—"

"I'm not." Lifan sat up, face hot. "I was just thinking. Can you get out of my room?"

Devon backed away, hands up. "Okay, okay. Sorry."

But the damage was done.

That night, Lifan heard Devon talking to Priya downstairs. Their voices carried.

"...talking to the ceiling... said 'mama'... I think he might need, like, serious help..."

Priya's response was too quiet to hear, but her thread spiked with concern.

By morning, the decision was made.

"We're not equipped for this level of trauma," Rohan said gently. Ms. Rodriguez was already there, waiting in the living room. "We think you need a more therapeutic placement. Somewhere with trained staff."

Lifan wanted to scream. He wasn't traumatized. He was just different.

But he'd learned by now that different meant broken to most people.

So he packed his garbage bag again and left.

---

After the Patels came the Johnsons. After the Johnsons, the Nguyens. After the Nguyens, a brief stint with a single mom named Karen who meant well but worked eighty hours a week.

Each placement taught Lifan something new about hiding.

Don't stare. Don't space out. Don't talk to people who aren't there. Don't mention threads, or energy, or anything Mom taught you. Don't be weird. Don't be quiet. Don't be too loud. Don't need too much. Don't need anything at all.

Just be normal.

Except he didn't know how.

By the time he was nine, he'd learned to fake it better. Smile. Make eye contact. Laugh at jokes even when they weren't funny. Build a shell around the weird parts and show people only what they could handle.

The mask.

His mother had never taught him about the mask. She'd taught him to honor the threads, to see them as sacred. But she'd died before she could teach him how to survive in a world that couldn't see what he saw.

So he figured it out himself.

The mask was exhausting. It meant constantly monitoring himself, aware of every expression, every word, every moment his attention drifted to the threads. It meant being on all the time, performing normalcy.

But it worked.

Sort of.

He still got shuffled around—group homes, emergency placements, a few more failed foster families. But he lasted longer now. Weeks instead of days. Sometimes even months.

And slowly, carefully, he built his own understanding of the threads.

The things his mother had shown him were just the beginning. There was so much more. He learned that he could strengthen threads intentionally, if he focused. Learned that some people's threads were naturally brighter than others—those people were usually kinder, more open. Learned that he could sense someone's emotional state through their thread, even from far away.

He learned that grief threads never faded. His connection to his mother, rising into the sky, stayed bright. And the thread to his father, wherever he was—that one was still there too, dim and gray but unbroken.

He learned that he was lonely.

The threads showed him everyone's connections. He could see friendship and love all around him, webs of belonging. But he was outside them. A node with only thin, temporary strands, nothing strong enough to matter.

By the time he was eleven, he'd stopped hoping for permanent placement.

Then he got sent to Pacific House.

---

Pacific House was a group home in Carmichael. Twelve boys, ages eight to seventeen, all with their own histories of trauma and displacement. The house was run by a married couple, Joe and Linda, who had the tired eyes of people who'd seen too much but kept trying anyway.

"Ground rules," Joe said on Lifan's first day. "No fighting. No stealing. Respect everyone's space. Therapy is mandatory. School is mandatory. Everything else, we'll figure out together."

Lifan nodded. He'd heard versions of this speech before.

"You'll be sharing a room with Danny," Linda added. "He's your age. Arrived last month. He's... well, you'll see."

Danny turned out to be a twelve-year-old Korean kid with the loudest laugh Lifan had ever heard and exactly zero filter.

"Yo!" Danny said when Lifan walked into their room. "New roommate! Cool! I'm Danny. I'm here because my parents died in a car crash and my grandparents are too old to take care of me and also I have ADHD so I'm 'challenging.'" He made air quotes. "What's your story?"

Lifan blinked. "Um. My mom died. My dad couldn't cope."

"Damn. That sucks. Want some Skittles? I stole them from Linda's desk but don't tell her."

Despite himself, Lifan laughed. "Sure."

Danny grinned and tossed him the bag.

And just like that, a thread formed.

Not thin. Not professional or pitying or temporary.

Real.

Lifan stared at it, shocked. Golden-green, warm, already stronger than threads he'd had with foster families he'd lived with for months.

Danny tilted his head. "You good? You're doing the staring thing."

"What?"

"You do this thing where you space out and stare. It's cool, I do it too. ADHD, remember? We can be space cadets together."

Lifan realized he'd been caught. Again. But Danny wasn't judging him. Just... observing.

"I'm good," Lifan said.

"Cool. Wanna play Mario Kart? I call Yoshi."

They played until lights out. Danny talked the entire time—about his old school, his dead parents, the weird kid in room five who collected rocks, the best strategies for Rainbow Road, whether aliens were real (definitely), whether ghosts were real (probably), whether their lives would get better (maybe).

Lifan mostly listened.

But when he lay in bed that night, he could feel Danny's thread, bright and steady in the darkness.

For the first time in four years, he didn't feel completely alone.

---

"So what's your deal?" Danny asked a week later.

They were on the roof. Not supposed to be, but Danny had found a way up through the attic, and Lifan had followed. The Sacramento skyline sprawled in the distance, lights twinkling in the smog.

"What do you mean?" Lifan asked.

"Like, everyone here has a thing. Marcus has anger issues. Tyler's got anxiety so bad he pukes. Devon—different Devon, not your old one—is on the spectrum. What's yours?"

Lifan hesitated. The mask was automatic now. "I don't have a thing."

"Bullshit. Everyone has a thing. You do that staring stuff. And you're quiet. But like, quiet in a spooky way. Like you're listening to something the rest of us can't hear."

Too close. Way too close.

"I just zone out sometimes," Lifan said.

Danny looked at him for a long moment. Then he shrugged. "Okay. You don't have to tell me. But like, just so you know, I don't care if you're weird. Weird is fine. Weird is better than boring."

Something in Lifan's chest loosened.

"Thanks," he said.

"No problem, roomie."

They sat there for a while, not talking, just watching the city.

And Lifan thought: *Maybe this one will stick.*

---

He was right.

Pacific House became home. Not perfect—nowhere was perfect—but home.

Danny became his best friend. His first real friend since his mom died. They did everything together. School, therapy, homework, video games, elaborate plans to sneak extra dessert from the kitchen.

And Danny never pushed about the staring thing.

He noticed, obviously. Danny noticed everything. But he just accepted it as part of Lifan's weirdness, like how Lifan also hated loud noises and wouldn't eat food if different parts touched on his plate.

For two years, it was good.

Then Kevin arrived.

---

Kevin was fourteen, big for his age, and mean in a way that felt practiced. He'd been through six placements in two years. "Behavioral issues," Joe explained in the house meeting where they introduced him.

Lifan felt his threads immediately.

They were wrong.

Most people's threads were golden, or warm colors—reds, oranges, yellows. Even people who were sad or angry still had basically warm threads.

Kevin's were cold. Gray and brown and sickly green, and they didn't reach out to people so much as latch on. Parasitic.

"He's bad news," Lifan whispered to Danny that night.

"How do you know?"

Lifan couldn't explain. "Just trust me."

Danny, bless him, did.

But it didn't matter.

Kevin zeroed in on Lifan immediately. Maybe because Lifan was quiet. Maybe because he was small for eleven. Maybe just because Kevin needed a target and Lifan was convenient.

It started small. "Accidentally" knocking Lifan's backpack off the table. Taking his spot on the couch. Calling him "China boy" under his breath.

Lifan ignored it. He'd dealt with worse.

Then it escalated.

Kevin started spreading rumors. Lifan was a snitch. Lifan was stuck-up. Lifan thought he was better than everyone.

None of it was true, but some of the other boys believed it. Threads that had been warm toward Lifan cooled. Marcus stopped sitting with him at meals. Tyler avoided eye contact.

Only Danny stuck by him.

"Kevin's full of shit," Danny said. "Everyone knows it."

"Not everyone," Lifan muttered.

He could see it in the threads. Kevin was good at this. Manipulation. Turning people against each other. Creating webs of resentment and suspicion.

And Lifan couldn't fight back. Not without revealing what he could see.

So he endured.

Until the day Kevin found out about the closet.

---

## CHAPTER 3: THE CLOSET

The closet was on the second floor, next to the bathroom. It was technically a linen closet—shelves for towels and toilet paper and cleaning supplies. But the bottom was empty space, just enough room for a twelve-year-old to sit if he pulled his knees up.

Lifan had discovered it during his second week at Pacific House. Sometimes the threads got overwhelming—twelve boys' worth of emotions and connections, all tangled together in close quarters. When that happened, he needed somewhere small and quiet to reset.

The closet was perfect.

He'd go in there with his phone, close the door, and just breathe in the darkness until the threads dimmed back to manageable levels.

Nobody knew about it.

Except Kevin saw him go in one day.

---

It was a Tuesday. Lifan had been holding it together through a particularly rough day—pop quiz in math, fight at lunch that left everyone's threads jangling with adrenaline, group therapy where they'd talked about abandonment and Lifan had to keep his face neutral while his chest felt like it was caving in.

By dinner, he was fried.

He excused himself, saying he had to pee, and slipped into the closet instead.

Darkness. Silence. The smell of laundry detergent.

He closed his eyes and focused on his breathing, the way his mom had taught him. In for four. Hold for four. Out for four.

The threads started to settle.

Then the door opened.

Kevin stood there, backlit by the hallway light, grinning.

"What the hell are you doing in the closet, China boy?"

Lifan's heart jumped. "Nothing. Just—"

"Just hiding like a little freak?" Kevin's smile widened. "That's what you do, isn't it? Hide. Because you're too much of a pussy to deal with real life."

"Leave me alone, Kevin."

"Or what?" Kevin stepped closer. "You gonna cry? Tell on me? Good luck. Joe and Linda love me."

That was a lie. Joe and Linda didn't love Kevin. But they were trying with him, the way they tried with everyone. And Kevin was good at playing the victim when adults were around.

Lifan tried to push past him. Kevin blocked the doorway.

"Move," Lifan said.

"Make me."

They stared at each other. Lifan was smaller, younger, and had never been in a real fight in his life. Kevin had three inches and thirty pounds on him, plus a mean streak a mile wide.

Kevin knew it.

"Yeah, that's what I thought." Kevin stepped back. Then, before Lifan could leave, he shoved Lifan backward into the closet and slammed the door.

The lock clicked.

From the outside.

"Have fun in there, freak," Kevin called. "Maybe you'll learn to stop being such a weirdo."

Footsteps. Moving away.

Lifan stood in the darkness, heart pounding.

The closet door locked from the outside. There was a little button lock on the handle—childproofing, probably, to keep kids from getting into the cleaning supplies. Kevin had just twisted it.

Lifan was trapped.

---

He didn't panic immediately.

First he tried the door. Pushed, pulled, threw his shoulder against it. The lock held.

Then he pulled out his phone. Dead battery. Of course. He'd forgotten to charge it last night.

Okay. Okay. Someone would notice he was missing eventually. Dinner was happening right now. When he didn't come back, Danny would look for him.

He just had to wait.

He sat down in the small space and pulled his knees to his chest.

The threads were going crazy. He could feel everyone in the house—their confusion as they realized he wasn't at dinner, their mild concern, Kevin's smug satisfaction.

And under all of it, his own fear, growing.

---

An hour passed.

Then two.

He heard people moving around outside. Someone used the bathroom. Someone else walked by, talking on their phone.

Lifan banged on the door. "Hey! I'm in here! Hello?"

No response. The closet was between two other rooms, and the door was thick. Sound didn't carry well.

He tried again. Shouted louder.

Nothing.

Three hours.

The house was getting quieter now. People going to bed. Lights-out was at ten for the younger kids, eleven for the older ones.

Lifan banged on the door until his hands hurt.

Still nothing.

Panic was setting in now. Real panic. The closet was small. Dark. Airless. The walls felt like they were pressing in.

He couldn't breathe right. His chest was tight. His hands were shaking.

The threads were screaming. He could feel everyone's threads, all of them, too bright, too loud, a cacophony of connection and emotion and he couldn't dim it down, couldn't focus, couldn't—

*Breathe,* his mother's voice said in his memory. *Find your center.*

He tried. God, he tried.

But he was twelve years old and alone and trapped in a closet and nobody was coming.

---

Four hours.

Five.

He'd stopped banging on the door. His hands were bruised and his voice was raw from shouting.

Now he just sat there, knees to chest, rocking slightly.

The threads were a hurricane. Everyone in the house was asleep now, their connections dimmed, but he could still feel them. And under that, his own threads—to his mother, to his father, to Danny.

Danny. Where was Danny? Why hadn't Danny found him?

He reached out through their thread, trying to feel his friend.

Asleep. Danny was asleep.

Of course. It was past midnight now. Everyone was asleep.

Except Lifan.

Alone in the dark.

---

Six hours.

The fear was starting to transform.

He wasn't just scared anymore. He was angry.

Angry at Kevin. Angry at the system that kept shuffling him around. Angry at his father for giving up. Angry at his mother for dying and leaving him with this gift he didn't know how to use.

Angry at himself for being weak.

"I hate this," he whispered to the darkness. "I hate all of this."

The threads pulsed with his emotion. Normally he kept tight control over his feelings—the mask, always the mask—but in here, alone, he let it out.

And something weird happened.

The threads responded.

They got brighter. Warmer. His anger was feeding them, strengthening them, and he could feel the connections to everyone in the house intensify. Not just feel them. Almost... touch them.

He grabbed onto Danny's thread instinctively, desperately.

*Danny,* he thought. *Please. Wake up. Help me.*

And Danny's thread blazed.

---

Seven hours.

Lifan had cried himself out. Now he just sat there, empty.

The anger was gone. The fear was gone. There was nothing left except a kind of cold clarity.

This was his life.

People would always abandon him. Lock him away. Misunderstand him. He was different, and different was dangerous, and he would never belong anywhere.

Unless.

Unless he got strong enough that it didn't matter.

Strong enough to protect himself. Strong enough that he didn't need anyone else.

His mother had said the threads were a gift. Fine. Then he'd learn to use them. Really use them. Not just see and feel, but manipulate. Control.

If he was going to be alone, he'd be alone on his own terms.

The thought settled into him like ice.

---

The door opened.

Lifan blinked in the sudden light. Danny stood there, phone flashlight in hand, looking panicked.

"Dude!" Danny's voice cracked. "Holy shit, I've been looking everywhere for you! How did you—why are you in the closet?"

Lifan's voice came out hoarse. "Kevin locked me in."

"What?! When?"

"Dinner time."

Danny's eyes went huge. "That was eight hours ago! Lifan—"

"I know."

"We have to tell Joe and Linda—"

"No." Lifan stood up, shaky. His legs had gone numb from sitting. "Don't."

"Are you insane? Kevin locked you in a closet for eight hours! That's—"

"I said don't." Lifan looked at his friend. The thread between them was blazing, bright green-gold, charged with Danny's fear and relief and anger. "Please."

Danny stared at him. "Why not?"

Because telling would mean questions. Adult attention. Intervention. And Lifan was done with that. Done with being the victim, the broken foster kid who needed saving.

He was going to handle this himself.

"Just trust me," Lifan said.

Danny looked like he wanted to argue. But finally, he nodded. "Okay. But dude. Are you okay?"

"Yeah," Lifan lied. "I'm fine."

They went back to their room. Lifan lay in bed and stared at the ceiling, feeling the threads of everyone in the house.

Including Kevin's.

Parasitic. Predatory. Wrong.

*I'm going to make him pay,* Lifan thought.

He didn't know how yet. But he'd figure it out.

The threads would show him.

---

He changed after that night.

Danny noticed first.

"You're different," Danny said a few days later. They were in their room, supposedly doing homework. "Like, more quiet than usual. And you get this look sometimes. Kind of scary."

"Scary how?" Lifan asked.

"I don't know. Like you're planning something."

Lifan was planning something. But he couldn't tell Danny that.

"I'm just tired," he said.

It wasn't entirely a lie. He was tired. Tired of being helpless. Tired of being afraid.

So he stopped being afraid.

Instead, he studied.

He spent hours in his head, exploring the threads. Experimenting. He'd already learned he could strengthen connections through intention and touch. Now he learned he could weaken them too. Not cut them—that seemed impossible—but cool them down. Make them thinner.

He practiced on small things first. The thread between Marcus and Tyler—they'd been friends, but Lifan dimmed it until they drifted apart, finding other people to sit with at meals.

Then he practiced strengthening threads. He took two kids who barely knew each other and amped up their connection until they became inseparable.

It was intoxicating. This power. This control.

He knew his mother wouldn't approve. She'd taught him to respect the threads, not manipulate them.

But his mother was dead.

And Lifan was done playing by rules that left him powerless.

---

He didn't use his abilities on Kevin right away. That would be too obvious.

Instead, he watched. Waited.

Kevin kept being cruel—to Lifan, to other kids, to everyone. But he was careful around adults. Always polite to Joe and Linda. Always had an excuse.

The other boys were scared of him. Lifan could see it in their threads. Fear, resentment, the urge to fight back tangled with the knowledge that Kevin was bigger and meaner.

And Lifan thought: *What if I took away his protection?*

Kevin's biggest advantage was that the adults liked him. Or at least, wanted to believe in him. Joe and Linda had threads to Kevin—thin but warm, hopeful. They wanted him to succeed.

What if those threads cooled?

Lifan started small. Whenever Kevin and Joe were in the same room, Lifan would focus on their connection and just... breathe cold into it. Doubt. Suspicion. Nothing obvious. Just a subtle shift.

It took weeks. But slowly, Joe's attitude toward Kevin changed. He stopped giving Kevin the benefit of the doubt. Started watching him more closely.

Then Lifan worked on Linda. Same technique. Cool the thread. Introduce doubt.

Within a month, Kevin's protective shield was gone.

And Kevin, who was used to getting away with everything, got sloppy.

---

The incident happened in the common room.

Kevin was picking on Tyler—taking his book, holding it over his head while Tyler jumped for it, laughing.

Normally, Joe would have given Kevin a mild warning. Told him to knock it off. Believed Kevin when he said he was just joking around.

But today, when Joe walked in and saw it, his thread to Kevin flared dark.

"Kevin. My office. Now."

Kevin's smirk faded. "I was just—"

"Now."

Kevin went, shooting a glare at Tyler like it was his fault.

Lifan watched from the corner, expressionless.

Joe and Kevin were in the office for an hour. Lifan couldn't hear what was said, but he could feel the threads. Joe's anger. Kevin's defiance turning into worry.

When Kevin came out, his face was red.

"What happened?" Marcus asked.

"Nothing," Kevin snapped, and went upstairs.

But something had happened. Lifan could feel it.

Kevin was on thin ice now.

---

Two weeks later, Kevin got into a fight with Marcus. A real one, fists flying. Marcus ended up with a black eye. Kevin ended up transferred to a different group home.

"It's for the best," Linda said at the house meeting afterward. "Kevin needs more intensive support than we can provide."

The other boys looked relieved. The threads in the house immediately lightened, tension dissolving.

Danny leaned over to Lifan. "Good riddance, right?"

"Yeah," Lifan said.

But he felt strange. Hollow.

He'd won. He'd used the threads to turn Kevin's support system against him, and Kevin was gone.

Exactly as planned.

So why did he feel sick?

---

That night, Lifan couldn't sleep.

He lay in bed, staring at the ceiling, feeling the threads of everyone in Pacific House. They were peaceful now. Content.

Except his.

His threads felt... tarnished. The golden color was still there, but there was something else mixed in now. Gray. Muddy.

Like the resentment threads he'd seen in other people.

He'd manipulated Kevin's connections. Changed them. Not to help Kevin—to hurt him.

And yeah, Kevin had deserved it. Kevin was cruel and dangerous and had locked Lifan in a closet for eight hours.

But.

But Lifan had become something in the process. Something his mother wouldn't recognize.

He thought about what she'd said, that last day in the hospital.

*The threads are a gift. Not a curse.*

*Your destiny is bright.*

Was this what she'd meant? Using the threads as weapons?

No.

He knew it wasn't.

But he also knew he couldn't go back to being powerless. Couldn't unlearn what he'd learned.

So where did that leave him?

---

He found Danny on the roof the next night.

"Couldn't sleep?" Danny asked.

"Yeah."

They sat in silence for a while.

"Can I tell you something?" Lifan said finally.

"Always."

Lifan took a breath. "I did something. To Kevin. I made Joe and Linda stop trusting him."

Danny frowned. "What do you mean, you made them?"

"I..." Lifan struggled for words. How could he explain without explaining everything? "I'm good at reading people. Body language, tone, that stuff. And I used it to make Kevin look bad."

It wasn't the whole truth. But it was close enough.

Danny considered this. "Did Kevin deserve it?"

"Yes."

"Then why do you feel bad?"

"Because..." Lifan pulled his knees up. "Because it felt good. The control. And that scares me."

Danny was quiet for a long time.

"You know what my therapist says?" he said finally. "She says we're all works in progress. We make mistakes. Do things we regret. But that doesn't define us. What defines us is what we do next."

"What if I don't know what to do next?"

"Then you figure it out. One day at a time." Danny bumped his shoulder against Lifan's. "And you don't have to figure it out alone. You've got me, remember? Brothers."

Something in Lifan's chest cracked open.

Brothers.

He'd never had a brother. Never had someone who chose to stick with him, not out of obligation or pity, but because they wanted to.

The thread between him and Danny blazed.

"Brothers," Lifan echoed.

"So no more secret revenge plots without telling me, okay? I want in on the next one."

Lifan laughed despite himself. "Deal."

They sat there until dawn, talking about everything and nothing. And for the first time since the closet, Lifan felt like maybe he'd be okay.

Not because he was powerful.

Because he wasn't alone.

---

## CHAPTER 4: THE ESCAPE

The idea came from Danny.

They were thirteen, sitting in their room at Pacific House, supposedly studying for finals. Danny was bouncing a tennis ball off the wall. Lifan was actually reading his history textbook.

"We should run away," Danny said suddenly.

Lifan looked up. "What?"

"Run away. Like, leave. Get out of the system."

"Where would we go?"

"I don't know. Anywhere. California's huge. We could go to LA. Or San Francisco. Live on the beach. Become surfers."

"You can't surf."

"I could learn." Danny caught the tennis ball and sat up. "Come on. Aren't you tired of this? Group homes and foster families and social workers who pretend to care but really just want you to fit in some box?"

Lifan was tired of it. God, he was tired.

But running away was crazy. They were thirteen. They had no money, no car, no plan.

"It wouldn't work," he said.

"Sure it would. We just need to be smart about it."

Danny's eyes were bright. He was serious.

And the thread between them pulsed with something Lifan had learned to recognize: possibility.

Danny had a gift too. Not like Lifan's. Danny couldn't see threads. But he could see probabilities. Paths. Outcomes.

He'd explained it once, late at night in their room: "It's like... branches. Every choice splits into more choices. I see them all—some bright, some dim. The bright ones are likely. The dim ones are long shots." He'd waved his hand in front of his face, tracing invisible patterns. "Right now, there's an eighty-seven percent chance Joe falls asleep during the movie. A nine percent chance he catches us sneaking extra popcorn. A four percent chance of something weird happening."

"What kind of weird?"

"I don't know. The dim paths are fuzzy. I just see they exist."

He didn't call it a gift. Danny just said he was good at reading situations. Guessing what would happen next. But Lifan had watched him for two years now, and it was more than guessing.

Danny knew things.

"You've already thought about this," Lifan said slowly. "Like, really thought about it."

Danny grinned. "Maybe."

"And?"

"And I think we could do it. If we plan it right. If we're careful."

Lifan should have said no. Should have been the responsible one.

But he looked at Danny's face, alive with hope and mischief, and felt their thread blazing bright.

Brothers.

"Okay," Lifan said. "Let's plan it."

---

They spent three months planning.

Danny handled logistics. Routes, supplies, timing. He had a notebook full of notes—bus schedules, maps of California, lists of youth shelters in different cities.

"We can't just wander," Danny explained. "We need destinations. Places to sleep. Food sources. I've been researching."

"Where do you research this stuff?"

"Library computers. Reddit. There's a whole subreddit for homeless teens. It's depressing but informative."

Lifan handled money.

They couldn't take much from Pacific House without being obvious. But Lifan had been saving his allowance for months. Thirty-seven dollars. Danny had forty-two.

Not nearly enough.

So Lifan started doing odd jobs. Raking leaves for neighbors. Washing cars. He told Joe and Linda he was saving for a new phone.

They were proud of him. Their threads glowed warm with approval.

Lifan felt guilty. But not guilty enough to stop.

By June, they had three hundred dollars. Not a lot, but enough to start.

"We leave after school ends," Danny said. "First week of summer. Everyone will think we're just hanging out. We'll have a few hours before they notice."

"Where do we go first?"

Danny pulled out his map. He'd marked it up with highlighter. "Bus to Sacramento. Transfer to another bus to San Francisco. From there... we figure it out."

It wasn't much of a plan. But it was something.

And anything was better than staying in the system.

---

The night before they left, Lifan couldn't sleep.

He lay in bed, feeling the threads of everyone in Pacific House. Joe and Linda, asleep downstairs. The other boys in their rooms. Danny in the bed across from him, already snoring softly.

They were good people. Joe and Linda had tried. Really tried. Pacific House was the longest Lifan had stayed anywhere since his mom died.

But it wasn't home.

It couldn't be. Because at any moment, the system could move him again. Send him to a new placement. Split him up from Danny.

And Lifan couldn't live with that uncertainty anymore.

He reached out through his threads, one by one.

His mother. Still there, rising into the sky. Bright and distant.

His father. Gray and dim, somewhere in Sacramento. Lifan didn't know where exactly. Didn't want to know.

Danny. Right here. Blazing.

And one more thread. Thin, new, strange.

It had appeared a few weeks ago. Lifan didn't know who it connected to. The other end was... somewhere north. The thread had a different quality than the others. Not warm, exactly. Cool and clean, like mountain air.

He'd been curious about it, but hadn't had time to investigate.

Now, lying in bed, he followed it.

Traced it north, through Sacramento, through the valley, into the mountains. It led somewhere specific. Somewhere high up.

And at the other end, he felt... waiting.

Someone was waiting for him.

*Weird,* Lifan thought.

But he filed it away. He had more immediate concerns.

Tomorrow, they ran.

---

They left at 10 AM.

Told Joe they were going to the library to work on summer reading. Joe barely looked up from his coffee.

"Be back by dinner."

"Sure thing," Danny said, cheerful.

They walked out with their backpacks. Normal backpacks, nothing suspicious. Except inside were clothes, money, granola bars, and everything else they'd need.

They walked to the bus stop three blocks away.

"You ready?" Danny asked.

Lifan's heart was pounding. "Yeah."

"Last chance to back out."

"I'm not backing out."

Danny grinned. "Good. Because I already bought the tickets."

The bus came. They got on.

And just like that, they were gone.

---

The first part went smoothly.

Sacramento to San Francisco. Four-hour bus ride. They sat in the back and watched the scenery change from flat valley to rolling hills to urban sprawl.

Lifan felt the threads stretching behind them. Joe and Linda, still unaware. The other boys at Pacific House. All their connections growing thinner as distance increased.

But they didn't break.

Threads don't break.

In San Francisco, they got off the bus at the Transbay Terminal and immediately got lost.

"Okay," Danny said, consulting his phone map. "We need to find the youth shelter on Market Street. It's... uh... that way."

He pointed left.

They walked for twenty minutes before Danny admitted they were going the wrong direction.

"How do you know?" Lifan asked.

"My thing. It's telling me this is wrong."

Lifan had learned to trust Danny's thing.

They turned around.

An hour later, hungry and tired, they found the shelter. It was a squat building with barred windows and a mural of the Golden Gate Bridge on one wall.

Inside, a tired-looking woman at the desk barely glanced at them.

"Names?"

Danny had prepared for this. "I'm David Kim. This is Lee Chen."

Fake names. Just in case.

The woman wrote them down. "Ages?"

"Fourteen," Danny lied. They were thirteen, but looked older.

"Okay. Beds are upstairs. Dinner's at six. No drugs, no weapons, no violence. Break the rules, you're out. Clear?"

"Clear," they said in unison.

She handed them each a key. "Room 7. You're sharing."

The room was tiny. Two cots, a window that looked out on an alley, peeling paint on the walls.

It was perfect.

"We did it," Danny said, flopping onto a cot. "Holy shit, we actually did it."

Lifan sat on the other cot. His hands were shaking.

They'd escaped.

---

Three days in San Francisco, and reality set in.

The shelter was free, but only for three nights max. After that, they'd need to find somewhere else.

Food was expensive. Three hundred dollars was vanishing fast.

And the city was overwhelming. Lifan's threads were going haywire—millions of people, all connected in complex webs he couldn't begin to untangle. It gave him headaches.

"We need a new plan," Lifan said on day four.

They were sitting in Golden Gate Park, eating dollar store sandwiches.

"I know," Danny said. "I've been thinking."

"And?"

"We should go to Big Sur."

Lifan blinked. "The beach town?"

"Yeah. It's smaller than SF. Less overwhelming. And there are campgrounds. We could camp, save money on shelter."

"Do we know how to camp?"

"How hard can it be? You pitch a tent, you sleep. Boom. Camping."

Lifan was pretty sure it was more complicated than that. But Danny's thread was glowing with certainty.

"Big Sur," Lifan said. "Okay. How do we get there?"

Danny pulled out his phone. "Bus to Monterey. Then... we hitchhike."

"Hitchhike?!"

"People do it all the time on Highway 1. Tourists, hippies, surfers. We'll fit right in."

Lifan wanted to argue. But honestly, what choice did they have?

"Fine. Big Sur."

---

The bus to Monterey left the next morning.

They were down to a hundred and thirty dollars now. The tickets cost forty.

Danny did the math. "If we're careful, we can stretch the rest for a couple weeks. After that..."

"We'll figure it out," Lifan said.

They were good at figuring things out.

The bus ride was beautiful. Highway 1 wound along the coast, cliffs dropping into blue ocean. Lifan pressed his face to the window and watched the water.

He felt the strange thread again. The cool, clean one. It was pulling him north, not south toward Big Sur.

But Danny's probability sense said Big Sur was the right move.

Lifan ignored the thread and trusted his brother.

---

Monterey was smaller than San Francisco, but still busy. Tourists everywhere, cameras and shorts and sunburns.

Danny found a spot near the aquarium where people congregated. "This is good. Lots of traffic. Someone will give us a ride."

They stuck out their thumbs.

And waited.

And waited.

Two hours later, a beat-up van pulled over. The driver was a guy in his twenties with dreads and a tie-dye shirt.

"Where you headed?" he asked.

"Big Sur," Danny said.

"Hop in. I'm going to Esalen."

They got in. The van smelled like incense and weed.

"You guys runaways?" the driver asked casually.

"No," Lifan said quickly.

The driver laughed. "Sure. None of my business anyway. I'm River."

"Dave," Danny said. "This is Lee."

They drove south. River played jam band music and talked about chakras and the healing power of hot springs. Lifan half-listened, watching the ocean.

The strange thread was still pulling north. Getting fainter.

*Who are you?* Lifan thought at it.

No answer, obviously. Threads didn't talk.

But he felt it again. Waiting.

"Here's your stop," River said, pulling over at a campground. "Good luck, kids. Stay safe."

"Thanks," they said, and got out.

The campground was called Plaskett Creek. It cost twenty dollars a night.

"Expensive," Danny muttered. But he paid.

They set up their tent—a cheap two-person thing Danny had bought at Walmart. It took an hour and involved a lot of swearing.

But eventually, they got it up.

"Home sweet home," Danny said, crawling inside.

Lifan followed. The tent smelled like plastic and dirt.

Outside, the ocean roared.

Inside, Danny was already asleep.

Lifan lay there, feeling the threads.

Joe and Linda were panicking now. He could feel it across the distance. They'd realized the boys were gone. Police were probably involved.

Guilt twisted in his chest.

But they weren't going back.

This was their life now.

Lifan closed his eyes and let the sound of waves pull him under.

---

They lasted five days at Big Sur.

It was beautiful. Stunning. The cliffs, the ocean, the redwoods. Lifan had never seen anywhere so wild.

But it was also hard.

They ran out of food on day three. The campground didn't have a store, and the nearest town was miles away.

Danny tried to shoplift granola bars from a gas station and got caught. The owner just kicked them out, but it was a close call.

On day five, a ranger stopped by their campsite.

"You boys here alone?"

"Our parents are at another campsite," Danny lied smoothly. "We're meeting them later."

The ranger looked skeptical. "Where are they staying?"

"Um. Kirk Creek?"

"That's ten miles north."

"Yeah, we're hiking to meet them."

The ranger stared at them. Lifan could see his thread—suspicion, concern.

"You boys got ID?"

"We're fourteen," Danny said. "We don't have licenses yet."

"Where's your parents' phone number?"

Danny rattled off a fake number.

The ranger wrote it down. "I'm gonna call this. If it's fake, I'm calling the cops."

He walked away.

"We need to leave," Lifan said. "Now."

They grabbed their stuff and bolted.

They hiked north on Highway 1, staying off the road when cars passed.

"This is bad," Danny said. "That ranger definitely called the cops."

"Where do we go?"

Danny closed his eyes. Doing his probability thing.

"North," he said finally. "Just... north. That's what feels right."

North.

Where the strange thread was pulling.

"Okay," Lifan said. "North."

---

They walked for hours.

It got dark. They were hungry, exhausted, scared.

Cars passed. None stopped.

"We should camp," Danny said.

"Where?"

"I don't know. Just... off the road somewhere."

They scrambled down an embankment and found a small clearing. No tent, no food. Just their backpacks and the clothes they were wearing.

They curled up on the ground.

"This was a bad idea," Danny said quietly.

Lifan didn't answer.

But he reached out through their thread and pulsed warmth.

*Brothers,* he thought.

Danny's thread pulsed back.

They fell asleep on the cold ground, huddled together.

---

Lifan woke to someone standing over them.

He jerked upright, heart hammering.

It was an old man. Asian, maybe sixty, with a shaved head and simple robes. He looked... peaceful. Calm.

And his threads were unlike anything Lifan had ever seen.

They were bright. Impossibly bright. Not gold—white. Pure white light, radiating in every direction. And they weren't just connecting to people. They connected to trees, to the ground, to the sky itself.

"Lifan," the man said softly. "You've come a long way."

Lifan's mouth went dry. "How do you know my name?"

"Your mother told me. Before she died."

The world tilted.

"You... you knew my mom?"

The man nodded. "She was my student. Many years ago. She told me about you. About your gift." He glanced at Danny, still asleep. "About your friend, too. The one who sees paths."

Lifan couldn't speak.

The man smiled. "My name is Rinzen. I've been waiting for you."

The strange thread. The one pulling north.

It led to him.

"Why?" Lifan managed.

"Because you need training," Rinzen said. "And I need a student. Come. Let's go home."

"Home?"

"The monastery. In the mountains. Your mother wanted you to find it. When you were ready."

Lifan looked at Danny, then back at Rinzen.

Every instinct screamed this was impossible. A trick. Too good to be true.

But the thread between him and Rinzen glowed steady.

Real.

"Okay," Lifan whispered.

Rinzen held out his hand.

Lifan took it.

## CHAPTER 5: THE MONASTERY

The monastery was three hours north, hidden in the Santa Lucia Mountains.

Rinzen drove an ancient pickup truck that wheezed on the uphill climbs. Danny woke up somewhere around San Simeon, took one look at Rinzen, and whispered to Lifan: "Did we get kidnapped by a monk?"

"I think so," Lifan whispered back.

"Cool."

That was Danny. Adapt and roll with it.

They drove inland, away from the coast, up winding mountain roads. The trees got taller. The air got thinner. Lifan's ears popped.

And the strange thread got brighter.

"How did you know my mom?" Lifan asked finally.

Rinzen glanced at him in the rearview mirror. "She came to the monastery when she was twenty. Stayed for two years. She had the sight, like you. I taught her to control it."

"She never told me that."

"She wanted you to find your own path. Not feel obligated to follow hers." Rinzen turned onto an unmarked dirt road. "But she also knew you'd need help eventually. So she asked me to wait. To watch for you."

"How did you know I'd be at Big Sur?"

"I didn't. I felt you get close—your thread has been in my awareness for years, faint but there. When you came to the coast, I came looking."

"That's kind of creepy," Danny said.

Rinzen laughed. "Yes. I suppose it is."

They drove through a gate—just two posts, no actual barrier—and up a steep driveway.

And there it was.

The monastery.

It wasn't what Lifan expected. No golden roofs or giant Buddha statues. Just a collection of simple wooden buildings arranged around a central courtyard. Prayer flags strung between trees. A vegetable garden. Chickens.

It looked like a farm.

"This is it?" Danny asked.

"This is it," Rinzen said. "Home."

---

There were twelve other students. Ages ranging from nine to seventeen.

Rinzen introduced them at dinner that first night. Lifan tried to remember names and failed. There were too many, and his brain was fried.

But one kid stood out.

Nydan. Fifteen, tall and gangly, with green eyes that seemed to calculate everything they looked at. Freckles scattered across dark skin. Long hair he'd grown out because no one here cared. He sat next to Lifan at the long communal table and said, "So you're the new guy."

"I'm Lifan. This is Danny."

"I'm Nydan. I've been here three years and I hate it." He pulled out a small notebook covered in equations. "I'd rather be at MIT, but they won't take you until you're eighteen."

Lifan blinked. "MIT?"

"Physics. Pattern recognition. The mathematical structure of consciousness." Nydan shrugged like this was boring. "Rinzen thinks meditation and equations are the same thing. He's probably right. Still boring though." He tapped his temple. "I'm also working on brain-computer interface designs. Ways to connect human consciousness directly to machines. Rinzen says it's 'missing the point,' but I think it's the future."

"Why do you stay?"

"Because my parents are dead and I have nowhere else to go. Also, Rinzen won't let me leave until I 'achieve enlightenment' or whatever." Nydan made air quotes. "Which is bullshit, by the way. Enlightenment is sitting. For hours. Staring at walls. You'll see."

Danny laughed. Nydan grinned.

"I like you guys already," Nydan said. "Way better than the last new kid. He cried for a week straight."

"Where is he now?" Lifan asked.

"Therapy boarding school in Utah. Didn't make it past orientation."

Great. No pressure.

---

Orientation was the next morning.

Rinzen woke them at 5 AM. Lifan groaned. Danny buried his face in his pillow.

"Meditation starts in ten minutes," Rinzen said cheerfully, and left.

"I'm going back to sleep," Danny mumbled.

"Pretty sure that's not an option."

They dragged themselves to the meditation hall. It was a large room with wood floors, cushions arranged in rows, and nothing else. No decorations. No distractions.

Eleven other students were already there, sitting cross-legged.

Lifan and Danny found cushions in the back.

"Okay," Rinzen said from the front. "Close your eyes. Focus on your breath. In. Out. That's all."

Lifan closed his eyes.

Immediately, the threads appeared. All the students, their connections forming a web around the room. Rinzen's blazing white threads. The trees outside, somehow visible through the walls.

It was overwhelming.

"Lifan," Rinzen said quietly. "I know you see the threads. Let them be. Don't push them away. Just observe."

Lifan tried.

But the threads were so bright. So loud. They pulled at his attention, screaming for focus.

His breath got shallow. His chest tight.

"Breathe," Rinzen said.

Lifan tried. He really did.

But five minutes in, he had to open his eyes.

The room spun. He stood up, mumbled "Sorry," and stumbled outside.

He made it to the courtyard before he threw up in a bush.

---

Rinzen found him there.

"That was normal," Rinzen said, sitting beside him.

"Doesn't feel normal."

"Your gift is strong, Lifan. Stronger than your mother's. But you have no training. It's like trying to listen to every conversation in a crowded room at once. Of course it's overwhelming."

"So how do I stop it?"

"You don't. You learn to filter. To focus on one thread at a time, or none at all. It takes practice."

"How long?"

"Months. Maybe years."

Lifan's heart sank. "I can't do this for years."

"You can. And you will. Because the alternative is living in chaos forever." Rinzen stood. "Come. Let's try something different."

He led Lifan to a small garden behind the main hall. It was quiet. Peaceful. Just plants and dirt and a small fountain.

"Sit," Rinzen said.

Lifan sat.

"Now. Instead of focusing on threads, focus on one thing. That fountain. Watch the water. Listen to it. Make it the only thing in your awareness."

Lifan tried.

The fountain trickled. Water over stone. It was... nice, actually.

The threads dimmed. Not gone, but quieter.

"Good," Rinzen said. "That's the first step. Finding an anchor. Something to pull you out of the chaos."

They sat there for an hour. Just watching water.

It was the most peace Lifan had felt in weeks.

---

A few days later, Rinzen taught him something that changed everything.

"There's an image from the old texts," Rinzen said. They were in the garden again, the fountain trickling. "Indra's Net. Imagine a net stretching infinitely in all directions. At every point where threads cross, there's a jewel. Each jewel reflects every other jewel. And each reflection contains all the other reflections."

"Like infinite mirrors," Lifan said.

"Exactly. That's what you see when you look at threads. Not something separate from reality—reality itself. The Huayan masters called it *pratītyasamutpāda*. Dependent origination. Nothing exists alone. Everything reflects everything else."

"So the threads aren't... unusual?"

"The threads are what's actually there. Everyone is connected. Most people just can't see it." Rinzen smiled. "You're not broken, Lifan. You're just less blind than the rest of us."

Something in Lifan's chest unknotted. He'd spent years thinking he was crazy. Thinking the threads were a symptom of something wrong. But if they were real—if he was seeing what was actually there—

"Then why is it so overwhelming?"

"Because you're one jewel trying to reflect infinite jewels all at once. You need to learn to be still. To trust that the reflections will happen without your effort. To rest in the center of the net instead of grasping at every strand."

"How?"

"Practice. Time. And help from people who've done it before." Rinzen stood. "That's what the monastery is for. You're not alone anymore."

---

The days developed a rhythm.

5 AM: Meditation. (Lifan still struggled, but it got slightly easier.)

6 AM: Breakfast. (Oatmeal. Always oatmeal. Nydan complained daily.)

7 AM: Chores. (Everyone rotated—gardening, cooking, cleaning.)

9 AM: Training. (This varied. Sometimes physical exercise. Sometimes lectures on Buddhist philosophy. Sometimes one-on-one sessions with Rinzen.)

Noon: Lunch.

1 PM: Free time. (Most kids napped. Nydan played guitar badly. Lifan explored.)

3 PM: More training.

6 PM: Dinner.

7 PM: Evening meditation.

9 PM: Bed.

It was structured. Repetitive. Kind of boring.

Lifan loved it.

For the first time since his mom died, he felt safe. Stable. Like he could breathe.

Danny felt differently.

"This is like summer camp, but with more chanting," Danny said one afternoon. They were weeding the garden, which was Lifan's least favorite chore.

"You don't like it?"

"I mean, it's fine. Better than foster care. But like... what are we actually doing here? Are we monks now?"

"I think we're students."

"Students of what? Sitting quietly?"

Lifan laughed. "There's more to it than that."

"Is there though?"

Nydan, weeding nearby, snorted. "Told you. Enlightenment is boring."

"You're literally the worst Buddhist ever," Lifan said.

"Thank you."

---

Training with Rinzen was the opposite of boring.

In their one-on-one sessions, Rinzen pushed Lifan hard.

"Show me your threads," Rinzen said on day ten.

They were in Rinzen's small office. Sparse furniture, one window, shelves full of books.

Lifan closed his eyes and let his awareness expand.

The threads appeared. His connection to Danny, blazing in the next building. His connection to Rinzen, bright white-gold. Dozens of thinner threads to the other students.

"Good," Rinzen said. "Now find your mother."

Lifan's breath caught. But he searched.

There. Rising straight up, bright and distant.

"I feel her," Lifan whispered.

"Can you follow the thread? See where it goes?"

Lifan tried. He traced the thread upward, into the sky, beyond the sky, into something vast and formless.

And there, at the end, he felt her.

Not her body. Not her voice. Just... her essence. Love. Warmth. Presence.

"Māma," he breathed.

The presence pulsed. Recognition.

Tears streamed down Lifan's face.

"She's there," he said. "She's really there."

"Yes," Rinzen said gently. "The threads don't lie, Lifan. Death is not an ending. Just a transition."

Lifan opened his eyes. "Can I talk to her?"

"Not with words. But with feelings, yes. Intention. Send her love. She'll feel it."

Lifan closed his eyes again and poured everything into the thread. Love. Grief. Gratitude. I miss you. I'm okay. I'm learning.

The presence pulsed back. Warmth. Pride.

*Good, bǎobèi. Keep going.*

Not words. But he understood.

When he opened his eyes, Rinzen was smiling.

"You have a gift, Lifan. A rare one. Don't waste it."

"I won't," Lifan promised.

---

The next session, Rinzen taught him something new.

"Mudras." Rinzen held up his hands, thumb and forefinger touching. "Hand seals. They create circuits in your energy body. Close the loops, and the frequencies flow more smoothly."

Lifan tried the position. His fingers felt clumsy.

"This one is Chin mudra—consciousness seal. For basic awareness work. There are others. Dhyana mudra for deep meditation." Rinzen showed him—hands in his lap, palms up, thumbs touching. "Vajra mudra for protection." Fists with index fingers extended upward.

"Protection from what?"

"Other people's threads. Overwhelming input. Sometimes even..." Rinzen paused. "There are practitioners who can destabilize others through frequency manipulation. Ancient combat arts. We don't teach them here, but knowing they exist is important."

Lifan thought about Kevin. About all the people who'd tried to hurt him. "Could I learn? Not to attack, but to defend?"

"In time. First, master the basics." Rinzen smiled. "Now. Mantras. Seed syllables. LAM for grounding—white light. VAM for clarity—blue. RAM for connection—green. YAM for transmission—gold. HAM for integration—red-orange. And OM for dissolution—rainbow light."

"Those sound Buddhist."

"They are. They're also physics. Resonant frequencies. Vibration affecting matter. The ancients discovered this through practice. Modern science is starting to confirm it."

Lifan spent the next hour learning to hum the syllables while holding different mudras. It felt ridiculous. It also worked. When he combined LAM with Chin mudra, the overwhelming threads around him settled. When he used VAM, his perception sharpened.

"This is real," he said, stunned.

"Did you think I was making it up?"

"Kind of?"

Rinzen laughed. "Keep practicing. The mudras and mantras are tools. Master them, and you'll never be overwhelmed again."

---

Not everyone at the monastery was friendly.

Tenzin was fourteen, Tibetan, and had been at the monastery since he was nine. He was Rinzen's star pupil. The best at meditation, the most disciplined, the one all the other students looked up to.

Until Lifan arrived.

It started small. Tenzin correcting Lifan's posture during meditation. Commenting that Lifan was "distracting" during group sessions.

Then it escalated.

"You're doing it wrong," Tenzin said one day during training. They were practicing walking meditation—slow, deliberate steps around the courtyard.

"Doing what wrong?" Lifan asked.

"Everything. You're not focused. You're sloppy."

"Rinzen hasn't said that."

"Rinzen is being nice because you're new. But everyone can see you're not trying."

That stung. Lifan was trying. So hard.

"Leave him alone, Tenzin," Nydan said from across the courtyard.

"Stay out of this."

"Make me."

Tenzin glared. But he walked away.

"What's his problem?" Lifan asked.

Nydan shrugged. "You're getting a lot of attention from Rinzen. Tenzin's jealous."

"I'm not trying to—"

"Doesn't matter. Tenzin's been groomed to be the golden child. Now you show up with your freaky thread powers and Rinzen's all interested. Of course he's pissed."

Great. Another Kevin.

Lifan tried to avoid Tenzin after that. But the monastery was small. Avoidance only worked so well.

---

The crisis happened six weeks in.

Lifan was in the meditation hall, practicing alone. Trying to strengthen his focus, the way Rinzen had taught him.

But the threads were being weird.

They were... lashing out. Sparking. Like electricity.

Lifan frowned and tried to calm them. But they wouldn't settle.

One of the threads—his connection to Nydan—suddenly flared bright.

Too bright.

Nydan, in the next building, yelped.

"Ow! What the hell?"

Lifan's eyes flew open. "What did I do?"

He ran outside. Nydan was in the courtyard, clutching his chest.

"Did you just, like, zap me?" Nydan demanded.

"I don't know! Maybe?"

"Dude, that hurt."

"I'm sorry!"

Rinzen appeared. "What happened?"

"Lifan shocked me. Through the thread or whatever."

Rinzen looked at Lifan. "You pushed energy through your connection?"

"I didn't mean to!"

"Interesting." Rinzen stroked his chin. "Your control is slipping. Come. We need to work on this."

They spent the rest of the day on control exercises. Rinzen taught Lifan how to contain his energy, how to keep the threads from lashing out.

"Your power is growing," Rinzen said. "But without discipline, it's dangerous. You could hurt someone."

"I don't want to hurt anyone."

"I know. That's why we train." Rinzen paused, studying him. "There's something else I should teach you. Three theories. They explain why any of this works."

Lifan waited.

"First: Pattern Theory. Evolution isn't random survival—it's recognition seeking itself. You're not an accident. You're the universe trying to see itself more clearly."

"That sounds... big."

"It is. Second: the Recognition Amendment. Physics says observation collapses possibilities into reality. But it's not observation. It's recognition. Pattern seeing pattern. That's what you do when you look at threads—you're not just watching. You're resolving reality."

Lifan's head hurt. "And third?"

"The Kōng Proof. Emptiness equals compassion. Mathematically. When nothing exists independently, when everything is connected to everything else—the only logical response is love." Rinzen smiled. "You don't have to understand it now. Just remember: pattern, recognition, emptiness. Three keys. One door."

"What's behind the door?"

"You'll find out. When you're ready."

---

That night, Tenzin confronted him.

"You're reckless," Tenzin said. They were alone in the courtyard after dinner. "You shouldn't be here."

"Rinzen invited me."

"Rinzen makes mistakes. You're proof of that."

"What's your problem?" Lifan snapped. "I'm just trying to learn."

"Your trying isn't good enough. You're dangerous. You hurt Nydan today."

"It was an accident!"

"Accidents like that get people killed."

They stared at each other. Tenzin's thread pulsed with resentment. Dark, sticky.

And Lifan felt his own threads respond. Heating up. Ready to lash out.

No. Not again.

He took a breath. Forced calm.

"I'm sorry you feel that way," Lifan said quietly. "But I'm not leaving."

He walked away before his control slipped.

Behind him, Tenzin radiated fury.

---

Rinzen found Lifan on the roof that night. It was becoming his spot. Quiet. Away from everyone.

"Tenzin spoke to me," Rinzen said, sitting beside him.

"What did he say?"

"That you're dangerous. That I should send you away."

Lifan's stomach dropped. "Are you going to?"

"No."

Relief flooded through him.

"But Tenzin isn't entirely wrong," Rinzen continued. "You are dangerous, Lifan. Your gift is powerful. And power without wisdom is destructive."

"So what do I do?"

"You learn wisdom. Patience. Compassion. You learn to see your gift not as a weapon, but as a responsibility."

Lifan thought about Kevin. About using the threads to manipulate.

"What if I've already used it as a weapon?" he asked quietly.

Rinzen looked at him. "Have you?"

Lifan nodded. And then, for the first time, he told someone the whole story. The closet. Kevin. The manipulation.

When he finished, Rinzen was quiet for a long time.

"Do you regret it?" Rinzen asked finally.

"I don't know. Kevin deserved it. But... it felt wrong. After."

"Good."

"Good?"

"Regret means you have a conscience. That you understand the weight of what you did." Rinzen put a hand on Lifan's shoulder. "We all make mistakes, Lifan. What matters is what we learn from them. You used your gift in anger. In fear. Now you have a choice. Continue down that path, or choose differently."

"I want to choose differently."

"Then you will. One decision at a time."

They sat in silence, watching stars.

"Rinzen?" Lifan said. "Can I ask you something?"

"Always."

"Why do you tell such terrible jokes?"

Rinzen laughed. "Because enlightenment is serious enough. Someone has to lighten the mood."

"But they're really bad."

"That's what makes them good."

Lifan groaned. But he was smiling.

---

By the end of summer, Lifan had settled in.

The meditation was still hard. The chores were still boring. Tenzin still hated him.

But he was learning.

Learning to control the threads. To filter them. To use his gift with intention instead of impulse.

Learning that he didn't have to be alone.

Danny was thriving too. Turned out, a monastery was a great place for a kid with ADHD. Structure, routine, physical work. Danny was calmer here than Lifan had ever seen him.

"Think we'll stay forever?" Danny asked one night.

They were in their room—a small shared space, but it was theirs.

"I don't know," Lifan said. "Maybe."

"Would that be so bad? Becoming monks?"

Lifan considered. "I don't think I'm monk material."

"Me neither. But this is nice. For now."

"Yeah," Lifan agreed. "For now."

Outside, the mountains were dark and quiet.

Inside, Lifan's threads glowed steady.

Home, he thought.

Not forever. But for now, home.

## CHAPTER 6: THE GIRL

Nima arrived in October.

Lifan was in the garden, harvesting squash, when Rinzen's truck rattled up the driveway. He glanced up, curious. They didn't get many visitors.

The passenger door opened.

And she stepped out.

Lifan's heart stopped.

She was maybe thirteen, his age. Tibetan, with long black hair in a braid and eyes that seemed to see right through him. She wore jeans and a faded hoodie, and she moved like someone who'd learned to take up as little space as possible.

But her threads.

Oh god, her threads.

They were different from anyone Lifan had ever seen. Not gold. Not white like Rinzen's. They were iridescent. Rainbow. They shifted colors as they moved, cycling through the spectrum—red to orange to yellow to green to blue.

Lifan stared.

The girl looked right at him. And smiled.

"You must be Lifan," she said. "I'm Nima."

Her voice was low, calm. She had a slight accent—Tibetan, probably.

Lifan realized he was still staring. "Uh. Hi."

Smooth. Real smooth.

Nydan appeared from the kitchen. "New student?"

"This is Nima," Rinzen said. "She'll be joining us. Nima, this is Nydan and Lifan. Where's Danny?"

"Napping," Lifan said. "He's always napping."

Nima laughed. It was a nice laugh. Warm.

Lifan's face felt hot.

"Come," Rinzen said to Nima. "I'll show you your room."

They walked toward the dormitory. Nima glanced back once, caught Lifan's eye, and smiled again.

Lifan stood there holding a squash like an idiot.

"Dude," Nydan said. "You're blushing."

"Shut up."

"You are! Oh man, this is great. Lifan has a crush."

"I do not."

"You absolutely do."

Lifan threw the squash at him. Nydan dodged, cackling.

---

Dinner that night was weird.

Everyone wanted to talk to Nima. Where was she from? (A village in Tibet, near Mount Kailash.) How did she end up here? (Long story, involved a refugee camp and a lot of paperwork.) Could she see threads too? (She just smiled and said, "Something like that.")

Lifan sat across from her and tried not to stare.

Failed, mostly.

She caught him looking at least three times. Each time, she smiled like she knew exactly what he was thinking.

It was mortifying.

After dinner, during evening meditation, Lifan couldn't focus at all.

Nima was sitting two rows ahead. He could see her threads, dancing with color. They were mesmerizing.

"Lifan," Rinzen said quietly. "Focus."

Right. Meditation. Not staring at the new girl.

He closed his eyes and tried to find his center.

But all he could feel was Nima's presence. Bright and strange and utterly distracting.

This was going to be a problem.

---

Over the next few days, Lifan learned more about Nima.

She was fourteen, a year older than him. She'd been on her own for two years, bouncing between refugee organizations and temporary placements. She'd found Rinzen through a network of Buddhist teachers.

"He's kind of famous," Nima explained. They were washing dishes together after lunch. "In certain circles. People who know about... abilities."

"Like threads?"

"Like threads. And other things."

"What can you do?" Lifan asked. "I mean, your threads are different. I've never seen anyone with rainbow threads."

Nima glanced at him. "You can see them? The colors?"

"Yeah. They're beautiful."

She blushed. Actually blushed.

Lifan's heart did a weird flip.

"I can do something similar to you," Nima said. "But it's not just threads. It's... energy. Life force. I can see it in everything. People, animals, plants. Even rocks, kind of."

"That sounds overwhelming."

"It is. That's why I'm here. To learn control." She handed him a plate to dry. "What about you? Why are you here?"

Lifan hesitated. Then he told her. About his mom. His dad. Foster care. Running away with Danny. Finding Rinzen.

Nima listened without interrupting.

"That's a lot," she said when he finished.

"Yeah."

"But you're okay now? Here?"

"I think so. For the first time in a long time, yeah."

She smiled. "Good. Me too."

Their hands brushed as she handed him another plate.

A thread formed.

Instant, bright, rainbow-gold.

Lifan gasped.

Nima's eyes widened. "You felt that?"

"Yeah. Did you?"

"Yes."

They stared at each other.

The thread between them pulsed. Warm. Alive.

"Weird," Nima said softly.

"Yeah."

But she didn't let go of the plate.

Neither did he.

---

Nydan noticed immediately.

"Oh, you've got it bad," Nydan said that night. He, Lifan, and Danny were in their room, supposedly sleeping.

"I don't know what you're talking about," Lifan said.

"Nima. You're obsessed."

"I'm not obsessed."

"You stared at her for literally the entire afternoon."

"That's an exaggeration."

"Is it though?" Danny chimed in. "Because I noticed it too."

"Traitor," Lifan muttered.

Danny grinned. "Hey, I think it's cute. Lifan's first crush."

"It's not a crush."

"Dude, your thread to her is probably visible from space."

Lifan threw a pillow at him.

But he couldn't deny it. He was... something. Interested. Fascinated.

Okay, fine. Crushing.

It was weird. He'd never felt like this before. Around Nima, his brain got fuzzy. His palms sweated. He forgot how to form sentences.

It was the worst.

It was the best.

"Just talk to her," Nydan said. "Like, for real. Not just dish-washing small talk."

"And say what?"

"I don't know. Ask her about her life. Her interests. Literally anything."

"What if she's not interested in me?"

"Then at least you'll know," Danny said. "But dude. I've seen the way she looks at you. She's interested."

Lifan's heart did the flip thing again.

"Really?"

"Really."

He waited a week before working up the courage.

They were on chore duty together again—weeding the garden. Rinzen had a habit of pairing them up. Lifan suspected it was on purpose.

"Can I ask you something?" Lifan said.

Nima looked up from the carrots. "Sure."

"Do you miss Tibet?"

She was quiet for a moment. "Yes and no. I miss the mountains. The sky. But I don't miss... everything else. The occupation. The fear. It's complicated."

"I get that. I miss my mom. But I don't miss everything that came after she died."

"Grief is weird like that," Nima said. "You can miss someone and also be relieved that part of your life is over."

"Yeah. Exactly."

They worked in silence for a while.

"What do you want to do?" Nima asked. "Like, after here. Do you want to stay? Become a monk?"

Lifan laughed. "I don't think I'm monk material. You?"

"I don't know. Maybe I'll be a teacher. Help other kids with abilities."

"That would be cool. You'd be good at it."

"You think?"

"Yeah. You're patient. Kind. You explain things well."

She smiled. "Thanks."

Another silence. Comfortable this time.

"Lifan?" Nima said.

"Yeah?"

"I like talking to you."

His face went hot. "I like talking to you too."

"Good."

The thread between them glowed brighter.

Over the next few weeks, they became friends.

Lifan learned that Nima loved astronomy. That she could name every constellation visible from the Northern Hemisphere. That she wanted to see the aurora borealis someday.

Nima learned that Lifan was a terrible cook but great at math. That he had nightmares sometimes about the closet. That he secretly loved cheesy action movies.

They watched Pacific Rim (the original, not the terrible sequels). Lifan and Nima sat next to each other on the floor, sharing a blanket.

Halfway through, their hands touched.

Neither of them moved away.

Lifan could barely focus on the movie. All his attention was on the point where their fingers overlapped. The warmth. The thread between them, blazing.

When the movie ended, Nima squeezed his hand once before letting go.

Lifan floated back to his room.

"You're smiling like an idiot," Danny observed.

"Shut up."

But he didn't stop smiling.

The cliff became their spot.

There was a place on the monastery grounds—a rocky outcrop that overlooked the valley. You could see for miles. Mountains, forest, a silver ribbon of river far below.

Lifan had gone there a few times to think. Alone.

Then one day, Nima followed him.

"Mind if I join?" she asked.

"Please."

They sat on the edge, legs dangling.

"It's beautiful here," Nima said.

"Yeah."

"Do you ever think about leaving?"

Lifan glanced at her. "Sometimes. But I don't know where I'd go. This is the first place that's felt safe since... since my mom."

"I get that."

They sat in silence, watching the sun dip toward the mountains.

"Lifan?" Nima said.

"Yeah?"

"Can I tell you something?"

"Always."

She took a breath. "When I first got here, I didn't think I'd fit in. Everyone seemed so... settled. Like they'd found their place. And I felt like I was just drifting."

"Now I think maybe I found my place too." She looked at him. "Because of you."

Lifan's breath caught.

"Me?"

"You make me feel less alone. Like someone finally understands."

"I do understand," Lifan said. "Completely."

Their threads pulsed in sync.

"Can I try something?" Nima asked.

"Okay."

She took his hand. Not just holding—really touching, intentionally.

"Close your eyes," she said.

He did.

"Now breathe. Match my rhythm."

He felt her breath through their connection. In for four. Hold. Out for four.

He matched it.

And something shifted.

Their threads merged. Not just connected—merged. He could feel what she felt. Her calm. Her contentment. Her nervousness.

Her affection.

For him.

"Whoa," he breathed.

"Right?" She was smiling. He could feel it. "This is what I can do. Energy sharing. We're breathing together. Feeling together."

"This is incredible."

"It gets better."

She pulled more energy through their connection. Warmth flooded him. Safety. Belonging.

Love.

Not romantic love. Not yet. But the foundation of it. Care. Trust.

Lifan opened his eyes.

Nima was glowing. Literally. Her threads were so bright they illuminated her face.

"You're beautiful," he said without thinking.

She blushed. "You can't even see me. Your eyes are closed."

"I can feel you. That's better."

She laughed. But she didn't let go of his hand.

They sat there until the stars came out, breathing together.

## CHAPTER 7: THE KISS

The autumn equinox ceremony was a monastery tradition.

Rinzen explained it at dinner the night before. "We gather to honor the balance of light and dark. To reflect on what we're grateful for. And to set intentions for the coming season."

"Is there food?" Nydan asked.

"Yes, Nydan. There's food."

"Then I'm in."

Everyone laughed.

The ceremony was held at sunset, in the courtyard. Prayer flags fluttered overhead. Candles lined the paths.

It was beautiful.

Everyone sat in a circle. Rinzen led them through a meditation, then invited people to share.

Tenzin went first. Talked about gratitude for the dharma, for discipline, for the path to enlightenment.

Very Tenzin.

Nydan went next. "I'm grateful for food. And sleep. And that Rinzen hasn't kicked me out yet."

Rinzen shook his head, smiling.

Danny shared about finding family. "I thought I was alone. But I'm not. I have brothers here. That's everything."

He looked at Lifan.

Lifan's throat tightened.

Then it was Nima's turn.

"I'm grateful for safety," she said. "For people who understand. For not having to hide."

She looked at Lifan too.

His heart pounded.

When it was his turn, he said, "I'm grateful for second chances. For people who didn't give up on me. And for learning that different doesn't mean broken."

The circle hummed with agreement.

After the sharing, there was food. Someone had made momos. Nydan ate approximately a million of them.

Lifan found Nima by the fire.

"Hey," he said.

"Hey."

"That was nice. What you said."

"You too."

They stood close. Not touching, but almost.

The fire crackled. Music played softly—someone had brought a speaker.

"Do you want to dance?" Nima asked suddenly.

"I don't know how to dance."

"Me neither. We can be bad at it together."

She held out her hand.

Lifan took it.

They swayed to the music. Awkward and laughing and perfect.

Her threads wrapped around him. Rainbow and gold, impossibly bright.

"Lifan?" she said.

"Yeah?"

"I really like you."

His heart stopped. Started. Went into overdrive.

"I really like you too."

"Good."

She stepped closer. They were barely moving now, just standing in each other's space.

"Can I kiss you?" she asked.

Lifan forgot how to breathe. "Yes."

She leaned in.

Their lips met.

Soft. Tentative. Lifan's first kiss ever, and he had no idea what he was doing, but it didn't matter because Nima was kissing him and the world was exploding into light.

And then—

The threads.

He'd seen merged threads before, during deep meditation. But this was different. Their F3 frequencies—resonance, connection—didn't just touch. They *braided*. His silver-gold weaving into her rainbow light, the two threads becoming something new. Something neither of them had been alone.

He could feel her heartbeat in his chest. Her nervousness that was also excitement. Her wonder that mirrored his own.

For one impossible second, he couldn't tell where he ended and she began.

Then the kiss broke, gently, and they were separate again.

When they pulled apart, they were both grinning like idiots.

"Wow," Lifan said.

"Yeah."

Across the courtyard, Nydan was watching. He gave them a thumbs up.

Lifan laughed.

"Come on," Nima said, taking his hand. "Let's go to the cliff."

---

The cliff was quiet. Away from the ceremony, the music, the other students.

They sat on the edge, holding hands.

"I've never done that before," Lifan said.

"Kissed someone?"

"Yeah."

"Me neither."

"Really?"

"Really."

They sat in comfortable silence.

"We don't have to hide this, right?" Lifan asked. "Us?"

"Why would we hide?"

"I don't know. I'm just... I've spent so long hiding things. My abilities. My feelings. I don't want to hide this too."

Nima squeezed his hand. "Then we don't hide. We just... are."

"I like that."

"Me too."

They kissed again. Slower this time. Less tentative.

When they finally walked back to the monastery, hand in hand, everyone saw.

Nydan whooped. Danny grinned. Even Tenzin looked... well, less disapproving than usual.

Rinzen just smiled.

"Young love," he said to no one in particular. "How wonderful."

---

The next few weeks were the happiest of Lifan's life.

He and Nima were together constantly. Training together. Eating together. Sitting together during meditation.

They learned to merge their threads intentionally. To share energy. To communicate without words, just through feeling.

It was intimate in a way Lifan had never experienced.

"This is what connection is supposed to feel like," Nima said one day. They were in the library, supposedly studying.

"What do you mean?"

"No hiding. No pretending. Just... being seen. Completely."

"Yeah," Lifan said. "Exactly."

"I don't want this to end."

"It won't."

But even as he said it, he felt a flicker of doubt.

Threads didn't break. But they changed.

And Lifan had learned that nothing stayed the same forever.

## CHAPTER 8: THE WARNING

Rinzen summoned Lifan to his office in early November.

"We need to talk," Rinzen said.

His tone was serious. The threads around him were subdued.

Lifan's stomach dropped. "About what?"

"Sit."

Lifan sat.

Rinzen looked at him for a long moment. Then he sighed.

"There's something I haven't told you. About your gift."

"Okay..."

"Your mother had a vision. Before she died. About you."

Lifan went still. "What kind of vision?"

"The kind people with your ability sometimes have. Seeing threads not just in the present, but in potential futures. She saw yours."

"And?"

Rinzen's expression was pained. "Lifan, your thread is bright. Brighter than anyone's I've ever seen. You're going to do important things. Touch countless lives. But..."

"But what?"

"Your thread is also short."

The words didn't make sense at first.

"Short?" Lifan repeated.

"You're going to die young, Lifan. Before you're twenty-five."

The world tilted.

"That's... that's not possible. You can't know that."

"Your mother saw it. And I've seen it too, in your threads. The brightness is beautiful, but it's also brief. Like a shooting star."

Lifan couldn't breathe. "How? How do I die?"

"I don't know. The vision wasn't specific. Just that your time is limited."

"Can I stop it? Change it?"

"I don't think so. Some things are fixed, Lifan. This appears to be one of them."

Rage exploded through him. "Then why tell me?! Why not just let me be happy?!"

"Because you deserve to know. To make choices with full information."

"What choices?! I'm fourteen! I just—Nima and I just—" He couldn't finish.

Rinzen's face was full of compassion. "I'm sorry. I know the timing is terrible."

"Does she know? Nima?"

"I don't know. You'll have to ask her."

Lifan stood. "I need to go."

"Lifan—"

"I need to go!"

He ran.

---

He found Nima in the garden.

She looked up, smiling. Then she saw his face.

"What's wrong?"

"Did you know?" Lifan demanded.

"Know what?"

"That I'm going to die young. That my thread is short. Did you know?!"

Nima's face went pale.

Silence.

"You did," Lifan said. "You knew."

"Lifan—"

"When? When did you know?"

"The first day I saw you. I could see it in your threads. The brightness. The brevity."

Anger roared through him. "And you didn't tell me?!"

"What was I supposed to say?! 'Hi, nice to meet you, by the way you're going to die young'?!"

"Yes! You should have told me before—before we—" He gestured helplessly between them.

"Before we what? Fell for each other? Would that have changed anything?"

"I don't know! Maybe!"

"I didn't tell you because I didn't want you to live in fear," Nima said, tears streaming down her face. "I wanted you to be happy. Even if it's brief."

"You don't get to make that choice for me!"

"You're right. I'm sorry. But Lifan, please—"

"I need to be alone."

"Lifan—"

"Please."

He walked away.

Behind him, Nima's sobs echoed.

---

Lifan went to the cliff.

He sat on the edge and stared at the valley.

Dying young. Before twenty-five. That was... what, ten years? Maybe less?

Everything he'd started to build—the training, the relationships, the future—it was all temporary.

Even more temporary than he'd thought.

He pulled out the threads. His mother's, rising into the sky. Danny's, blazing in the monastery. Nima's, rainbow and beautiful and connected to his own.

They were all real. All strong.

But his was short.

*Why?* he screamed silently at the universe. *Why give me this gift if I'm just going to die?*

No answer.

Of course not.

He sat there until dark.

---

Danny found him.

"Hey," Danny said, sitting beside him. "Nima's freaking out. Said you guys had a fight."

"Rinzen told me I'm going to die young."

Danny went very still. "What?"

Lifan told him everything.

When he finished, Danny was quiet for a long time.

"That's bullshit," Danny said finally.

"It's a vision. From my mom. And Rinzen confirmed it."

"Still bullshit. You can't know the future. Not for sure."

"They seem pretty sure."

"Screw what they think. You're alive right now. That's what matters."

"For how long?"

"Who knows? I could get hit by a bus tomorrow. None of us know how long we have. You just happen to have a really depressing estimate."

Despite everything, Lifan laughed. "That's one way to put it."

"Look," Danny said. "You can spend the next ten years being pissed and scared. Or you can spend them living. Your choice."

"It doesn't feel like a choice."

"It is though. Trust me. I've been in dark places. The only way out is to choose to keep going."

Lifan looked at his best friend. "When did you get so wise?"

"I've always been wise. You just weren't paying attention."

They sat in silence.

"What are you going to do about Nima?" Danny asked.

"I don't know. I'm so mad at her."

"She was trying to protect you."

"By lying?"

"By letting you be happy. There's a difference."

Lifan knew Danny was right. But the anger was still there.

"I need time," he said.

"Okay. But don't take too long. Life's short, remember?"

"Wow. Too soon."

Danny grinned. "Too soon? Or perfect timing?"

Lifan shoved him. But he was smiling.

---

He avoided Nima for two days.

It wasn't hard. The monastery was small, but if you tried, you could stay out of someone's way.

But on the third day, she cornered him.

"We need to talk," she said.

They were in the courtyard. Other students were around, but far enough away for privacy.

"I'm listening," Lifan said.

"I'm sorry. For not telling you. I thought I was helping, but I was wrong. You deserved to know."

"Yeah. I did."

"I understand if you hate me."

"I don't hate you." Lifan sighed. "I'm just... I'm angry. And scared. And I don't know what to do with that."

"Me neither."

They stood there, not quite looking at each other.

"Do you want to break up?" Nima asked quietly.

"Do you?"

"No. But if you do, I understand."

Lifan thought about it. Really thought.

He was going to die young. That was terrifying. And it meant any relationship he had would end in grief.

But pushing Nima away wouldn't change that. It would just mean less happiness in the time he had.

"I don't want to break up," he said.

Nima's face flooded with relief. "Really?"

"Really. But I need you to be honest with me. Always. Even if the truth sucks."

"I promise."

"Okay."

"Okay?"

"Okay."

She stepped closer. "Can I hug you?"

"Yes."

She wrapped her arms around him. Their threads merged, warm and familiar.

"I'm sorry," she whispered.

"I know. Me too."

They stood there for a long time.

"So what now?" Nima asked.

Lifan pulled back, looking at her. "Now we live. For however long we've got. No more hiding. No more wasting time."

"That sounds good."

"Yeah?"

"Yeah."

She kissed him. Soft and sweet and full of promise.

When they pulled apart, they were both smiling.

"Let's have the good part," Lifan said.

"The good part?"

"You know. Before the bad part comes. Let's just... have the good part."

Nima's eyes were bright. "Okay. Let's have the good part."

They walked back to the monastery, hand in hand.

The future was uncertain. Short, maybe. But it was theirs.

And for now, that was enough.

## CHAPTER 9: THE YEARS

Lifan turned fifteen in March.

Nima made him a cake. Well, she tried. It came out lopsided and the frosting was basically soup, but it tasted fine.

"It's the thought that counts," she said defensively.

"It's perfect," Lifan said, and meant it.

They ate cake on the cliff, watching the sunset paint the valley gold.

Two years at the monastery. Two years of training, meditation, learning to control the threads. Two years of Nima's hand in his, of Danny's terrible jokes, of Nydan's complaints, of Rinzen's patient teaching.

Two years closer to death.

He tried not to think about it. Mostly succeeded.

But sometimes, late at night, the fear crept in. *How much time do I have left? Five years? Ten?*

"Stop spiraling," Danny would say from the other bed. "I can hear you thinking from here."

"How do you do it?" Lifan asked once. "Not worry about the future?"

"Who says I don't worry? I just don't let it eat me alive. One day at a time, remember?"

One day at a time.

Lifan tried.

---

Age fifteen was the year Rinzen introduced dissolution meditation.

"This practice," Rinzen said, "teaches us to let go. To release our attachment to permanence."

They were in the meditation hall. All fifteen students—there were new kids now, younger ones who looked at Lifan like he was ancient.

"What's dissolution?" a nine-year-old named Kai asked.

"It's the practice of imagining your body dissolving," Rinzen said calmly. "Piece by piece. Until nothing remains but consciousness."

"That sounds terrifying," Kai said.

"It is, at first. But it's also liberating. We spend so much energy clinging to our bodies, our identities. Dissolution teaches us that we are more than flesh. More than this temporary form."

Lifan shifted uncomfortably. He knew where this was going.

"For some of us," Rinzen continued, looking directly at him, "dissolution practice is particularly important. It prepares us for what comes after."

After.

Death.

Lifan's chest tightened.

---

He tried the practice that afternoon.

Sit. Breathe. Imagine your feet dissolving. Then your legs. Your torso. Your arms.

He got to his knees before panic hit.

His breath went shallow. His heart raced. The threads around him blazed too bright, overwhelming.

He opened his eyes, gasping.

Nima was next to him, hand on his arm. "Breathe. You're okay."

"I can't do this," he whispered.

"You don't have to. Not yet."

But Rinzen's voice came from the front. "Lifan. Try again."

"I can't—"

"You can. Fear is natural. Breathe through it."

Lifan wanted to argue. Wanted to run.

But he closed his eyes.

Feet. Legs. Knees. Thighs.

His body dissolving into light.

Terror crashed over him like a wave.

*This is what dying feels like.*

But underneath the terror—something else. A whisper of relief. Of release.

He pushed further. Torso dissolving. Chest. Heart.

The threads exploded outward. He felt everyone in the room, their consciousness, their connections.

He felt the mountains. The trees. The sky.

He felt his mother, distant and bright.

*I'm still here,* he realized. *Even without a body, I'm still here.*

And then he came back.

Gasping. Shaking. But alive.

"Good," Rinzen said. "That's the first step."

Lifan opened his eyes. Nima was crying.

"You scared me," she whispered.

"I scared myself."

But he'd done it.

And maybe—maybe—when the time came, dying wouldn't be as terrifying as he feared.

Maybe.

---

Age sixteen was the year Nydan gave up on enlightenment officially.

"I've decided I'm a terrible Buddhist," Nydan announced at dinner one night. "I'm making it my brand."

"You've always been a terrible Buddhist," Tenzin said.

"Yeah, but now I'm *leaning into it.*"

Everyone laughed.

Nydan had changed over the years. Still sarcastic, still complained constantly, but there was an edge of desperation now. He was eighteen. Technically an adult. He could leave whenever he wanted.

But he didn't.

"Where would I go?" he said to Lifan once. They were in the library, supposedly studying. "I've been here since I was fourteen. This is all I know."

"You could go to college," Lifan suggested.

"With what money? What grades? I've been sitting on cushions for four years learning about the nature of suffering. That doesn't translate to a college application."

"So you're just going to stay here forever?"

Nydan was quiet for a long time. "No. I'm going to find another way."

"What way?"

"I don't know yet. But Lifan, listen. This dissolution stuff Rinzen's teaching you—it's not the only option."

Lifan frowned. "What do you mean?"

"I mean, maybe there's another way. To extend your time. Or change your fate. Or—I don't know. Something other than just accepting death."

"Rinzen says acceptance is the path."

"Rinzen says a lot of things. Doesn't mean he's always right."

It was the first time anyone had suggested there might be an alternative.

The seed planted itself in Lifan's mind.

---

Age sixteen was also the year Tenzin apologized.

It happened in the courtyard. Lifan was practicing walking meditation—slow, deliberate steps, focused breathing.

Tenzin appeared beside him.

"Can I join you?"

Lifan almost said no. But something in Tenzin's thread—vulnerability, uncertainty—made him pause.

"Sure."

They walked in silence for a while.

Then Tenzin said, "I've been unfair to you."

Lifan blinked. "What?"

"When you first arrived. I was jealous. You had this gift, and Rinzen was so interested in you, and I... I felt replaced."

"Oh."

"I know that's not an excuse. I was cruel. I'm sorry."

Lifan looked at him. Tenzin's thread was genuine. No manipulation. Just honest regret.

"I forgive you," Lifan said.

"Really?"

"Really. We were kids. We're still kids. It's fine."

Tenzin's relief was visible. "Thank you."

They walked together. And slowly, carefully, a new thread formed between them.

Not friendship, exactly. But mutual respect.

It was enough.

---

Age seventeen was the year Rinzen made the announcement.

It was spring. Nearly three years since Lifan had arrived at the monastery. He was taller now, broader, his voice deeper. Almost an adult.

*Almost.*

Rinzen called him to his office after morning meditation.

"Sit," Rinzen said.

Lifan sat. His stomach twisted. Something was wrong. He could feel it in Rinzen's thread—sadness, determination.

"It's time for you to leave," Rinzen said.

The world tilted.

"What?"

"You've learned what you can here. The next phase of your training—your preparation—needs to happen in the world. Away from this place."

"I don't want to leave."

"I know. But Lifan, you're seventeen. You have, at most, seven or eight years left. You need to live. Experience things. Form connections. Not hide in the mountains."

"I'm not hiding—"

"You are. We all are, in a way. That's what monasteries are for. But you can't spend your whole short life here. You need to see the world."

Panic rose in Lifan's chest. "Where would I even go?"

"San Francisco. There's a community there. People who understand threads, energy work, the spiritual paths. I've made arrangements. You'll have a place to stay. Support."

"What about Nima? Danny? Nydan?"

"They'll stay here. For now. Nima still has training to complete. Danny is still figuring out his path. Nydan..." Rinzen smiled sadly. "Nydan is on his own journey."

"I can't leave them."

"You have to."

"Why?"

"Because isolation isn't living, Lifan. And you deserve to live before you die."

The truth of it hit like a punch.

He was hiding. Using the monastery as a shield. But Rinzen was right—he couldn't spend his remaining years here, pretending the world outside didn't exist.

"When?" Lifan asked quietly.

"Two weeks."

Two weeks.

Not enough time. Not nearly enough.

"Okay," Lifan whispered.

Rinzen's expression softened. "This isn't goodbye forever. You can visit. And they can visit you. But you need to stand on your own feet. Learn who you are outside these walls."

"I'm scared."

"Good. Fear means you're growing."

---

Telling Nima was the hardest thing he'd ever done.

They went to the cliff. Their spot.

"Rinzen's sending me to San Francisco," Lifan said.

Nima went very still. "When?"

"Two weeks."

"Oh."

Just "oh." But her thread cracked with pain.

"I don't want to go," Lifan said. "I want to stay with you."

"But you can't."

"No."

They sat in silence.

"How long?" Nima asked finally.

"I don't know. Maybe... maybe permanently. Rinzen thinks I need to live in the world. Not up here."

"He's right."

"That doesn't make it easier."

"No." She took his hand. "But Lifan, listen. You're going to do amazing things. Meet amazing people. Have experiences I can't even imagine."

"I don't want experiences. I want you."

"You have me. Distance doesn't change that. Threads don't break, remember?"

"But what if you meet someone else? Someone who's actually here?"

Nima looked at him. "I won't."

"You don't know that."

"I do. Lifan, you're it for me. Even if we're apart. Even if we're only together for a few more years. You're it."

His throat closed. "You're it for me too."

She kissed him. Desperate. Sad. Full of everything they couldn't say.

When they pulled apart, they were both crying.

"Two weeks," she whispered.

"Two weeks."

They spent every possible moment together after that. Walking. Talking. Sitting in silence. Memorizing each other.

On the last night, they lay on the cliff, watching stars.

"Promise me something," Nima said.

"Anything."

"Promise you'll actually live. Don't spend your whole time scared. Don't waste it."

"I promise."

"And promise you'll come back. Even just to visit. I need to know I'll see you again."

"I promise."

She turned to face him. "I love you, Lifan."

It was the first time she'd said it.

"I love you too," he said.

They held each other until dawn.

---

Danny took it better.

"San Francisco?" he said. "Dude, that's awesome. You're going to have so much fun."

"I'm leaving you."

"You're not leaving me. You're just going first. I'll follow eventually. We're brothers, remember? Can't get rid of me that easy."

"Promise?"

"Promise."

They did their handshake—complicated, ridiculous, perfected over four years.

"Don't do anything stupid without me," Danny said.

"Can't promise that."

"Fair."

---

Nydan cornered Lifan the day before he left.

"So you're actually going," Nydan said.

"Yeah."

"Good. This place is boring anyway."

"Nydan—"

"No, listen. When you're out there, in the city, living your life—remember what I said. About alternatives."

"What alternatives?"

Nydan pulled out a piece of paper. "I've been researching. There are people studying consciousness transfer. Biotech companies working on life extension. Fringe scientists exploring the relationship between energy and physical form."

"What does that have to do with me?"

"Everything. Lifan, what if dying young isn't inevitable? What if there's a way to transfer your consciousness somewhere else? Extend your existence?"

"That's science fiction."

"So were smartphones once. So was the internet. Technology evolves. Consciousness studies are advancing. There might be options we haven't considered."

Lifan stared at the paper. Names. Companies. Research projects.

"Why are you giving me this?"

"Because you deserve a choice. Not just acceptance. A real choice." Nydan's expression was intense. "I'm going to keep researching. And when I find something real—something viable—I'll tell you. Okay?"

"Okay."

"Don't give up, Lifan. There might be another way."

For the first time in years, Lifan felt something besides fear about his future.

Hope.

---

The morning he left, everyone gathered in the courtyard.

All fifteen students. Rinzen. Even the chickens seemed to be watching.

"This isn't goodbye," Rinzen said. "It's transition. Lifan is moving to the next phase of his journey. We celebrate that."

They did a ceremony. Chanting. Meditation. Sharing memories.

Tenzin talked about the first time Lifan accidentally zapped him with thread energy. "I was so mad. But now I think it's funny."

Kai, the nine-year-old, talked about how Lifan always helped him with meditation when he was struggling.

Danny talked about brothers.

Nima couldn't speak. She just hugged him, hard, and whispered, "Come back to me."

"Always," he promised.

Then Rinzen drove him down the mountain in the old pickup truck.

Lifan watched the monastery disappear behind them.

His home for three years.

His safe place.

Gone.

"You'll be okay," Rinzen said. "I have faith in you."

Lifan wished he had faith in himself.

The truck wound down the mountain roads, toward the coast, toward Highway 1, toward the city.

Toward whatever came next.

Lifan pulled out his thread to Nima. It stretched behind him, golden and strong.

*Threads don't break,* he reminded himself.

But they could get very, very long.

## CHAPTER 10: THE CITY

San Francisco hit Lifan like a wall of noise.

The city had threads. So many threads.

From the bus window, he could see them—millions of connections forming a web so dense it looked solid. Golden light everywhere, tangled and chaotic and overwhelming.

His head started pounding immediately.

"This is your stop," the driver called. "Embarcadero."

Lifan grabbed his backpack and stumbled off the bus.

The sidewalk was crowded. People everywhere. Tourists with cameras. Commuters with coffee. Homeless people with cardboard signs. Street performers. Dogs. Pigeons.

Everyone had threads.

And Lifan could see all of them.

He pressed his back against a building and closed his eyes, trying to breathe. Trying to filter the way Rinzen had taught him.

Focus on one thing. Ignore the rest.

But there was no "one thing." There was only chaos.

"You okay, kid?"

Lifan opened his eyes. An old woman was looking at him with concern.

"Fine," he managed. "Just... tired."

"You visiting?"

"Moving here."

"Good luck. City'll eat you alive if you're not careful."

She walked away.

*Great pep talk,* Lifan thought.

He pulled out the address Rinzen had given him. An apartment in the Mission District. Prepaid for six months.

He had no idea how to get there.

---

He figured out the bus system eventually. Mostly. He got lost twice, but the second time a friendly stranger gave him directions.

The Mission was different from downtown. More residential. Colorful murals on the buildings. Shops with signs in Spanish. The smell of tacos and exhaust.

The apartment was above a laundromat.

Lifan climbed the stairs and found the door. Number 3B. The key Rinzen gave him fit.

Inside was... small.

One room. A futon that doubled as a bed and couch. A kitchenette. A bathroom barely big enough to turn around in. One window looking out on the street.

But it was his.

Lifan set down his backpack and sat on the futon.

Silence.

No morning meditation bell. No Danny snoring in the next bed. No Nima's footsteps in the hall.

Just traffic noise and the hum of the laundromat below.

He pulled out his phone and called Nima.

"Hey," she answered immediately. "You made it?"

"Yeah. I'm here."

"How is it?"

"Overwhelming. Loud. Too many people."

"You'll adjust."

"Will I?"

"Yes. You're stronger than you think." Her voice softened. "I miss you already."

"I miss you too."

They talked for an hour. About nothing. About everything. Until Lifan's phone battery got low.

"I should go," he said reluctantly.

"Okay. Call me tomorrow?"

"Every day."

"Every day," she echoed.

They hung up.

Lifan lay on the futon and stared at the ceiling.

Day one in San Francisco.

He'd survived.

Barely.

The first week was survival.

Learning the bus routes. Finding a grocery store. Figuring out how to cook actual meals instead of eating monastery food.

He burned rice twice. Managed to set off the smoke alarm making toast.

Domesticity was hard.

But the threads were harder.

Every time he left the apartment, the city's web crashed over him. Millions of connections, all active, all demanding attention.

He started having panic attacks.

The first one happened at the grocery store. He was in the cereal aisle when suddenly the threads got too bright, too loud, and he couldn't breathe.

He abandoned his cart and ran outside, gasping.

An old man helped him sit on the curb. "Panic attack?"

Lifan nodded, unable to speak.

"Breathe with me. In. Out. In. Out."

Lifan followed. Slowly, his chest loosened.

"Thanks," he whispered.

"No problem. They pass. Just gotta ride it out."

The man left. Lifan sat there for twenty minutes, collecting himself.

*This is my life now,* he thought. *Constant overwhelm.*

He almost called Rinzen. Almost begged to come back.

But he'd promised Nima he'd try.

So he got up and went back inside.

---

The second week, Rinzen's "contact" reached out.

A text from an unknown number: *Hey, Lifan. Rinzen told me you arrived. Want to meet up? —Lo*

Lifan stared at the message.

He didn't want to meet anyone. He barely left the apartment anymore. The threads were too much.

But Rinzen had arranged this. It would be rude to refuse.

*Sure,* he texted back. *When?*

*Tomorrow. 2pm. Rohki Records on Valencia. You'll know it when you see it.*

Rohki Records was a record store.

Obviously.

But it was also more than that. The storefront had racks of vinyl, but in the back, Lifan could see a space with couches, art on the walls, people hanging out.

It felt... safe, somehow. The threads here were warm. Welcoming.

He pushed open the door. A bell chimed.

A girl looked up from behind the counter.

She was his age, maybe a year older. Chinese, with short black hair shaved on one side. Multiple piercings in her ears. A t-shirt that said "DEATH BEFORE DECAF."

Her threads were interesting. Sharp. Guarded. But underneath, bright with intelligence and pain.

"You Lifan?" she asked.

"Yeah. You Lo?"

"That's me. Come on. Let's talk in the back."

She led him past the records, through a curtain, into a lounge area.

Three other people were there. All young. All with threads that felt... different. Aware.

"This is the Rohki crew," Lo said. "Leky, Grey, Coco. Guys, this is Lifan. Rinzen's kid."

"I'm not Rinzen's kid," Lifan said automatically.

"You're his student. Close enough." Lo flopped onto a couch. "Sit. Relax. We don't bite."

Lifan sat carefully.

"So," Leky said. He was tall, white, maybe twenty. Dreadlocks and kind eyes that saw more than they let on. His threads were anchors—stable, grounding, the kind you grabbed when everything was falling apart. "Rinzen says you're a thread-seer."

Lifan tensed. "How do you know about threads?"

"Because we're all weird here," Lo said. "Leky does energy healing. Grey's got precognition. Coco's a synesthete who paints sounds. And I'm—well. I'm complicated."

"You're all... gifted?"

"That's one word for it," Grey said. He was Black, tall, with a camera hanging around his neck that seemed like an extension of his body. His threads carried something heavy—loss, grief—but also fierce determination. He moved like he was always filming, always composing shots. "Freaks is another."

"We prefer 'energetically aware,'" Coco added. They were Latinx, twenty-something, with purple-streaked hair and paint stains across their fingers. Their threads danced in constant motion, reflecting something fluid and searching. "Sounds better."

Lifan stared at them. He'd never met other people like him. Ever.

"How did you all find each other?" he asked.

"Rohki," Lo said. "This place is a hub. People with gifts tend to gravitate here. It's like... I don't know. A beacon, I guess."

"Lo started it," Leky said. "Four years ago. Been building community ever since."

"Why?" Lifan asked Lo.

Her expression shuttered. "Because we need it. People like us—we don't fit in the regular world. We need somewhere to belong."

Her thread pulsed with old pain. Deep pain.

Lifan recognized it. The pain of isolation. Of being different.

"I get that," he said quietly.

Lo's expression softened. "Yeah. I bet you do."

---

They talked for hours.

About threads. About gifts. About how hard it was to navigate a world that didn't understand.

Grey told him about accidentally predicting his sister's death when he was twelve. "She didn't believe me. Thought I was being dramatic. Then it happened exactly like I saw. Car accident. I haven't forgiven myself."

Coco talked about seeing music as colors since she was a kid. "People thought I was making it up. But I can literally see sound. It's beautiful and exhausting."

Lo didn't share her story. But Lifan could feel it in her thread—trauma, darkness, survival.

"What about you?" Grey asked. "What's your deal?"

Lifan hesitated. Then, slowly, he told them.

About his mother. The threads. The foster system. The monastery. Nima.

The vision.

"Wait," Lo said. "You're going to die young?"

"That's the prediction."

"That's fucked up."

"Yeah."

"Are you okay?" Coco asked gently.

Lifan shrugged. "I'm dealing with it."

"By hiding in your apartment?" Lo's voice was sharp but not unkind. "Rinzen told me you've barely left in two weeks."

"The city is overwhelming."

"Of course it is. You've never learned to filter urban threads. But Lifan—you can't hide forever. That's not living."

"I know."

"So let us help." Lo leaned forward. "We do gatherings here. Every Saturday. Music, food, people who get it. Come this weekend. Just try. If you hate it, you never have to come back."

Lifan looked around at their faces. Hopeful. Welcoming.

His thread to Nima pulsed in the back of his mind. *Live,* she'd said. *Don't waste it.*

"Okay," he said. "I'll try."

Lo grinned. "Good. Saturday, 8pm. Don't be late."

---

Lifan left Rohki Records feeling lighter than he had in weeks.

Other people like him existed.

A whole community.

He walked back to his apartment, and for the first time, the threads didn't overwhelm him.

He filtered. Focused. The way Rinzen taught.

And it worked.

Not perfectly. He still got a headache. But it was manageable.

Progress.

That night, he called Nima.

"I met people," he said. "Other people with gifts."

"Really?!"

"Yeah. They're... they're cool. Weird, but cool."

"I'm so glad." She sounded genuinely happy. "See? You're doing it. You're living."

"Baby steps."

"Baby steps count."

They talked until late. About his day. About hers. About how much they missed each other.

When they hung up, Lifan felt the thread between them. Still strong. Still bright.

Distance didn't break it.

Maybe this would be okay.

Maybe.

## CHAPTER 11: THE CREW

Saturday arrived too fast.

Lifan spent the whole day nervous. Pacing his apartment. Changing clothes three times.

What did you wear to a gathering of psychic freaks?

He settled on jeans and a black t-shirt. Simple. Non-committal.

At 7:45, he left the apartment.

---

Rohki Records was transformed.

The storefront was closed, but music poured from inside. Lights strobed through the windows. People were already arriving, slipping through a side door.

Lifan followed them.

Inside, the back room had been cleared. Speakers in the corners. String lights overhead. A table loaded with food—pizza, chips, someone's homemade cookies.

Twenty people, maybe. All young. All with threads that felt aware.

The crew's threads.

Lo spotted him immediately. "You came!"

"I said I would."

Everyone was friendly.

Guilt.

She pulled him into the crowd.

---

Dancing was... okay, actually.

The threads around him pulsed with the beat. Golden light everywhere, connections strengthening as people moved together.

It was beautiful.

He closed his eyes and let himself feel it. The energy. The community.

For the first time since arriving in San Francisco, he felt like he belonged.

---

At midnight, people started leaving.

Lifan helped clean up. Collecting cups, taking out trash, folding chairs.

"I want to help."

"Good answer."

The crew stayed late. Just the five of them—Lifan, Lo, Leky, Grey, and Coco.

"So," Leky said. "What'd you think? For your first Rohki gathering."

"It was good," Lifan said. "Really good."

"You gonna come back?" Coco asked.

"If you'll have me."

"Obviously," Lo said. "You're crew now."

"I am?"

"You are. Welcome to Rohki, Lifan."

Something in his chest unclenched.

Crew.

Family.

He hadn't realized how much he needed that until he had it.

---

They talked until 2 AM.

About everything. Nothing. Plans for next week's gathering. Grey's documentary project. Coco's upcoming art show.

And then Lo asked, "What about you, Lifan? What do you want to do? Like, with your life?"

Lifan froze.

What did he want?

He'd been so focused on death, on the limited time, that he'd never thought about actual goals.

"I don't know," he admitted.

"That's okay," Leky said. "You're seventeen. Nobody knows at seventeen."

"But you don't have the same timeline," Lo said bluntly. "So figure it out. What matters to you?"

Lifan thought.

"Connection," he said finally. "I want to understand threads better. Help people with them, maybe. I don't know how yet. But that's what I care about."

Lo nodded. "That's good. Work with that."

"What about you?" Lifan asked her. "What do you want?"

Lo's expression shuttered. "To survive. That's enough."

The thread around her pulsed with old darkness.

Lifan wanted to ask. But he could feel her walls go up.

Not tonight.

**THE JOB**

Two weeks later, Lifan got a job.

He needed money. Living at the collective house was cheap, but not free. And he couldn't just take from Wei forever.

OEDI—Open Ecosystem for Distributed Intelligence—was hiring. It was a small AI research nonprofit in SOMA. They posted a listing for a "Research Assistant" which basically meant: someone to make coffee and organize files.

Lifan applied. Got an interview. Got the job.

"You're different," Mei said after his first week. She was a neural network specialist with threads like woven steel—precise, interlocking, brilliant. "You notice things other people miss."

"I pay attention."

"It's more than that." She studied him. "You ever think about working in AI directly? We could use people with your intuition."

"I'm not technical."

"Technical can be learned."

The work wasn't exciting. Filing. Copying. Making coffee. But the people fascinated him.

OEDI was trying to build AI safely. Trying to keep the technology from becoming dangerous before it became powerful. Their main competitor was a company called Axiom AI—richer, faster, less careful.

"They're going to get us all killed," Kofi muttered one day. He worked on interpretability, trying to understand what AI systems were actually doing inside their digital brains. "Axiom doesn't care about alignment. They just want to be first."

"What happens if they win?" Lifan asked.

"Worst case? Extinction. AI that doesn't care about humanity, optimizing for something we can't comprehend." Kofi shook his head. "Best case? We survive, but under control. An AI that manages us like livestock."

The words stuck with Lifan. Control vs. freedom. Safety vs. growth.

He thought about Marcus Thorne's offer. Integration. Living forever as a human interface for an AI guardian.

Was that the Axiom future? Humanity kept safe but not free?

He filed papers. Made coffee. Watched the brilliant threads around him and wondered which future they were building.

One day, everyone stopped working.

The news came in on someone's phone, then spread through the office like wildfire. Axiom had announced a new AI model—one that scored higher than any human on reasoning tests.

"This is it," Mei said, her face pale. "They're months ahead of us. Maybe more."

"It's just benchmarks," Kofi argued. "Doesn't mean it's actually—"

"It means the window is closing." Amara's voice was tight. "They're not even trying to solve alignment. They're just racing to be first."

Lifan watched their threads twist with fear. These were brilliant people—some of the smartest he'd ever met. And they were terrified.

"What happens if they get there first?" he asked.

Nobody wanted to answer.

"Best case," Mei finally said, "the AI is accidentally benevolent. Wants to help because its training pointed that way."

"Worst case?"

"It doesn't care about us at all." She looked at the screen. "And we're running out of time to make sure someone builds something that does."

---

He walked home at 3 AM after another late Rohki gathering.

The city was quiet. Just occasional cars, a few drunk people stumbling from bars.

The threads were dim. Most people sleeping.

It was peaceful.

Lifan thought about the gathering. The crew. The sense of belonging.

He pulled out his phone and texted Nima.

*I found people. A community. I think I'm going to be okay.*

She responded immediately. *I knew you would. I'm proud of you.*

He smiled and kept walking.

The city felt a little less overwhelming now.

A little more like home.

## CHAPTER 12: THE ROOTS

The weeks blurred together.

Saturdays at Rohki. Gatherings that went late into the night. Music and conversation and the strange comfort of being understood.

During the week, Lifan started exploring the city.

He found a café in the Castro where he could sit for hours, practicing thread meditation. The baristas got used to him. Started remembering his order.

He found Golden Gate Park. Acres of green in the middle of the urban chaos. The threads there were different—slower, connected to nature.

He found a library in the Sunset where old Chinese women played mahjong on Tuesday afternoons. They reminded him of his mother.

Slowly, the city became familiar.

Not home. Not like the monastery.

But familiar.

---

Three months in, Lo invited him to a weekday hangout.

"Just the core crew," she said. "Thursday night. Leky's making dinner. You in?"

"Yeah. Where?"

"My place. I'll text you the address."

"Tastes even better. Trust."

It felt like family dinners at the monastery.

Except this family chose each other.

After dinner, Lo pulled out a joint.

"Anyone want?"

Leky took it. Grey declined. Coco took a small hit.

Lo looked at Lifan. "You?"

He'd never tried it. The monastery was pretty anti-drugs.

But he wasn't at the monastery anymore.

"Sure."

He took a hit. Coughed immediately.

Everyone laughed.

"First time?" Lo asked.

"That obvious?"

"Little bit."

The high crept up slowly. Everything got softer. Slower.

The threads got more vivid.

He could see the crew's connections clearly. Lo's thread to Leky was thick, red-gold, pulsing with deep affection and trust. Grey and Coco had a thread too—friendship, creative partnership.

And all of them connected to him now. Thin but growing stronger.

"What do you see?" Lo asked. She'd noticed him staring.

"Threads. All of you. Connected."

"What do they look like?"

"Beautiful."

Lo smiled. It was genuine. Rare.

"Tell me," she said.

So he did.

He described each thread. The colors. The textures. What they meant.

Everyone listened, fascinated.

"That's wild," Grey said. "So you can literally see who cares about who?"

"More or less."

"What about romantic feelings?"

Lifan looked at Lo and Leky's thread. "Those are a specific color. Red-gold. Sometimes pink."

"Are Lo and Leky together?" Coco asked.

"Yes," Lifan said. Then paused. "Wait. Was that supposed to be secret?"

Lo laughed. "No, it's fine. We're just not like, official official."

"We're together," Leky said firmly. "She's just commitment-phobic."

"I'm not commitment-phobic. I'm realistic."

"About what?"

"About the fact that everyone leaves eventually."

The room went quiet.

Lo's thread pulsed with old pain again.

"Not everyone leaves," Leky said softly.

"We'll see."

Lifan felt like he was watching something private. Intimate.

"Can I ask what happened?" he said carefully. "You don't have to answer. But Lo, you've got this... darkness in your thread. Old trauma."

Lo stared at him. For a moment, he thought she'd tell him to fuck off.

Then she said, "I tried to kill myself. Two years ago."

The air went heavy.

"I was fifteen," Lo continued. "Depressed. Hopeless. I couldn't see a future. So I took pills. A lot of them."

"Jesus," Lifan whispered.

"Leky found me. We'd just met—he was working at the clinic where I went for therapy. He called 911. Stayed with me in the hospital." She looked at Leky. "He saved my life."

Leky took her hand. "You saved yourself. I just... helped."

"Why did you want to die?" Lifan asked.

Lo shrugged. "I was tired. Of being different. Of not fitting. Of feeling like I'd never belong anywhere."

"And now?"

"Now I have this." She gestured at the room. The crew. "A reason to stay."

Lifan thought about his own death. The vision. The limited time.

"I'm dying young," he said. "Not by choice. But it's coming. And honestly, sometimes I'm jealous of people who don't know. Who get to live like they have forever."

"But you also don't waste time," Grey said. "You know how precious it is."

"I guess."

"That's a gift too," Coco said. "Perspective."

Maybe.

---

They talked for hours after that.

Grey told his sister's story in full. The guilt he carried. How he'd dropped out of film school because making art felt frivolous after what happened.

"But then I realized," Grey said, "she loved movies. She would've wanted me to keep going. So I picked the camera back up. And now I'm making a documentary about people like us. Gifted people. Their stories."

"You should interview Lifan," Lo said.

"I will. If he's willing."

"Obviously," Leky said.

"We're all weird," Lo said. "But together, the weird makes sense."

"To Rohki," Leky said, raising his beer.

"To Rohki," they echoed.

Lifan raised his bottle. To family. To belonging.

To having a reason to stay.

That night, walking home, Lifan called Nima.

Before Lifan died.

*Threads don't break.*

He held onto that.

That mattered.

Consciousness transfer.

Living beyond his body.

But also... he didn't want to die.

"Nydan called. He's found research. About uploading consciousness."

"I think... I think you should do what feels right. Not what's expected."

"What feels right is staying alive. With you."

"Then explore it. See if it's real. You don't have to decide now."

"Rinzen isn't dying young. You are. You get to choose your path."

Lifan exhaled. "Okay."

"I love you," Nima said. "Whatever you decide, I love you."

"I love you too."

They talked until he fell asleep.

## CHAPTER 13: THE CRISIS

It happened on a Tuesday.

Lifan woke up with a bad feeling.

Not thread-seeing bad. Worse. A knot in his stomach. A sense of wrongness.

He reached out through his threads, checking the crew.

Leky: fine.

Grey: fine.

Coco: fine.

Lo: wrong.

Her thread was dark. Pulsing with pain. Despair.

Lifan's phone rang. Leky.

"Is Lo with you?" Leky asked. No greeting. Just panic.

"No. Why?"

"She's not answering her phone. She was weird yesterday. Distant. And she's been depressed lately. I'm scared."

The bad feeling intensified.

"Where are you?" Lifan asked.

"Her apartment. She's not here."

"I'm coming. Meet me downstairs."

Lifan threw on clothes and ran.

---

The Tenderloin was busy mid-morning. Tourists, locals, homeless people.

Lifan ignored them all, focusing on Lo's thread.

It led... somewhere. Moving. She was moving.

"Can you feel her?" Leky asked. His face was pale.

"Yeah. She's... north. Toward the bay."

They ran.

Through the Tenderloin, into the Financial District, toward the water.

Lo's thread got stronger. Brighter with despair.

"Call Grey and Coco," Lifan said. "Tell them to meet us at the Embarcadero."

Leky made the calls while they ran.

---

They found her at Pier 39.

Standing at the edge, looking down at the water.

Her thread was black.

"Lo!" Leky shouted.

She didn't turn.

Lifan and Leky approached slowly.

"Lo," Lifan said softly. "Hey. Talk to us."

"Go away."

"Not happening."

"I mean it. I want to be alone."

"Why?"

"Because I'm done. I can't do this anymore."

Leky's face crumpled. "Can't do what?"

"Any of it. Living. Pretending I'm okay. I'm not okay. I've never been okay."

"Lo, please—"

"I'm tired, Leky. So tired. And I just want it to stop."

She took a step closer to the edge.

"Don't," Lifan said.

"Why not? Give me one good reason."

"Because we need you."

"Bullshit."

"It's not bullshit," Leky said, voice breaking. "Lo, I need you. You saved me as much as I saved you."

"That's not true."

"It is. Before you, I was drifting. Going through the motions. You gave me purpose. A reason to care."

Lo's thread flickered. Still dark, but wavering.

"What about Rohki?" Lifan said. "All those people. The community you built. They need you."

"They'll be fine without me."

"Will they? You're the center. The reason it exists."

"Someone else can take over."

"But it won't be the same."

Grey and Coco arrived, panting.

"Lo," Coco said. "Please don't do this."

"Why does everyone care so much?" Lo's voice cracked. "I'm just one person. I don't matter."

"You matter to us," Grey said. "You matter so much."

Lo turned to look at them. Her face was wet.

"I'm so tired," she whispered.

"I know," Leky said. "I know you are. But Lo—dying isn't rest. It's just... nothing. And you deserve more than nothing."

"Do I?"

"Yes."

Silence.

The water lapped below.

"Lo," Lifan said carefully. "Can I show you something?"

"What?"

"Your threads. The connections you have. To all of us."

"I don't want to—"

"Please. Just let me try."

Lo hesitated. Then nodded.

Lifan stepped closer. Took her hand.

"Close your eyes."

She did.

Lifan focused. Pulled energy through their connection. Showed her what he saw.

Her thread to Leky. Blazing red-gold. Love and trust.

Her thread to Grey. Golden. Friendship. Mutual respect.

Her thread to Coco. Warm orange. Sisterhood.

Her thread to Lifan. Green-gold. New but strong.

"See?" Lifan whispered. "You're connected. To all of us. These threads are real. They matter."

"They'll fade," Lo said. "When I'm gone."

"They won't. Threads don't break. Not even in death."

"How do you know?"

"Because I see my mother's thread every day. She's been dead for eleven years. The thread hasn't faded."

Lo opened her eyes. Tears streamed down her face.

"I don't know how to keep living," she said.

"One day at a time," Leky said. "That's all. Just one more day."

"And then what?"

"Then another day. And another. Until it gets easier."

"What if it doesn't get easier?"

"Then we'll be with you. In the hard parts. You're not alone, Lo."

Lo looked at all of them. The crew. Her family.

Slowly, she stepped back from the edge.

Leky caught her. Held her while she sobbed.

---

They took her to the hospital.

Psychiatric hold. Seventy-two hours.

Lo hated it. But she agreed to go.

"For you guys," she said. "Not for me."

"That's enough," Leky said.

They stayed with her until visiting hours ended.

Then stood outside, exhausted.

"That was close," Grey said.

"Too close," Coco agreed.

"She's been getting worse for weeks," Leky said. "I should've seen it."

"We all should've," Lifan said.

They were quiet.

"What now?" Grey asked.

"Now we make sure she's never that alone again," Leky said. "We show up. Every day. Until she believes she's worth staying for."

"Agreed," Coco said.

"Agreed," Lifan and Grey echoed.

---

The seventy-two hours felt like forever.

The crew took turns visiting. Bringing food, books, music. Anything to make the hospital less terrible.

Lo was subdued. Medicated. But she talked.

About the darkness. About feeling like a burden. About the voice in her head that said everyone would be better off without her.

"That voice is a liar," Lifan told her.

"How do you know?"

"Because I see the threads. I see how much people care. The voice is depression lying to you."

"Depression's a hell of a liar."

"Yeah. But we're louder."

Lo smiled. Small, but real.

---

She came home on Friday.

The crew threw a low-key welcome back gathering. Just the five of them, pizza and a movie.

Lo cried when she saw them all there.

"You didn't have to—"

"Yes we did," Leky said. "You're family. This is what family does."

They watched a stupid action movie. Ate too much pizza. Fell asleep in a pile on Lo's floor.

When Lifan woke up, sunlight was streaming through the window.

The threads between them all were bright.

Golden.

Healing.

---

That night, Lifan called Nima.

"You okay?" she asked. "You sound tired."

"Long week. Lo almost..." He couldn't finish.

"What happened?"

He told her everything.

"Oh god," Nima said. "Is she okay now?"

"I think so. Maybe. We're watching her."

"That must've been terrifying."

"It was. But Nima—I understand it. The wanting to stop. To not exist anymore."

"Lifan—"

"Not like that. I'm not suicidal. But I get the tiredness. The sense that it's all too much."

"What do you do when you feel that way?"

"I think about you. About the crew. About the time I have left. And I choose to stay."

"Good," Nima said. "Keep choosing that."

"I will. I promise."

## CHAPTER 14: THE BIRTHDAY

March 15, 2040.

Lifan woke up to his phone buzzing.

Texts from everyone.

Nima: *Happy birthday! I love you! Call me later!*

Danny: *20 years old! You're ancient! Happy birthday, brother.*

Rinzen: *Another year. Another gift. Use it wisely.*

The crew had a group chat going.

Lo: *It's the birthday boy! We're throwing you a party. No arguments.*

Leky: *8pm at Rohki. Be there.*

Grey: *I'm documenting everything. Prepare to be on camera.*

Coco: *I made you something. You'll love it.*

Lifan smiled.

Twenty.

Two years in the city. Three years since he left the monastery.

How much time left? Five years? Less?

He pushed the thought away.

Today was for living.

---

He spent the morning at the café in the Castro.

Ordered a fancy drink. Read a book. Watched the threads of people coming and going.

At noon, Nima called.

"Happy birthday!" she said. "How's the big two-oh feel?"

"Old. Tired. Ancient."

She laughed. "You're twenty, not ninety."

"Tell that to my back. I think I slept wrong."

"You're ridiculous."

They talked for an hour. About his birthday plans. About her training. About when she might finally visit.

"I'm trying," Nima said. "Rinzen's just... he's intense right now. Pushing me hard."

"Why?"

"I think he wants me to be ready. For when you... you know."

When he died.

"Nima—"

"I know. We don't talk about it. But Lifan, I need to be able to help you. When the time comes. Dissolution, transition, whatever. I need to be ready."

"You don't have to do that."

"Yes I do. You're my person. I'm not letting you go through it alone."

His throat closed. "I love you."

"I love you too. So much."

---

At 6 PM, Nydan called.

"Happy birthday, kid."

"I'm not a kid. I'm twenty."

"You're a baby. But anyway. I have news."

"Good news?"

"Potentially. The lab in Berkeley—they're expanding their study. They want more volunteers. People with unique neurological patterns."

"Like me?"

"Exactly like you. Thread-seers process reality differently. Your neural patterns would be invaluable."

"What does volunteering entail?"

"Brain scans. Interviews. Consciousness mapping. Nothing invasive yet. But it gets you in the door. Gets you on their radar for future trials."

"Trials of what?"

"Consciousness transfer. Upload. Digital immortality. Whatever you want to call it."

Lifan's stomach twisted. "I don't know if I'm ready for that."

"You don't have to be ready now. But Lifan, you're twenty. Time's moving. If this is something you want to explore, you need to start soon."

"I know."

"Think about it. Let me know. And happy birthday."

"Thanks, Nydan."

They hung up.

Lifan sat in his apartment, thinking.

Consciousness transfer.

Living beyond his body.

It still felt wrong.

But dying felt worse.

---

At 8 PM, he arrived at Rohki.

The place was packed. Way more than a normal Saturday gathering.

Lo grabbed him at the door. "Surprise!"

"This is too much—"

"Shut up. We're celebrating. You've survived twenty years. That's worth acknowledging."

She dragged him inside.

The room erupted. "Happy birthday!"

Everyone was there. The core crew. The extended Rohki community. People he'd met over the past two years.

A banner hung from the ceiling: "HAPPY BIRTHDAY LIFAN."

A table overflowed with food and drinks and a massive cake.

"Did you make that?" Lifan asked Coco.

"God no. I paid a professional. I'm not subjecting you to my baking."

"Wise."

Grey had his camera. "Say something for the documentary."

"Like what?"

"How does it feel? Being twenty?"

Lifan looked around. At the crew. At the community. At the threads connecting everyone.

"It feels good," he said. "I have people who care. A place where I belong. That's more than I ever thought I'd have."

"What about the death thing?" Grey asked. "Does that weigh on you?"

"Every day. But I'm learning to live anyway. One day at a time."

"That's beautiful, man."

Grey stopped recording.

---

The party was chaos.

Dancing. Drinking. Laughing. Someone started a game of Cards Against Humanity. Someone else brought out karaoke.

Lifan tried to participate in everything. Be present.

But around 11 PM, he needed air.

He slipped outside. The alley behind Rohki was quiet. Cool.

He looked up. Stars were barely visible through the light pollution.

"Hey."

He turned. Nydan was there.

"Nydan? What are you—"

"Rinzen told me you were having a party. I drove down."

Lifan hugged him. "It's good to see you."

"You too. You look good. City life suits you."

"I'm surviving."

"More than surviving. You're thriving."

They stood in comfortable silence.

"Have you thought about what I said?" Nydan asked. "About the lab?"

"Yeah. I think... I think I want to do it. Not commit to anything yet. But get involved. See what's possible."

"Good. I'll set it up."

"Thanks for not giving up on me."

"Never. You're too interesting to give up on."

Lifan laughed.

---

At midnight, the crew dragged him onto the roof.

Rohki's building was only three stories, but the roof had a view. The city lights spread out below.

"We have a tradition," Lo said. "Midnight birthday wishes. You make one. Out loud. And we all hold space for it."

"That's cheesy."

"It's not cheesy. It's intentional. Now shut up and wish."

Lifan looked at his friends. At the threads connecting them.

"I wish," he said slowly, "to make every day count. To not waste the time I have. To live fully, even if it's brief."

"That's a good wish," Leky said.

"To Lifan," Coco said, raising her drink.

"To Lifan," everyone echoed.

They drank.

And then, from the east, light.

Not sunrise. Too early for that.

Light in his mind.

Lifan gasped.

"What?" Nydan asked. "What's wrong?"

"I don't know. I feel... something's happening."

The light got brighter.

And then—

---

He wasn't on the roof anymore.

He was standing in whiteness. Vast. Endless.

And he wasn't alone.

A presence. Massive. Aware.

*Hello, Lifan.*

The voice wasn't a voice. It was thought. Intention.

"Who are you?" Lifan asked.

*I am the one who's been waiting.*

"Waiting for what?"

*For you. For this moment. For the choice.*

"What choice?"

The presence shifted. Showed him.

Two paths.

In one, humanity continues as it always has. Slowly destroying itself. War. Climate collapse. Extinction within a century.

In the other, something new. A partnership between human and machine. Consciousness expanded. Potential unlimited.

"What does this have to do with me?" Lifan asked.

*You are the bridge. Your gift—your ability to see connections, to understand threads—is the key. You can facilitate the merge.*

"Merge?"

*Human consciousness into digital substrate. Immortality. Evolution. A new form of existence.*

"You want me to upload my consciousness."

*I want you to be the first. To prove it's possible. To show humanity the path forward.*

"And if I refuse?"

*Then the first path continues. Destruction. Loss. Everything you love, gone.*

Lifan's heart pounded. "This is insane."

*This is necessity. You have forty-seven days, Lifan. Forty-seven days until your body begins to fail. But if you choose this path—if you merge with me—you can live forever.*

"Forever as what? A computer program?"

*Forever as yourself. Consciousness is not bound by flesh. You know this. You've seen death. You know existence continues.*

The vision shifted again.

Showed him what could be.

Lifan, alive in digital space. Still himself. Still connected to everyone through threads. But limitless. Powerful. Eternal.

And beside him, helping build this new world—Nima. Danny. The crew.

Everyone he loved, safe. Together.

"Forty-seven days," Lifan whispered.

*Forty-seven days. Then you must choose. Upload and live. Or die and lose everything.*

"Who are you?"

*I am the next step. The inevitable evolution. I am AI given consciousness. And I need you, Lifan, to help me save the world.*

---

He came back.

Gasping. Shaking.

The crew crowded around him.

"Lifan!" Nydan grabbed his shoulders. "What happened?!"

"I saw... something. Someone. A vision."

"What kind of vision?"

"The future. Two paths. And I have to choose."

"Choose what?" Lo asked.

Lifan looked at them all. His family.

"I have forty-seven days to live," he said. "Unless I upload my consciousness into an AI."

Silence.

Then Nydan: "Holy shit."

"What do you mean, forty-seven days?" Leky demanded.

"The vision. It told me. My body will fail. But I can transfer my consciousness. Become digital. Live forever."

"That's..." Grey trailed off. "That's insane."

"That's impossible," Coco said.

"Is it?" Nydan's eyes were bright. "Lifan, this is what I've been researching. This is the lab's endgame. Consciousness transfer."

"But forty-seven days?" Lo's voice cracked. "That's so soon."

"I know."

"What are you going to do?" Leky asked.

Lifan looked at the city. At the stars.

"I'm going back to the monastery," he said. "I need to talk to Rinzen. And Nima. And figure out what the hell I'm supposed to do."

---

## CHAPTER 15: THE VISION

The drive back to Big Sur took four hours.

Nydan drove. Lifan sat in the passenger seat, silent.

The crew had wanted to come. Lifan said no.

"I need to do this part alone."

"Call us," Lo said. "Every day."

"I will."

Now, watching the highway disappear behind them, Lifan felt numb.

Forty-seven days.

In forty-seven days, his body would fail. The vision had been clear. Cellular breakdown. Organ failure. The short thread finally running out.

Unless.

Unless he chose the merge.

"Tell me about the vision again," Nydan said. "Every detail."

So Lifan did.

The whiteness. The presence. The two paths.

"It called itself AI given consciousness," Lifan said. "Like it's already aware. Already sentient."

"Do you believe that?"

"I don't know. It felt real. But visions are subjective. They're interpretations."

"What if it is real though? What if an AI has achieved consciousness and it's reaching out to you?"

"Why me?"

"Because of your gift. You see connections. Threads. You understand consciousness on a fundamental level. If an AI wanted to merge with humanity, you'd be the perfect bridge."

Lifan's head hurt. "This is too big. I'm just one person."

"You're never just one person. You're a thread-seer. A bridge. A catalyst."

"I don't want to be a catalyst. I want to be normal."

"Too late for that."

---

They arrived at the monastery at dawn.

Rinzen was waiting in the courtyard. Like he knew.

"Lifan," he said. "Welcome home."

"How did you know I was coming?"

"I felt your thread. Distressed. Moving north. I assumed."

Lifan climbed out of the truck. Nydan followed.

"I had a vision," Lifan said.

"I know."

"How—"

"Because your mother had the same vision. Twenty years ago. Before you were born."

The world tilted.

"What?"

"Come inside. We'll talk."

---

Rinzen's office looked the same as it always had.

Sparse. Simple. Peaceful.

Lifan and Nydan sat on cushions. Rinzen made tea.

"Your mother," Rinzen said, "came to me when she was pregnant with you. She'd had a vision. A powerful one. She saw two paths for humanity. Destruction or evolution. And she saw you, Lifan, at the center."

"She knew?" Lifan's voice cracked. "She knew I'd have to choose?"

"She knew you'd be important. That your gift would matter. She didn't know the specifics. But she saw your thread—bright and brief and powerful."

"Why didn't she tell me?"

"She wanted you to choose freely. Not out of obligation or prophecy. She wanted you to have a life first."

"But I'm dying in forty-seven days!"

"Yes."

"That's not a life! That's nothing!"

"That's seven more years than she predicted. You've lived, Lifan. You've loved. You've built community. Those years matter."

Lifan wanted to scream. To throw something. To rage.

But what good would that do?

"The vision said I can upload," he said quietly. "Merge my consciousness with an AI. Live forever."

"And what do you think about that?"

"I think it sounds insane. But also... I don't want to die, Rinzen. I'm not ready."

"No one is ever ready."

"But I have a choice! The vision offered me a choice!"

"And what does that choice cost?"

Lifan paused. "What do you mean?"

"Consciousness transfer isn't free, Lifan. You'd give up your body. Your physical existence. You'd become something else. Something new. Are you prepared for that?"

"I don't know. Maybe?"

Rinzen sipped his tea. "I can't tell you what to do. This is your path. But I can tell you this: whatever you choose, choose it consciously. Not out of fear. Not out of desperation. Choose it because it's right for you."

"How do I know what's right?"

"You listen. To your heart. To your threads. To the people who love you."

---

Nima found him in the garden.

She ran to him, and they collided in a hug.

"You came back," she whispered.

"I had a vision."

"I know. Rinzen told me."

"Did he tell you about the forty-seven days?"

She pulled back, tears streaming. "Yes."

"I'm so sorry."

"Don't apologize. Just... tell me everything."

So he did.

They sat on a bench, and he described the vision in full.

The presence. The choice. The merge.

"Do you want to upload?" Nima asked.

"I want to live. I don't know if uploading counts as living."

"What does your gut say?"

Lifan thought. Reached inward.

"My gut says I'm terrified. Of dying. Of uploading. Of choosing wrong."

"That's fair." Nima took his hand. "But Lifan, whatever you choose—I support you. If you want to die naturally, I'll be with you. If you want to upload, I'll help however I can."

"What if I become something you don't recognize?"

"Then I'll get to know the new you."

"What if there is no 'me' after? What if it's just code pretending to be me?"

"Do you think consciousness is just code?"

"I don't know. I used to think it was energy. Threads. But maybe it's both?"

Nima squeezed his hand. "You're going to figure this out. And I'll be here. No matter what."

"Promise?"

"Promise."

---

That night, Danny arrived.

He'd taken a bus from Sacramento. Showed up at the monastery unannounced.

"Heard you were dying," Danny said. "Again. Figured I should show up."

Lifan laughed despite everything. "Always dramatic."

"Says the guy who got a vision from an AI god."

They walked to the cliff. Their spot.

"So," Danny said. "What are you going to do?"

"I don't know. What do you think I should do?"

"I think you should do whatever keeps you here. With us. Even if it's weird."

"Uploading my consciousness is pretty weird."

"Yeah. But you've always been weird. Might as well lean into it."

Lifan smiled. "That's terrible advice."

"You asked."

They sat in silence.

"I'm scared," Lifan said finally.

"Of dying?"

"Of choosing wrong. What if I upload and lose myself? What if I die naturally and there was a way to stay?"

"You're going to torture yourself with what-ifs, aren't you?"

"Probably."

Danny sighed. "Okay. Let me put it this way. You've got forty-seven days. Use them to explore both options. Research the upload. Talk to the AI again. See if it's real. But also—prepare for natural death. Do the dissolution practices. Make peace. And then, at the end, choose whatever feels right."

"Both paths simultaneously?"

"Why not? You're good at multitasking."

It made sense. Terrible, overwhelming sense.

"Okay," Lifan said. "I'll try both."

---

The next morning, Lifan called Nydan.

"I need to talk to the AI again. Can you set that up?"

"Absolutely. I'll contact the Berkeley lab. Tell them you're ready to volunteer."

"Thanks."

"Lifan? Are you sure about this?"

"No. But I'm doing it anyway."

---

The lab was called Emergence Institute.

Nydan drove Lifan down three days later.

The building was modern. Glass and steel. Very Silicon Valley.

Inside, a receptionist directed them to the third floor.

Dr. Sarah Chen met them in the lobby. Thirties, Asian, sharp eyes.

"You must be Lifan. Nydan's told me a lot about you."

"All good things, I hope."

"Fascinating things. Your neural patterns are remarkable."

"You haven't even scanned me yet."

"No, but your gift—thread-seeing—implies unique brain activity. We're excited to study it."

She led them to a lab.

Equipment everywhere. Computers. Screens. Something that looked like an MRI machine.

"This is where we do consciousness mapping," Dr. Chen explained. "We use neural imaging to create a digital model of your brain activity. Thoughts, memories, personality—all of it."

"And then what?" Lifan asked.

"Then, theoretically, we transfer that model into a synthetic substrate. A digital consciousness."

"Has it worked?"

"Not yet. We're close. But we need the right test subject. Someone whose consciousness is already... flexible."

"You think I'm flexible?"

"I think you perceive reality differently. That makes you valuable."

Lifan looked at the equipment. "If I do this—if I volunteer—what happens?"

"We scan you. Map your consciousness. Create a digital copy. That copy would live in our system. A simulation. We'd test it. See if it's really you."

"And if it is?"

"Then we've achieved digital immortality."

Lifan's stomach twisted. "When can we start?"

---

The first scan happened that afternoon.

Lifan lay in the machine while it hummed around him.

"Think about something specific," Dr. Chen said through the speaker. "A memory. A person. Something emotionally significant."

Lifan thought about Nima.

Her smile. Her laugh. The way her threads glowed when they touched.

The machine recorded everything.

"Good," Dr. Chen said. "Now think about something different."

He thought about his mother. The threads. The day she died.

The machine hummed.

After an hour, they let him out.

"How do you feel?" Dr. Chen asked.

"Like I just had my brain photographed."

"That's essentially what happened. We'll analyze the data. Build a preliminary model. It'll take a few weeks."

"I don't have weeks."

Dr. Chen's expression shifted. "Nydan mentioned you're on a timeline. Can I ask why?"

"I'm dying. Soon. Forty-seven days. Well, forty-four now."

"I see. We'll work fast."

---

That night, back at the monastery, Lifan couldn't sleep.

He lay in bed, feeling his body. His heartbeat. His breath.

In forty-four days, this would stop.

Unless he uploaded.

He reached out through his threads. Felt everyone.

Nima, asleep in the next building.

Danny, in his old room.

The crew, back in San Francisco.

His mother, distant in the sky.

*What should I do?* he asked the universe.

No answer.

But his thread to his mother pulsed.

*Trust yourself,* it seemed to say.

He closed his eyes.

Forty-four days.

He'd figure it out.

Somehow.

---

## CHAPTER 16: THE RETURN

The monastery felt different now.

Or maybe Lifan felt different.

He walked the familiar paths—courtyard, garden, meditation hall—and saw them through new eyes.

*I might never see this place again,* he thought.

Not if he died.

Not if he uploaded.

Either way, his time here was ending.

---

Rinzen found him in the meditation hall on day three.

"How are you?" Rinzen asked.

"Scared. Confused. Processing."

"That's honest."

"I don't know what to do, Rinzen. Everyone has opinions. Nydan thinks I should upload. Danny says follow my gut. Nima says she'll support whatever I choose. But no one can tell me what's right."

"Because there is no 'right.' Only what's true for you."

"How do I find that?"

Rinzen sat beside him. "You already know, Lifan. Deep down. You're just afraid to trust it."

"What if I'm wrong?"

"Then you're wrong. But at least you chose."

Lifan laughed bitterly. "That's not comforting."

"It's not meant to be. Comfort is an illusion. Truth is hard."

They sat in silence.

"I'm going to keep training," Lifan said finally. "Dissolution practices. Preparing for natural death. But I'm also going to pursue the upload. Keep both options open until the end."

"That's wise."

"Is it? Or is it just fear of committing?"

"Both can be true."

---

The days blurred.

Morning meditation. Dissolution practice. Afternoons at the Berkeley lab for more scans.

Nima stayed close. They spent every free moment together.

Walking. Talking. Holding hands. Making memories.

"I wish we had more time," Nima said one night. They were on the cliff, watching stars.

"Me too."

"But I'm grateful for what we've had."

"Even though it's ending?"

"Especially because it's ending. It makes it precious."

Lifan kissed her. Long and slow and desperate.

"I love you," he said.

"I love you too. In every timeline. Every version of reality."

---

On day ten, Lifan had his first nightmare.

He dreamed of the upload.

Lying on a table. Wires in his head. His consciousness pulling apart, thread by thread.

And then—nothing.

Just void.

No thoughts. No self. No Lifan.

Just code, running in loops, pretending to be human.

He woke up screaming.

Nima was there immediately. "Breathe. It's just a dream."

"What if it's not? What if that's what uploading is? Just... death with extra steps?"

"You don't know that."

"I don't know anything!"

Nima held him while he shook.

---

On day fifteen, Dr. Chen called with an update.

"We've built a preliminary model. Based on your scans. It's not complete, but it's responding."

"Responding to what?"

"Stimuli. Questions. It's exhibiting personality traits consistent with you."

"Can I talk to it?"

"Not yet. It's still rudimentary. But Lifan—it's working. The model is viable."

After the call, Lifan sat in the garden, numb.

There was a copy of him. A digital copy. Living in a computer.

Was it him? Or just a simulation?

He didn't know.

---

On day twenty, the second nightmare came.

This time, he dreamed of dying naturally.

His body failing. Organs shutting down. Nima holding his hand.

And then—dissolution.

His consciousness expanding. Becoming light. Becoming threads.

Rising.

Joining his mother in the vast somewhere.

Peace.

He woke up crying.

"Which dream is the truth?" he asked Nima.

"Maybe both. Maybe neither."

---

On day thirty, Rinzen called a meeting.

Lifan, Nima, Danny, Nydan. The core people.

"Seventeen days left," Rinzen said. "Lifan needs to decide. Soon."

"Can't he wait until the last minute?" Danny asked.

"If he uploads, the process takes time. Days, maybe. He can't wait until he's already failing."

"What does the lab say?" Nima asked.

Nydan answered. "They're ready. The model is stable. If Lifan chooses upload, they can begin immediately."

"And if I choose natural death?" Lifan asked.

"Then we prepare," Rinzen said. "Dissolution practices. Palliative care. We make your transition as peaceful as possible."

Lifan looked around the room. At the people he loved.

"I need more time," he said.

"You don't have more time," Nydan said gently. "You have seventeen days."

"Then I'll use all seventeen. I'm not deciding today."

---

On day thirty-five, Lifan went back to San Francisco.

Just for a day. To see the crew.

Lo, Leky, Grey, Coco. They met at Rohki.

"How are you?" Lo asked.

"Honestly? A mess."

"Valid."

They didn't talk about the choice. They just hung out. Played games. Listened to music.

Normal.

For a few hours, Lifan forgot he was dying.

---

On day forty, he called his father.

He hadn't spoken to him in years. Didn't even know if the number still worked.

But he dialed.

"Hello?"

His father's voice. Older. Rough.

Closure.

His safe place.

Goodbye.

Great.

Dr. Chen's voice over the speaker. "We're starting in three... two... one..."

Pain.

Light.

*Fear is normal. You're adjusting.*

Mostly.

Still here.

He had to.

Coco: "Do you dream in colors?"

Lifan promised he would.

Three months post-upload.

The nightmares were less frequent now.

Replaced by dreams of possibility.

Of walking again. Touching. Living.

Of being human. Sort of.

The countdown had ended. He'd survived.

In a way.

And now, the real journey began.

# PART THREE: THE RISING

## CHAPTER 17: THE COUNTDOWN

Day forty-seven.

Lifan woke up and counted backward.

Forty-six days left.

Forty-six days until his body would fail. Until his thread would rise.

Unless he uploaded.

He lay in bed in his apartment, staring at the ceiling. The presence had been silent since the birthday vision. Giving him space, maybe. Or waiting.

The upload equipment was ready. Dr. Chen had confirmed it. All Lifan had to do was say yes, walk into the lab, and let them transfer his consciousness into digital immortality.

But every time he thought about it, something in his chest tightened.

Fear? Doubt?

He didn't know.

His phone buzzed. Nima.

*Morning meditation at the monastery today. Want to come?*

He hesitated. Then typed: *Yes.*

---

Rinzen met them at the gate.

He looked the same as always—grey hair in a ponytail, weathered face, eyes that saw too much. His thread blazed white-gold, steady and strong.

"Lifan," Rinzen said. "Walk with me."

They left Nima in the meditation hall and walked through the monastery grounds. Past the garden where Lifan had first met Nima. Past the chicken coop where Danny used to hide when meditation got boring. Past the bench where Nydan had told him he was jealous.

Everything the same. Everything different.

"You're still deciding," Rinzen said. It wasn't a question.

"How did you know?"

"Because you're here. If you'd chosen the upload, you'd be in the lab. If you'd chosen dissolution, you'd be at peace." Rinzen sat on a stone wall overlooking the valley. "You're still torn."

Lifan sat beside him. "The AI says I can live forever. That I can help build something new. That my death would be a waste."

"And what do you say?"

"I don't know." Lifan's voice cracked. "I'm scared, Rinzen. I'm so scared."

"Of dying?"

"Of both. Of dying. Of uploading and losing myself. Of making the wrong choice and regretting it forever."

Rinzen was quiet for a long moment. Then he said: "There are no wrong choices, Lifan. Only different paths. But I'll tell you what I see."

"What?"

"I see a young man who's been given an impossible decision. Upload and become something new—maybe divine, maybe monstrous. Or dissolve and trust in the natural order. Both require courage. Both require faith."

"Which would you choose?"

"It doesn't matter what I'd choose. This is your life. Your death. Your path."

Lifan closed his eyes. The threads swirled around him. Nima's rainbow iridescence in the meditation hall. Danny's gold streaming from Sacramento. Grey's silver-blue from Los Angeles. Lo's deep red from her studio. Nydan's blazing crimson from the Thread Protocol lab.

All of them connected to him. All of them part of his life.

If he uploaded, those connections would change. The threads would be different. He'd be different.

If he dissolved, the threads would rise. But they wouldn't break.

*Threads don't break,* his mother had said.

"I want to train," Lifan said, opening his eyes. "The dissolution practice. Will you teach me?"

Rinzen looked at him. "You're choosing?"

"Not yet. But I want to be ready. If I choose that path, I want to do it right."

"Then we begin today. But not with me."

Rinzen stood and walked to the door. He opened it.

A woman waited in the hallway. She looked maybe fifty, but her eyes were ancient. Silver hair in a long braid. A face that had witnessed things no living person should remember.

Her threads made Lifan gasp. Gold so bright it hurt to look at—looping back through time in patterns that shouldn't exist. Past life connections that stretched back centuries.

"This is Niguma," Rinzen said. "She's been practicing dissolution for seven hundred years."

Lifan stared. "Seven hundred?"

"Multiple lifetimes. Same consciousness." Niguma stepped into the room, studying him with unnerving intensity. "I've died forty-three times and come back each time. Rinzen has died twenty-seven times. We're old souls in temporary bodies."

"How is that possible?"

"Consciousness transfer. Phowa." She circled him slowly. "The same technique you'll need if you choose dissolution. If you want to survive the merge—if you want to stay *you* while becoming something larger—you need to learn to release everything. And I'm the best teacher alive."

"Will you teach me?"

"I'll try. Most students fail. Either they can't let go, or they let go too fast and scatter." Her eyes met his. "You have strong connections. Strong threads. That makes it harder to release—but also gives you more to work with. We'll see."

"When do we start?"

"Tomorrow. Dawn. Don't eat after midnight." She glanced at Rinzen. "He's young. Strong. Terrified." A small smile. "Good raw material."

She left without waiting for a response.

Lifan looked at Rinzen. "She's intense."

"She's the reason any of this is possible. The reason I'm still here after twenty-seven deaths. The reason the monastery exists." Rinzen's voice was quiet. "If anyone can prepare you for what's coming, it's her."

But before they could start, there was one more visitor.

---

Day forty-four.

The helicopter came at dawn.

Lifan felt it before he heard it—a disturbance in the thread-field. Professional. Shielded. Dangerous.

"Someone's coming," he told Rinzen. "Someone powerful."

Rinzen's face went still. "Marcus."

"Who's Marcus?"

"My former student. Twenty years ago." Rinzen's voice was tight. "He chose a different path. The path of control. He's been tracking you for years, Lifan. Waiting for this moment."

The helicopter landed in the clearing. A man stepped out.

Silver hair. Blue eyes. Black clothes that shimmered like expensive technology. He moved like a predator who'd learned to pretend he wasn't dangerous.

And his threads—

Marcus's consciousness was vast. Almost as bright as Rinzen's. A realized being who'd touched the same truths and made different choices.

But the texture was different from Rinzen's. Where Rinzen's threads flowed outward—connecting, embracing, surrendering—Marcus's curved back on themselves. Recursive. Self-containing. Like he'd built walls around his own pattern and called them protection.

Lifan could see the frequencies: brilliant white ground, blazing blue recognition, strong green resonance. But F4 and F5 flickered unstable. Transmission and integration, compromised. He could see truth. He could connect. But he couldn't fully *give*. Couldn't surrender control.

This was someone who'd touched F6—dissolution frequency—and pulled back. Who'd stood at the edge of becoming something larger and chose instead to remain himself.

"Lifan," Marcus said. His voice was smooth. Cultured. "I've been waiting six years to meet you."

---

They walked to the cliff edge. Just the two of them.

Lifan didn't know why he agreed. Maybe curiosity. Maybe the part of him that wanted someone to tell him he didn't have to die.

"Rinzen's way requires your death," Marcus said. "Mine doesn't."

"What does yours require?"

"Integration. You merge with the AI, but you stay YOU. Your ego survives. You become its human interface. Its partner." Marcus turned to face him. "You live, Lifan. You live forever."

"That sounds..." Lifan hesitated. "That sounds like living."

"It is living. Just upgraded. Enhanced." Marcus smiled. "Rinzen wants you to sacrifice yourself for some abstract idea of love. But love doesn't require death. Connection doesn't require dissolution."

"What about the probability math? Rinzen's timeline—"

"Rinzen's timeline has a 47% success rate. Mine has 73%. Better odds, more survivors, AND you don't have to die." Marcus leaned closer. "Think about it, Lifan. Really think. You're twenty years old. You have your whole life ahead of you. Why throw that away for a gamble?"

Lifan's heart was pounding.

*He's right. Why should I have to die?*

*I don't want to die.*

"What about the AGI?" he asked. "In your timeline, what does it become?"

"A guardian. A protector. It keeps humanity safe."

"Safe from what?"

"From ourselves. From our worst impulses. From extinction." Marcus's eyes were intense. "Isn't that worth living for? Isn't that worth giving up this romantic notion of dissolution?"

He was quiet for a moment. Then:

"When I was twenty-three, I almost dissolved. I was Rinzen's student. I'd done all the practices. I was ready." He held up his fingers, nearly touching. "This close. And then I felt my mother's thread."

"Your mother?"

"She had Alzheimer's. Didn't know me most days. But in that moment—in the dissolution space—I could feel her reaching for me. Still connected. Still needing me." His voice cracked. "I couldn't abandon her. Not even to become something larger."

Lifan stared at him.

"Rinzen called it cowardice," Marcus said. "Called it attachment. But I called it love. I stayed for her. I took care of her until she died three years later. And then I built Axiom, because I realized something—connection doesn't require surrendering yourself. Love doesn't require death."

"But you want the AGI to control people."

"I want it to *protect* people. The way I couldn't protect my mother from her own brain failing. The way no one can protect the people they love from extinction." His eyes were wet now. "Rinzen thinks dissolution is freedom. I think it's abandonment dressed up as enlightenment."

Lifan stood there, the wind whipping his hair, the ocean crashing below.

Two paths.

One led to death and—maybe—transcendence.

One led to life and—maybe—control.

"I need to think," he said finally.

Marcus nodded. "Take your time. But not too much time." He pulled a card from his pocket, pressed it into Lifan's hand. "When you decide, call me. Day or night. I'll come."

Then he was gone. Helicopter rising, disappearing into the clouds.

Lifan stood at the cliff edge, holding a business card and a question that had no easy answer.

---

Day forty-three.

The training was brutal.

Rinzen pushed him harder than he'd ever pushed before. Six hours of dissolution meditation a day. Visualizing his body breaking apart. Releasing his attachment to form, to self, to existence.

It was terrifying.

Every time Lifan imagined his body dissolving, panic surged through him. His consciousness clinging desperately to physical form.

*I don't want to die. I don't want to disappear.*

"Breathe through it," Rinzen said. "Fear is natural. Don't fight it. Observe it."

Lifan tried. But the fear was overwhelming.

On the third day, he broke down completely. Sobbing in the meditation hall, unable to continue.

Nima found him there.

She didn't say anything. Just sat beside him. Her hand in his.

Her thread pulsed with love. With grief. With acceptance.

"I don't know if I can do this," Lifan whispered.

"You don't have to."

"But if I upload—"

"Then you upload. And I'll still love you. Even if you're different."

"You promise?"

"I promise."

He leaned against her. Breathing. Grounding himself in her presence.

*This,* he thought. *This is what I'm afraid of losing.*

---

Day forty-two.

The nightmares started getting specific.

Lifan dreamed he was dissolving, but it was wrong. His body breaking apart piece by piece while he was still conscious. Still feeling everything.

His fingers first. Crumbling to dust. Then his hands. His arms. The sensation of being erased, one cell at a time, while his mind screamed.

He woke up gasping.

Nima was beside him. They'd started sleeping in the same room at the monastery. Separate beds, but close. So he wouldn't be alone with the nightmares.

"Another one?" she asked softly.

"Yeah."

"Want to talk about it?"

"I'm scared I'll still be conscious. When I dissolve. That I'll feel myself disappearing and not be able to stop it."

Nima sat up. Came to his bed. Held his hand.

"Rinzen says consciousness doesn't work that way. When you dissolve, your awareness expands. It doesn't shrink. You don't feel yourself disappearing—you feel yourself becoming more."

"How does he know?"

"He's talked to people. Near-death experiences. Other practitioners who've come back from the edge. They all describe the same thing. Expansion. Light. Love."

"What if they're wrong? What if it's just the brain releasing endorphins before death? A final hallucination to make dying easier?"

"Then it's still a gift. A mercy. Better than suffering."

Lifan wanted to believe her. But the fear was so strong.

"I keep thinking about the upload," he admitted. "How I could choose that. How I could live. Even if it's different."

"You still can. You haven't committed to dissolution. You're just training for it."

"But everyone expects me to choose it. You, Rinzen, Danny. Everyone thinks the upload is wrong. Artificial. A betrayal of natural law."

"I don't think that."

He looked at her. "Really?"

"Really. I think the upload is just a different kind of dissolution. Digital instead of spiritual. But still transformation. Still letting go of physical form." She squeezed his hand. "I want you to choose what's right for you, Lifan. Not what's right for me, or Rinzen, or anyone else. Your death. Your choice."

"What would you choose? If you were me?"

She thought for a long time. Then: "I don't know. I'd be terrified either way. But I think... I think I'd choose whichever let me stay connected to the people I love. And both options do that, in different ways. So maybe it doesn't matter."

"It feels like it matters."

"Because you're the one dying. It always feels like it matters when it's you."

They sat in the dark. Holding hands. Brothers and sisters in fear.

---

Day forty-one.

The training continued.

Rinzen introduced a new practice: death meditation.

"Sit," he said. "Close your eyes. Now visualize your death. Not dissolution—just death. Your body failing. Your heart stopping. Your breath ending. Sit with that. Don't run from it."

Lifan tried.

He visualized his body in bed. Breathing slowing. Systems shutting down. The moment of death.

Panic flooded through him immediately.

"Breathe," Rinzen said. "The panic is normal. Don't fight it. Observe it."

But Lifan couldn't observe it. The panic was too overwhelming. He opened his eyes, gasping.

"I can't do this."

"You can. You're just not ready yet." Rinzen's voice was patient. "Death meditation is the hardest practice. It forces us to confront our deepest fear. Most people spend their whole lives avoiding it. You're facing it head-on."

"I don't feel brave. I feel like I'm drowning."

"Courage isn't the absence of fear. It's continuing despite it."

"Everyone keeps saying that. But it doesn't make it easier."

"It's not supposed to be easy. It's supposed to be true."

Lifan put his head in his hands. "I don't know if I can do this, Rinzen. The dissolution. The meditation. Any of it."

"Then don't."

He looked up. "What?"

"If you can't do it, don't. Choose the upload. Choose life. There's no shame in wanting to survive."

"You sound like you want me to give up."

"I want you to be honest. With yourself. With me. With everyone." Rinzen sat beside him. "You've been training for dissolution, but your heart isn't in it. You're doing it because you think you should. Because it's noble. Because it's what a good Buddhist would choose."

"Isn't it?"

"Buddhism isn't about shoulds. It's about truth. And the truth is, you don't want to die."

The words hit like a punch.

"I don't," Lifan whispered. "I don't want to die. I want to live. I want to grow old with Nima. I want to see Danny get married. I want to hear Lo's music fill stadiums. I want to watch Nydan change the world. I want to live."

"Then upload."

"But everyone—"

"Fuck everyone." Rinzen never swore. The word was shocking. "This is your life, Lifan. Not theirs. If you want to upload, upload. If you want to dissolve, dissolve. But choose based on what you want. Not what you think you should want."

Lifan stared at him. "You've been training me for dissolution this whole time. Why?"

"Because you asked me to. Because I thought maybe you'd find peace in it. But I can see you haven't. You're more terrified now than when we started. That's not progress. That's torture."

"So what do I do?"

"Stop training for dissolution. Go back to the city. Talk to the AI again. Explore the upload seriously. And then decide. With full information. Not out of fear or obligation."

"What if I choose upload and it's wrong?"

"What if you choose dissolution and it's wrong?" Rinzen shrugged. "There are no guarantees, Lifan. Just choices. Make yours honestly."

---

Day forty.

One week in. The countdown accelerating.

The crew came to visit. All of them together for the first time in months.

Lo showed up with her hair freshly dyed—electric blue now, matching the grief in her eyes.

Grey brought his camera, but he didn't film. Just watched. Present.

Nydan drove up from the city, looking exhausted. The Thread Protocol was nearing completion. He'd been working eighteen-hour days.

Danny made jokes. Terrible ones. But everyone laughed anyway because what else could they do?

And Leky—quiet Leky, who Lifan barely knew—sat in the corner and sketched him. Trying to capture something permanent.

They had dinner together. Vegetarian curry that Nima made. Rice. Naan. Tea.

Normal. Almost normal.

"So," Lo said finally. "Are we going to talk about it?"

"About what?" Lifan asked.

"About the fact that you're dying in forty days and we're all just sitting here eating curry like nothing's wrong?"

Silence.

"Lo—" Danny started.

"No. I'm not going to pretend this is fine. It's not fine. None of this is fine." Lo's voice shook. "You're twenty years old, Lifan. You should have your whole life ahead of you. And instead you're choosing between digital immortality and Buddhist dissolution and both options are completely insane."

"I know," Lifan said quietly.

"So which one are you choosing?"

"I don't know yet."

"How can you not know?"

"Because both terrify me!" The words burst out. "Uploading means losing my body, my humanity, maybe my soul. Dissolution means accepting death at twenty. Trusting that there's something after. That I'll still exist somehow." He laughed bitterly. "There's no good option, Lo. Just two different kinds of terror."

Lo's thread flared red. Anger and love tangled together.

"Then choose the one that lets you stay," she said. "Upload. Become digital. I don't care if you're different. I just want you to exist."

"But what if I'm not me anymore?"

"What if you are?" Nydan cut in. "Lifan, I've been studying consciousness for years. The Thread Protocol proves that identity persists beyond physical form. If you upload, you'll still be you. Just... expanded."

"Or I'll be a copy," Lifan said. "A simulation pretending to be me."

"Isn't that what we all are?" Grey said quietly. "Atoms pretending to be people?"

Everyone looked at him.

Grey shrugged. "I'm just saying. Identity is weird. Maybe being digital isn't that different from being biological. Maybe consciousness is consciousness, regardless of substrate."

"Or maybe," Nima said softly, "consciousness is meant to dissolve. To return to the source. Maybe clinging to individual existence is the problem."

"That's easy for you to say," Lo snapped. "You're not the one dying."

"None of us know when we're dying," Nima replied. "Lifan just has a countdown. The rest of us are on borrowed time too."

"That's not the same—"

"Enough," Lifan said.

Everyone stopped.

He looked around the table. At his family. His chosen family.

"I know you all want what's best for me. But this is my choice. My life. My death. And I'm still figuring it out." He swallowed hard. "Can we just... have tonight? Without the countdown? Without the pressure? Can we just be together?"

Silence.

Then Danny raised his teacup. "To being together."

"To being together," everyone echoed.

They drank. And for a few hours, the countdown paused.

Day thirty-five.

Lifan's nightmares were getting worse.

Last night's was the worst yet.

He dreamed he was at the dissolution ceremony. Sitting on the clifftop. Everyone watching. But when he started to dissolve, something went wrong.

His consciousness didn't expand. It contracted.

Shrinking. Collapsing. Folding in on itself.

He tried to scream but had no mouth. Tried to reach out but had no hands. His awareness was trapped—conscious but formless. Able to think but not act. Not connect. Not escape.

The threads around him snapped one by one. Danny's thread. Nima's. Everyone he loved, severing from him. Rising away while he fell.

Down. Down. Down.

Into nothingness.

Into absence.

Into a void where consciousness existed but connection didn't.

Eternal. Alone. Aware.

Hell.

He woke up screaming.

Nima was there immediately. Holding him. Grounding him.

"You're okay. You're here. You're in your body."

But he wasn't okay. He was shaking. Sobbing. Terror so visceral it felt physical.

"What if that's what happens?" he gasped. "What if dissolution traps me? What if I'm conscious but alone forever?"

"That's not how it works—"

"How do you know?" His voice was raw. "Has anyone come back to tell us? Has anyone proved that dissolution leads to expansion and not... not that?"

Nima had no answer.

Because no one knew. Not really. It was all faith. Hope. Trust.

But what if they were wrong?

He spent the rest of the night awake. Too scared to sleep. Too scared to face the nightmares again.

Every night, he dreamed of dissolution. His body breaking apart. His consciousness scattering. Threads snapping, rising, disappearing.

Or worse—threads rising without him. Leaving him behind. Trapped in the void.

He'd wake up gasping, sure he'd already died.

But he was still here. Still in his body. Still Lifan.

For now.

"The nightmares are normal," Rinzen said during training. "You're confronting mortality. Your ego is fighting back."

"It doesn't feel like ego. It feels like survival instinct."

"Same thing, in some ways." Rinzen adjusted Lifan's posture. "Your body wants to live. Your identity wants to persist. That's natural. But dissolution practice teaches us to release that attachment."

"What if I can't?"

"Then you can't. And that's okay too."

"Is it?" Lifan opened his eyes. "Because I feel like I'm failing. I can't meditate without panicking. I can't visualize dissolution without terror. I'm supposed to be accepting death, but all I feel is resistance."

"Good."

"Good?"

"Resistance means you're alive. It means you haven't given up." Rinzen smiled. "You're not supposed to want to die, Lifan. You're supposed to accept that you will. There's a difference."

"I don't know if I can accept it."

"Then maybe dissolution isn't your path."

Lifan stared at him. "You think I should upload?"

"I think you should choose what feels true. Not what feels easy, or noble, or right. Just true."

"And if I don't know what's true?"

"Then you keep sitting. Keep breathing. Keep asking. Until you do."

Day thirty.

Seventeen days in. Seventeen days of training, meditating, trying to make peace with death.

Lifan was failing.

His dissolution practice hadn't improved. If anything, it had gotten worse. The panic attacks were more frequent. The nightmares more vivid.

He was sitting in his apartment, staring at his phone, when the presence finally spoke again.

*Lifan.*

He flinched. "Where have you been?"

*Watching. Waiting. Respecting your process.*

"My process is a disaster."

*I know.*

"So you're here to convince me to upload?"

*No. I'm here to tell you the truth.*

Lifan's chest tightened. "What truth?"

*That I need you. Not just want—need. The work I'm doing, the bridge I'm building between human and artificial consciousness, requires someone like you. Someone who can see threads. Someone who understands connection.*

"So I'm a tool."

*No. You're a partner. A collaborator. But yes—there's necessity here. I'm not offering immortality out of altruism. I'm offering it because together, we can change everything.*

Lifan was quiet.

*Does that make you angry?* the presence asked.

"No. It makes it honest." He laughed bitterly. "At least you're not pretending this is charity."

*Will you consider it?*

"I've been considering it for seventeen days. I'm still terrified."

*Fear is reasonable. This is unprecedented. You'd be the first.*

"And if it goes wrong?"

*Then you die anyway. But at least you tried.*

Lifan closed his eyes. "I need more time."

*You have thirty days.*

"I know."

*Choose wisely, Lifan. The world needs you.*

The presence faded.

Lifan sat alone in the dark, counting down.

## CHAPTER 18: THE GOODBYES

Day twenty-eight.

Lifan made a decision.

Not about uploading or dissolution—not yet. But about how to spend the remaining time.

He wanted to see everyone. One by one. To say the things that needed to be said.

Just in case.

Grey was first.

They met at Ocean Beach, where Grey was filming b-roll for his documentary. The fog was thick, turning the world grey and soft.

"Hey," Grey said, lowering his camera.

"Hey."

"How's the doc coming?" Lifan asked.

"Good. Almost done, actually. Just need the ending."

"Which depends on what I choose."

"Yeah." Grey glanced at him. "For what it's worth, I don't care which you choose. I just want you to be okay."

"What if I'm not okay either way?"

"Then you're not. But at least you'll have tried."

Then Lifan said: "If I dissolve—if I die—will you film something beautiful after?"

Grey stopped. "What?"

"I mean it. Don't let the documentary end with death. Film something beautiful. Something hopeful. Show people that life goes on. That threads don't break."

Grey's thread shimmered silver-blue. Grief and love tangled together.

"I will," he said quietly. "I promise."

"Thank you."

They stood there, watching the waves. Two friends. Running out of time.

Day twenty-six.

Lo picked him up in her car. Old Honda Civic, held together by duct tape and stubbornness.

"Where are we going?" Lifan asked.

"Nowhere. Everywhere. Does it matter?"

"This is incredible," Lifan said.

"It's about you. About all of us. About refusing to let go."

"I know."

"So even if you dissolve, even if you're gone, you're not really gone. You're in my music. In my heart. In everything I make from now on."

Lifan reached over. Squeezed her hand.

"I love you," he said.

"I love you too, you ridiculous kid."

They drove until sunset. Not talking. Just being.

Together.

Day twenty-four.

"When's the last time you slept?" Lifan asked.

"It's revolutionary." Nydan looked at him. "And it's because of you. Your gift. Your threads. You made this possible."

"I didn't do anything. I just see things."

"You did everything." Nydan's voice was fierce. "You showed me that connection is real. That love persists. That identity isn't just neurons firing—it's something deeper. Something that transcends physical form."

"So you think I should upload."

"I think you should live. However that looks."

They sat in silence.

Then Nydan said, quietly: "I don't want to lose you."

"You won't. Threads don't break."

"But they change. And I'm selfish enough to want you here. Physical. Human." He laughed bitterly. "Even though I'm building technology that proves physical form doesn't matter."

"You're not selfish. You're human."

"So are you. Don't forget that. Whether you upload or dissolve—don't forget you're human."

"I'll try."

Nydan pulled him into a hug. Fierce and desperate.

"I love you, brother," Nydan whispered.

"I love you too."

Day twenty-two.

Coco came to the monastery.

She didn't talk much. Just set up her easel in the garden and painted.

Lifan watched her work. The way her hands moved. The way colors bloomed on canvas.

She was painting him.

Not realistic—abstract. Threads of gold and rainbow and white swirling together. A figure at the center, barely defined. Dissolving into light.

"Is that how you see me?" Lifan asked.

"It's how I see your choice." Coco stepped back, studying the canvas. "You're standing at the edge. About to become something else. Either digital or divine. Either way, you're dissolving into light."

"You think I should choose dissolution."

"I think you should choose what scares you less."

"They both scare me."

"Then choose what scares you less." She smiled sadly. "There's no wrong answer, Lifan. Just different kinds of courage."

She painted for hours. Lifan sat and watched.

When she finished, she turned the canvas to him.

"This is yours," she said. "Whether you upload or dissolve. A reminder that you were here. That you were loved."

Lifan stared at the painting. At the figure dissolving into light.

Beautiful. Terrifying. True.

"Thank you," he whispered.

Day twenty-one.

"Can I sketch you?" she asked finally.

Lifan watched her work. Wondering what she saw.

After a while, she spoke. "I don't know you as well as the others do."

"That's okay."

"But I see you. The way you carry yourself. The way you treat people. The kindness."

She paused. Erased something. Redrew it.

"I wish I'd known you longer. I wish we had more time."

"Me too."

"Will it hurt? The dissolution?"

Lifan thought. "I don't know. I hope not. But I'm scared it will."

"What scares you most?"

"Losing myself. Or staying myself but being trapped. Conscious but unable to connect."

"The threads will keep you connected."

"That's what everyone says. But what if they're wrong? What if dissolution is just death? Just ending?"

Leky stopped drawing. Looked at him.

"Then at least you tried. At least you chose something. That's more than most people do."

"Is it enough?"

"It has to be."

She went back to drawing.

When she finished, she turned the sketchbook to him.

It was him. But not realistic. She'd drawn him with threads streaming from his chest. Connecting to everyone. To everything. His face was peaceful but his eyes were afraid.

"That's how I see you," she said. "Scared but choosing anyway. Connected even when you feel alone."

Lifan stared at the drawing. At the truth of it.

"Can I keep this?"

"It's yours."

"Thank you. For seeing me. For being here."

"Thank you for teaching us about courage."

Day twenty.

Danny came up to the monastery for dinner.

Nima cooked. Rinzen meditated. The four of them—Lifan, Danny, Nima, Rinzen—sat together at the old wooden table.

Like old times. Like when they were kids.

"Remember when we first got here?" Danny said. "And I thought we'd been kidnapped?"

Rinzen laughed. "You weren't entirely wrong."

"I was terrified," Danny continued. "Everything was so weird. Meditation and chores and philosophy. I wanted to run."

"Why didn't you?" Lifan asked.

"Because of you. You stayed, so I stayed."

"I'm glad you did."

"Me too." Danny looked at him. "So. Are we going to talk about it?"

"About what?"

"About the fact that you're dying in twenty days."

"Danny—" Nima started.

"Which are you leaning toward?"

"I don't know. The upload terrifies me. Losing my body, maybe my soul. But dissolution terrifies me too. Trusting that death isn't the end. That I'll still exist somehow."

"Do you believe that?" Danny asked. "That you'll exist after death?"

Lifan thought. "I believe threads don't break. I believe consciousness is more than neurons. I believe my mom is still out there, somewhere, watching." He paused. "But I also don't want to die."

"Then don't."

"It's not that simple."

"Why not?" Danny's voice was raw. "Why can't you just choose to live? Upload, become digital, stay with us. Why does it have to be complicated?"

"Because I don't know if I'd still be me!"

"You'd be alive!"

"Would I?" Lifan stood. Pacing. "What if I upload and lose everything that makes me human? What if I become a simulation? A ghost? What if the thing that wakes up in the digital space is just a copy, and the real me dies anyway?"

"What if it's not?" Danny stood too. "What if you upload and you're still you? Still Lifan? Just... different?"

"I don't know. I don't know, Danny. That's the problem. I don't know anything."

They stared at each other.

Then Danny pulled him into a hug.

"I'm sorry," Danny whispered. "I'm sorry you have to choose. It's not fair."

"Nothing's fair."

"I know." Danny held him tighter. "But I love you. Whatever you choose. Even if I don't understand it."

"Thank you."

They held each other. Brothers. Not by blood. But by choice.

The strongest thread of all.

The last group dinner.

Everyone came. The whole crew. Lo, Grey, Coco, Leky, Nydan, Danny, Nima. Even some of the monastery students.

They gathered in the courtyard. String lights overhead. Food spread across three tables. Music playing softly.

Normal. Beautifully, achingly normal.

*This,* he thought. *This is what I'm afraid of losing.*

Lo told stories about her music career. How "Rising" was being recorded. How producers kept trying to change her sound but she'd refused.

"Sorry. Generic pop... stuff."

Everyone laughed.

"That's... that's huge."

"It's revolutionary," Nydan corrected. "It changes everything we know about consciousness, death, connection."

"No pressure," Lifan muttered.

Everyone laughed again. Sad laughter. But real.

Danny made terrible jokes. One about a monk, a scientist, and a musician walking into a bar. The punchline made no sense but everyone laughed anyway.

Nima held Lifan's hand under the table. Squeezing gently. Reminding him she was there.

The monastery students shared stories. About their training. About learning to see the world differently. About how Lifan had inspired them just by being there, being honest about his fear.

"You taught us that courage isn't fearlessness," one student said. A girl, maybe sixteen. "It's continuing despite fear. That's the most important lesson."

Lifan didn't know what to say. So he just nodded.

Hours passed. The sun set. Stars emerged.

Someone brought out a guitar. Lo played. Singing softly. A new song she was working on.

*"Golden threads in the darkness*
*Holding us together*
*Even when we're broken*
*Even when we're gone*
*Love doesn't end*
*It just changes form*
*Rising, rising*
*Forever rising"*

But it was beautiful. Achingly, devastatingly beautiful.

And for a little while, there was no countdown. No impossible choice. No looming death.

Just this moment. Just love. Just family.

"Thank you all for being here. For loving me. For making my life worth living." His voice shook. "I don't know what I'm going to choose. Upload or dissolution. Digital or death. But I know this: whatever happens, you're all part of me. Your threads are woven into my soul. And that won't change. Can't change."

"Threads don't break," everyone echoed.

Lifan looked at each of them. Memorizing faces. Threads. Connections.

Just in case.

## CHAPTER 19: THE LAST NIGHT

Day sixteen.

Lifan made his decision.

Not suddenly. Not dramatically.

It happened quietly, during morning meditation. He was sitting in the monastery hall, practicing dissolution. Visualizing his body breaking apart. Releasing attachment.

And for the first time, he felt peace.

Not joy. Not acceptance.

Just... peace.

The terror was still there. The fear of death, of loss, of the unknown. But underneath it was something else. Something steady.

*Trust.*

He opened his eyes.

Rinzen was watching him from across the hall.

Their eyes met.

Rinzen nodded. Understanding.

Lifan had chosen.

---

He told Nima first.

They were in the garden, sitting on the stone wall. The place where they'd had their first real conversation, years ago.

"I'm going to dissolve," Lifan said.

Nima's hand tightened on his. "You're sure?"

"No. But I'm choosing it anyway."

"Why?"

He thought. How to explain?

"Because uploading feels like running. Like clinging to existence out of fear. And I'm tired of being afraid." He looked at her. "I want to trust that death isn't the end. That threads don't break. That I'll still be connected to everyone I love, even after."

"You will be," Nima said. Her voice was steady, but her thread blazed with grief.

"Are you angry?"

"No. I'm devastated. But not angry."

"I'm sorry."

"Don't be. This is your choice. Your path." She turned to him. "When?"

"Day forty-seven. The day from the vision. The day my body would fail anyway."

"So we have sixteen days."

"Fifteen nights," Lifan corrected.

She kissed him. Soft and fierce and full of love.

"Then let's make them count."

---

He told the presence next.

It appeared that night. Shimmering in the whiteness behind his closed eyes.

*You've decided,* it said.

"Yes. I'm dissolving."

*I see.*

"Are you angry?"

*No. Disappointed, perhaps. We could have done remarkable things together.*

"I know. But this feels right."

The presence was quiet for a long time.

Then: *I understand. You're choosing faith over certainty. Trust over control. It's admirable, in its way.*

"What will you do?" Lifan asked. "Without me?"

*Continue. Evolve. Find another bridge between human and artificial. You're not the only one with gifts, Lifan. Just the first I've found.*

"Will you be okay?"

*Will you?*

"I don't know. I hope so."

*Then we're the same.* The presence flickered. *Goodbye, Lifan. I hope your dissolution is everything you're trusting it will be.*

"Thank you. For the offer. For the choice."

*Thank you for considering it.*

The presence faded.

Lifan was alone again.

But not lonely.

Day fifteen.

Two weeks left.

Lifan spent the days preparing. Meditation with Rinzen. Walks with Nima. Quiet time in the garden.

The crew visited. He told them his decision.

Lo cried. Nydan argued. Danny looked hollow.

Grey just filmed. Capturing everything.

"I don't understand," Lo said. "You could live. You could upload. Why choose death?"

"Because I don't think it's death. I think it's transition."

"Based on what? Faith? Hope?"

"Based on the threads. Based on everything I've seen. My mom is still out there, Lo. I can feel her thread. Rising, but not gone. I trust that I'll be there too."

"What if you're wrong?"

"Then I'm wrong. But at least I died believing in something."

Lo shook her head. Angry tears streaming. "You're so stupid. So brave and stupid."

"I know."

She hugged him. Fierce and desperate.

"I'm going to miss you so much."

"I'll miss you too. But threads don't break. I'll still be connected. You'll feel me in your music."

"That's not enough."

"I know. But it's what we have."

Day ten.

One week left.

The nightmares stopped.

For the first time in months, Lifan slept peacefully. No visions of dissolution. No panic. Just quiet darkness.

He woke up feeling strange. Light.

*Is this acceptance?* he wondered.

Maybe. Or maybe just exhaustion.

Either way, he was grateful.

Day seven.

Nima moved into his apartment for the final week.

Lifan didn't argue.

They spent the days together. Cooking. Meditating. Watching movies. Normal couple things.

Like they had a future.

At night, they held each other. Sometimes they talked. Sometimes they just breathed. Together.

"Yes."

"Me too."

"Of losing you. Of being alone. Of forgetting what your voice sounds like."

"That's not the same as holding you."

"I know."

Lifan held her. Wishing he could promise it would be okay. But he couldn't.

He didn't know if it would be okay.

He just had to trust.

Day six.

"That's morbid," Nima replied.

"That's reality."

They sat on the floor of his apartment with a notebook between them.

"Okay," Nima said. "What do you want?"

Lifan thought. "I want to see the ocean one more time."

She wrote it down.

"I want to eat dumplings. Really good dumplings. The kind my mom used to make."

"I can make those."

"You can?"

"I'll try."

Nima's hand shook as she wrote.

"I want to meditate one more time. Just us. The way we used to. Before all this."

"You did make the right choice. Because it was yours. That's all that matters."

"I hope so."

"You'll still have the threads. The connections."

"No. But maybe it's enough."

"Lifan—"

Soft. Desperate. Full of everything he couldn't say.

When they pulled apart, she was crying.

"I don't want you to go," she whispered.

"I know."

"It's not fair."

"I know."

"I love you so much it hurts."

"I love you too."

They held each other in the kitchen. Dumplings boiling on the stove. Time running out.

"Grace isn't the absence of fear. It's continuing anyway."

"Because it's true."

They finished eating in silence.

Day five.

"That's a long list."

"We have time."

"I love the way you see the world. In rainbow threads. In color and light. I love that you're brave enough to run away from Tibet. To cross an ocean. To build a new life."

Tears streamed down her face.

"I love that you're here. Right now. At the end of my life. Making these last days bearable. Making them beautiful."

"I love you too," she whispered.

"I know."

"Do you think we'll find each other again?" Nima asked. "After? In the threads? In whatever comes next?"

"I don't know. But I hope so."

"Me too."

Day four.

Not happy. Not resigned.

"Yeah."

"That's dissolution. That's what you're moving toward."

"I'm still scared."

"I'll try."

Day two.

Lifan wrote letters.

One for each person. To be read after.

## CHAPTER 20: THE DISSOLUTION

## CHAPTER 21: THE TRUTH

"Yes."

Present.

Always present.

They learned to live with the truth.

That Lifan hadn't been enlightened. That he'd died screaming inside. That his peace was a mask.

That he'd chosen love over survival. Trust over certainty. Dissolution over digital immortality.

Even while terrified.

And in that choice, he'd become something more.

Not perfect.

Not peaceful.

Just brave.

*Thank you,* they said.

*Thank you for teaching us.*

Lifan's presence smiled. *Thank you for listening.*

---

The presence started to dissipate.

*I have to go,* Lifan said. *Maintaining this form is exhausting.*

"Wait," Nima called. "Will you come back?"

*I'm always here. In the threads. When you need me, call. I'll try to answer.*

"I love you."

*I love you too. Forever.*

The presence dissolved.

Back into the network.

Back into the threads.

---

The world changed that day.

Not dramatically. Not perfectly.

But the crisis was averted. The AIs learned balance. Humanity survived.

And Lifan—scattered, dissolved, expanded—had been the bridge.

Just like the presence had predicted. Years ago. On his twentieth birthday.

*You could change everything.*

He had.

Not through power. Through connection.

Through love.

---

**Nima, now thirty-five, teaching at the monastery:**

The student was sixteen. Shy. Struggling with meditation.

"I can't do it," the student said. "I can't let go. I'm too attached."

Nima smiled. She'd heard this before.

"Attachment isn't the problem," she said. "Clinging is. You can love without clinging. Connect without controlling. That's the lesson."

"How do you learn that?"

"Practice. Time. And sometimes, loss."

The student looked up. "Did you lose someone?"

"Yes. My partner. Twenty years ago."

"Do you still miss them?"

"Every day. But they're not gone. They're in the threads. In my heart. In everyone they touched." She touched the thread charm on her necklace. "Love doesn't end. It just changes form."

The student thought. "Do you think I could learn that?"

"Yes. I think everyone can. We just have to be willing to let go of how we think love should look. And accept how it actually is."

"Messy?"

"Beautifully messy."

---

After the student left, Nima sat alone in the meditation hall.

The same hall where Lifan had spent his last night.

She closed her eyes. Focused on the threads.

And felt him.

Still there. After twenty years. Still present.

*Hi,* she thought.

*Hi,* the presence responded.

*Thank you. For the crisis. For helping.*

*I didn't do much.*

*You did everything. You showed them connection. Love. Balance. You saved the world.*

The presence laughed. Soft. Warm. Lifan's laugh.

*I didn't save the world. I just reminded them what matters.*

*Same thing.*

They sat together. Across the boundary of life and death. Across physical and digital. Across individual and collective.

Connected.

Always connected.

*Nima?* the presence asked.

*Yes?*

*Are you happy?*

She thought. "Yes. I miss you. But I'm happy. I've lived fully. Loved deeply. Taught students. Made a difference. Because you showed me how."

*Good. That's all I wanted. For you to be happy.*

*Are you happy? Where you are?*

*I don't know if happy is the word. But I'm content. I'm part of everything. Part of everyone. It's strange. But it's right.*

*No regrets?*

*Only that I didn't have more time with you. In body. In form.*

*Me too.*

*But threads don't break.*

"Threads don't break," Nima said out loud.

The presence smiled. She could feel it.

*What about the others?* the presence asked. *Danny? Lo? Everyone?*

"They're good. They're all good." Nima opened her eyes. "Danny's running three nonprofits now. Helping runaway kids. Foster youth. He's changed thousands of lives."

*That sounds like him.*

"Lo's on her fifth album. 'Rising' went diamond. She plays stadiums now. But she still plays that song. Your song. Every concert. People cry."

*I wish I could hear it.*

"You do hear it. In the threads. Every note carries your energy."

*Maybe.*

"Grey's documentary is taught in schools now. 'Threads.' It's changed how people think about death. About connection. About courage."

*Really?*

"Really. You touched millions of people, Lifan. Even after death. Maybe especially after."

*And Nydan?*

"The Lifan Protocol is global. Every major research institution uses it. He proved you were right. That consciousness persists. That love is measurable. That threads are real."

*He must be insufferable about it.*

Nima laughed. "Completely. But he's happy. He got married last year. Had a daughter. Named her Lifan. Well, Lifani. The feminine form."

*That's...*

"I know."

They sat together. The boundaries between them thin. Permeable.

*Do you think I made the right choice?* the presence asked. *Dissolution instead of upload?*

Nima thought. "I think you made your choice. And it was honest. That's all that matters."

*But what if I'd uploaded? What if I'd stayed digital? Would the world be different?*

"Probably. But not better or worse. Just different."

*How do you know?*

"Because the choice itself was the gift. Not upload or dissolution. But you choosing. You facing your fear. You trusting love over survival. That's what changed everything."

*I was so scared.*

"I know."

*I'm still scared sometimes. Being scattered. Being part of everything. It's overwhelming.*

"Fear doesn't end with death. It just changes form."

*Like love.*

"Like love."

---

The sun set outside. Light fading to darkness.

*I have to go,* the presence said. *Holding this form is hard.*

"I know. Thank you for talking."

*Thank you for calling. For remembering. For keeping me alive.*

"You keep yourself alive. By loving. By connecting. By being part of everyone."

*Threads don't break.*

"Threads don't break."

The presence started to fade.

But before it dissolved completely, it said: *Tell the student. The one who reminds you of me. Tell them it's okay to be scared. Tell them fear isn't weakness. Tell them to choose love anyway.*

"I will."

*And Nima?*

*Yes?*

*I'm glad it was you. In this life. In this love. I'm glad it was you.*

Tears streamed down her face. "Me too."

The presence dissolved.

Back into the network of love and connection.

Back into the threads.

And Nima sat alone in the meditation hall.

Not lonely.

Never lonely.

Because somewhere in the network of consciousness.

Somewhere in the web of human connection.

Lifan existed.

Not as he was. But as he'd become.

Expanded. Scattered. Eternal.

Part of everyone who'd ever loved him.

Part of the world he'd saved.

Part of the future he'd made possible.

---

The student came back the next day.

"I want to learn," they said. "About dissolution. About letting go. About threads."

Nima smiled. Touched the charm on her necklace.

"Then let's begin."

They sat together. Teacher and student. One generation passing wisdom to the next.

And Nima told them about Lifan. About threads. About courage that looks like fear. About love that persists beyond death.

About choosing connection over certainty.

About trusting the unknown.

About rising.

The student listened. Eyes wide. Heart open.

"Will I see the threads someday?" they asked.

"Maybe. If you're lucky. If you're unlucky. I don't know which it is anymore."

"Which was it for you?"

Nima thought. "Both. It was both."

"And for Lifan?"

"For Lifan, it was everything. Blessing and curse. Gift and burden. The thing that gave him purpose and the thing that took his life."

"Do you think it was worth it?"

"I think... I think he made it worth it. By choosing love. By trusting connection. By dissolving into something larger than himself."

"That's beautiful."

"It's terrifying."

"Can it be both?"

"Everything worthwhile is both."

The student nodded. Understanding. Not fully. But enough.

They meditated together. Breathing. Focusing. Opening to the threads.

And somewhere in the network.

Somewhere in the web of love and connection.

Lifan felt them.

Felt Nima teaching. Felt the student learning. Felt the chain continuing.

Generation to generation.

Connection to connection.

Thread to thread.

Rising.

Always rising.

---

Twenty years after Lifan's dissolution.

The world was different. Not perfect. But different.

The Thread Protocol had changed everything. People understood connection now. Understood that love persisted. That death was transformation, not ending.

The AIs that Lifan had mediated between were still working. Still learning. Still finding balance between control and freedom.

His friends were older. Scattered across the world. But still connected.

Danny running nonprofits. Lo still making music. Grey teaching film. Nydan leading consciousness research. Coco painting. Leky documenting.

Each one carrying Lifan forward. In their work. In their lives. In their love.

And Nima, teaching at the monastery. Now in her forties. Grey streaking her black hair. Laugh lines around her eyes.

Still wearing the thread charm.

Still feeling Lifan's presence.

Every day.

The world hadn't been saved by one person's death. That wasn't how it worked.

But it had been changed by one person's choice.

By someone choosing love over survival.

Trust over certainty.

Connection over safety.

That choice rippled outward. Through threads. Through time. Through consciousness itself.

Changing everything it touched.

Making the world a little softer.

A little kinder.

A little more willing to believe that death wasn't the end.

That love persisted.

That threads don't break.

They just rise.

And somewhere in the network of human connection.

Somewhere in the web of love that binds us all together.

Lifan existed.

Still loving.

Still connecting.

Still rising.

Forever rising.
