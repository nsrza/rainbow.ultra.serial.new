## EPISODE 1: THE RISING

*San Francisco, California*
*March 15, 2026*
*Lifan is six years old*

---

The light through the kitchen window was doing something strange.

Lifan watched from his spot on the floor, crayons scattered around him, drawing forgotten. Morning sun hit the water glass on the counter and broke into colors—but not the rainbow he'd learned in school. These colors moved. They stretched between things. Between the glass and the window. Between the eggs in the pan and his mother's hands. Between his mother and him.

Golden threads, shimmering like spiderwebs caught in light.

"妈妈," he said. "The eggs are connected to you."

Ning Chen turned from the stove, her face soft with the particular patience she reserved for her son's observations. At thirty-four, she carried herself with a stillness that made other people nervous—the kind of calm that suggested she knew things she wasn't saying. Her black hair was pulled back, a few strands escaping to frame her face.

"Connected how, 小梵?"

"With gold strings. They go from the eggs to your hands to your heart." He traced the air, following lines only he could see. "And you have the most strings of anyone. They go everywhere. To me—" he pointed at the bright thread between them, "—that one's so bright it hurts. To 爸爸's picture—" he gestured at the photo on the refrigerator, "—that one's sad. Blue and purple, like a bruise that's healing."

Ning set down the spatula. Crossed to him and knelt on the tile floor, her knees cracking softly. She smelled like sesame oil and the jasmine lotion she rubbed into her hands every night.

"小梵," she said, her voice careful. "What you're seeing—can you see the strings between other people? Not just to me?"

"Everyone has them. But most people only have a few. You have hundreds."

"And do they ever break? The strings?"

Lifan considered this. He'd been seeing the threads for as long as he could remember, but he'd only recently started paying attention to what they did.

"Sometimes they get thin," he said. "Like when Mrs. Patterson was mad at Mr. Patterson. Their string got really skinny. But it didn't break."

"Have you ever seen one break?"

The question felt heavy. Lifan looked at his mother's face and saw something there he didn't recognize—not worry exactly, but something deeper. Something that made her strings tremble.

"No," he said. "I don't think they break. I think they just... change."

Ning was quiet for a moment. Then she pulled him into her lap, holding him against her chest. He could feel her heartbeat, steady and strong, could see the thread between them pulsing with each beat.

"小梵, I need to tell you something. About your name."

"Lifan? It means 'sail against the wind.' You told me."

"That's what I tell other people. But your real name—your Chinese name—is 梵力. Do you know what 梵 means?"

"Fan? Like a fan for cooling down?"

"Different character. 梵 is the sound underneath all sounds. The hum that everything makes when it's truly alive. Some people call it the universe's heartbeat. And 力 means power, or strength." She pulled back to look at him, her eyes wet. "Your name means the power of that sound. The strength of connection itself."

"Why are you crying?"

"Because I'm proud of you. And because—" She stopped. The threads around her flickered, and Lifan saw something he'd never noticed before: one of her threads was different from the others. Thinner. Paler. Like it was already halfway gone.

"Mommy, one of your strings is sick."

Ning's breath caught. "Which one?"

He pointed at her chest, at the pale thread that seemed to lead somewhere he couldn't see. "That one. It's almost not there."

She pressed her hand to her chest, over her heart. Her face went very still.

"小梵, I need you to listen to me. What you see—the strings, the threads—it's a gift. A very rare gift. My mother had it, and her mother before her. We call it 見梵—seeing the 梵. Seeing what connects everything."

"Is that why I'm weird?"

"You're not weird. You're precious." She held his face in her hands. "But people don't understand what they can't see. So we have to be careful about who we tell. Do you understand?"

"Yes, Mommy."

"Good." She kissed his forehead, long and lingering. "Now, I need you to remember something else. Something important."

"What?"

"The threads don't break, 小梵. When someone we love goes away—when their body stops working—their threads don't break. They rise. Like smoke from a candle. They become part of everything instead of just one person."

"Is someone going away?"

Ning's eyes filled with tears, but she was smiling. That was confusing—tears and smiles usually didn't happen together, except with her. She had always been a puzzle he couldn't quite solve, even with his gift for seeing connections.

"Everyone goes away eventually," she said. "But the love stays. The threads stay. They just change form."

"I don't want you to go away."

"I know, baby. I know."

She held him for a long time. Through the kitchen window, the morning light kept breaking into colors, and the threads kept shimmering, and Lifan memorized the feeling of her arms around him, the smell of jasmine and sesame, the steady pulse of the brightest thread he'd ever seen.

---

The thing about that morning: she knew.

Lifan would realize it later, much later, when he was old enough to understand what premonition meant. His mother had known, somehow, that her time was running out. The pale thread he'd seen—the sick one—had been her heart, failing slowly for months. She'd never told anyone. She'd just started preparing.

Teaching him about his gift. Giving him the vocabulary to understand it. Making sure he knew that threads don't break.

They rise.

---

It happened at 3:47 in the afternoon.

Lifan was in the living room, watching cartoons. Ning was in the kitchen, washing dishes. The thread between them pulsed steadily, a metronome of connection, and Lifan had learned to take comfort in its rhythm. As long as that thread kept pulsing, everything was okay.

Then the rhythm stopped.

Not gradually—suddenly. Like a song cut off mid-note. Lifan felt it before he heard the crash of dishes, felt the thread between them go taut and then terrifyingly slack.

"妈妈?"

He ran to the kitchen.

She was on the floor. The dishes had shattered around her, and she was clutching her chest, her face pale and twisted with pain. The threads around her were going haywire—some pulsing frantically, some dimming, some starting to lift away from her body like they couldn't decide whether to stay or go.

"妈妈!"

Lifan dropped to his knees beside her. He didn't know what to do—he was six, he didn't know about 911 or CPR or anything useful. All he knew was that his mother was hurting and her threads were wrong and he had to fix it somehow.

"小梵—" Her voice was a whisper. "Don't be scared."

"You're hurt. I need to get help."

"Listen to me." Her hand found his, squeezing weakly. "Remember what I told you. About the threads."

"They don't break. They rise." Tears were streaming down his face. "But you're not going anywhere. You can't."

"I love you. More than all the threads in the world." She smiled—actually smiled, even though she was dying. "Watch them, 小梵. Watch what happens to my threads. So you know I'm telling the truth."

"No—"

"Watch."

So he watched.

Her threads were lifting now. One by one, the connections that had bound her to the world were letting go. The thread to the apartment—dimming, fading, floating upward. The thread to her favorite coffee mug, the one with the chip in the handle—releasing, rising. The thread to 爸爸's picture—that sad blue-purple one—stretching thin and then drifting toward the ceiling like smoke.

"No, no, no—"

"Don't be afraid." Her voice was barely audible now. "It doesn't hurt. I can feel it. I'm becoming part of everything."

And her main thread—the bright core that was *her*, the thing that made Ning Ning—that was rising too. Not breaking. Rising. Like it had somewhere to go. Like she was going somewhere beautiful.

"I don't want you to go."

"I'll never leave you, 小梵." Her hand in his was cold now, but her smile was warm. "I'll be in every thread you see. In the connection between all things. Look for me there."

"Mommy—"

"Remember. Threads don't break."

"They rise," he whispered.

"They rise."

Her eyes closed. Her hand went slack. And the last of her threads—the great golden rope that had connected her to him since before he was born—lifted from her body and rose toward the ceiling, toward the sky, toward something vast and beautiful that Lifan couldn't quite see but could feel, could feel in his bones, in his blood, in the part of him that would always be connected to her.

She was gone.

But she hadn't broken.

She had risen.

---

He sat with her body for two hours before the neighbors heard him crying. By then, the afternoon light had shifted, and the threads in the apartment had rearranged themselves around the absence where his mother used to be.

But the thread between them—the golden rope that had been the brightest thing in his world—wasn't gone. It had changed. Spread out. Diffused into the air itself, so that everywhere Lifan looked, he could see traces of her. In the dust motes floating in the sunlight. In the curtains moving with the breath of the apartment. In his own small hands, which were now threaded with gold they hadn't held before.

She had become part of everything.

And he could still feel her. Fainter, more distant, but there. Always there.

It would take him years to understand what this meant. Years to learn that what he'd witnessed wasn't an ending but a transformation. Years to discover that the gift his mother had given him—the ability to see connection—came with a cost he couldn't yet imagine.

But in that moment, sitting beside her body as the sirens approached, Lifan held onto the one truth that would carry him through everything that came next:

Threads don't break. They rise.

And if he looked hard enough, he could always find her.

---

*End of Episode 1*

## EPISODE 2: THE SYSTEM

*Various locations, California*
*2026-2030*
*Lifan is six to ten years old*

---

The foster care system was not designed for boys who saw threads.

This is what Lifan learned in the four years after his mother's death: there were things you could say and things you couldn't. Things that got you moved to a new home and things that got you labeled. Things that made adults look at you with pity and things that made them look at you with fear.

Saying "I miss my mom" was acceptable.

Saying "I can still see her in the dust motes" was not.

---

The Hendersons were first.

They were nice enough—older, white, a little nervous around him. They spoke slowly and loudly like he might not understand English, even though Ning had made sure his English was perfect. They gave him a room with dinosaur sheets and told him he could call them Grandma and Grandpa if he wanted.

He didn't want.

The problem was the threads. Mrs. Henderson had a knotted mess of them around her stomach—worry and fear and something darker that Lifan later learned was called anxiety. Every time she looked at him, the knots tightened. She was afraid of him, he realized. Afraid of his grief. Afraid that he would break and she wouldn't know how to fix him.

So she waited for him to break.

And Lifan, who had never been good at lying but was learning fast, showed her what she expected. He cried at appropriate moments. He ate his vegetables. He said thank you and please and yes ma'am. He did not mention the threads. He did not mention his mother's presence in the air. He did not mention any of the things that made him different, because different got you moved.

But Mrs. Henderson's thread-knots got tighter anyway. And after three months, the social worker came.

"Lifan is a lovely boy," Mrs. Henderson told her, wringing her hands. "But there's something... odd about him. The way he stares. Like he's looking at something that isn't there."

She wasn't wrong. He had been watching her anxiety threads multiply, wondering if she knew they were strangling her.

New home. New family. New rules to learn.

---

The Patels were second.

They had two biological children and three foster kids, a chaotic household where Lifan could blend in. Mr. Patel worked long hours. Mrs. Patel was kind but exhausted, her threads frayed from the constant pulling of too many demands.

Lifan liked it there. The noise covered his oddness. The other foster kids—a girl named Maria and a boy named Devon—were too focused on their own survival to notice his. He learned to navigate by watching their threads: when Devon's went sharp and red, it meant he was about to lash out, so Lifan made himself small and invisible. When Maria's went soft and gold, it meant she was safe to talk to, so Lifan would sit with her and listen to stories about the grandmother she missed.

He stayed for eight months. His longest placement.

Then Devon saw him talking to the air.

"What are you doing?"

Lifan froze. He'd been in the backyard, watching the threads rise from the garden where Mrs. Patel grew tomatoes. Sometimes, if he concentrated, he could feel his mother in those threads—a whisper of her presence, like perfume lingering in an empty room.

"Nothing."

"You were talking. To nobody."

"I was just... thinking out loud."

Devon's threads went sharp and red. "You're crazy. My last foster brother was crazy too. They took him away."

"I'm not crazy."

"Crazy people never think they're crazy." Devon smiled—a mean smile, the kind that came from having been hurt too much. "I'm telling Mrs. Patel."

He did.

New home. New family. New rules to learn.

---

What Lifan learned: seeing too much made people uncomfortable. Feeling too much made them suspicious. Being too still, too quiet, too watchful—all of it got you labeled. And once labeled, always labeled. The paperwork followed him from placement to placement, a thread of words he couldn't see but could feel: *attachment issues*, *possible developmental delay*, *occasionally exhibits unusual behavior*.

So he learned to perform.

He learned which expressions made adults relax. How to time his smiles so they seemed genuine. How to ask questions that made teachers feel smart and caregivers feel needed. He learned to compress his real self—the self that saw threads, that talked to his mother in empty rooms, that felt everything too deeply—into a small, tight ball in the center of his chest.

He showed them what they wanted to see.

He became what they needed him to be.

And underneath, in the quiet place where the threads still shimmered and his mother's presence still lingered, the real Lifan waited. Patient. Hidden. Alone.

This was the mask. And once he put it on, he found it very hard to take off.

---

Pacific House was different.

Not better—different. A group home for older kids, ages ten to seventeen, kids who had bounced through too many placements to be attractive to families anymore. The building was ugly—industrial gray concrete, barred windows, a yard made of cracked asphalt—but the threads inside were different from anywhere Lifan had been.

Survival threads. That's what he called them. Thin and tough, like wire, connecting the kids to each other in a web of mutual understanding. *We're all damaged here. We all know what it's like.*

Lifan arrived three days after his tenth birthday, which was also four years after his mother's death. March 18th, 2030. He carried a garbage bag with his clothes and a small gray stone he'd found in the Hendersons' garden, smooth and cool and somehow comforting.

The first person he saw was Danny.

Danny was eleven, Black, with close-cropped hair and eyes that never stopped moving. His threads were different from anyone Lifan had ever seen—organized, almost mathematical, like they'd been sorted into categories and filed away for later reference.

"New kid," Danny said, not a question.

"Yeah."

"You gonna cry? The last new kid cried for three days."

"No."

Danny's eyes narrowed, assessing. His threads shifted, calculating something Lifan couldn't quite follow.

"You're weird," Danny said finally. "Not bad weird. Just... weird."

"I know."

"At least you know it." Danny jerked his head toward the stairs. "Come on. I'll show you around before Kevin finds you."

"Who's Kevin?"

"You don't want to know. Come on."

Lifan followed him. And as they walked, he watched Danny's threads and saw something he hadn't seen in any of his foster placements:

Recognition. One orphan to another.

Maybe this place would be different after all.

---

## EPISODE 3: THE CLOSET

*Pacific House Group Home*
*October 2030*
*Lifan is ten years old*

---

Kevin found him anyway.

Kevin was sixteen, white, built like a wall. His threads were wrong in ways Lifan had never seen—tangled into knots that had rotted, blackened at the edges, pulsing with something that wasn't anger exactly. It was older than anger. Deeper. Kevin had been hurt so badly, so young, that his threads had curdled into something poisonous.

He ran Pacific House like a small kingdom. The staff were underpaid and overworked; they didn't notice what happened after lights out. Kevin noticed everything. He knew who was weak, who could be manipulated, who would keep their mouths shut.

Lifan had been there two weeks when Kevin cornered him.

"Heard you're a weird one," Kevin said. They were in the basement, the concrete room where the furnace hummed and no one came. "Heard you talk to yourself. See things that aren't there."

"I don't."

"Liar." Kevin smiled. His teeth were crooked. "I know a liar when I see one. Takes one to know one."

"What do you want?"

"Just making friends." Kevin's hand landed on Lifan's shoulder, heavy as a threat. "See, here's how it works. I keep the peace in this place. Nobody hurts each other unless I say so. Nobody steals unless I get my cut. And in exchange, everyone does what I say."

"Okay."

"Okay what?"

"Okay, I'll do what you say."

Kevin laughed. "See, that's what's weird about you. Most kids your age would be pissing themselves right now. But you're not scared, are you? You're watching. Like you're studying me."

Lifan said nothing.

"I don't like being studied." Kevin's grip tightened. "Makes me feel like a bug under glass. So here's what we're going to do. We're going to find out what you're really scared of."

---

The closet was in the basement.

Small. Dark. No windows, no light switch, just a door that locked from the outside. Kevin's special place for kids who didn't fall in line.

"Three days," Kevin said as he shoved Lifan inside. "No food, no water, no bathroom. Let's see if you're still weird when you come out."

The door closed. The lock clicked.

Darkness.

---

The first hour was fear.

Pure, animal fear. The kind that makes your heart race and your palms sweat and your mind go blank. Lifan had been afraid before—when his mother died, when he got moved to new homes, when Devon cornered him in the Patels' backyard. But this was different. This was darkness that pressed against him like a living thing, so complete that he couldn't tell if his eyes were open or closed.

His threads were useless here. Without light, he couldn't see them, could barely feel them. Just his own body, crammed into a space too small to stand, breathing air that tasted like dust and old fear.

He cried. Screamed. Begged for someone to let him out.

No one came.

---

The second hour was anger.

How dare Kevin do this? How dare the staff not notice? How dare his mother die and leave him alone in a world where things like this could happen?

The anger was hot and bright and almost comforting. Better than fear. Better than despair. He kicked the walls, slammed his fists against the door, wore his voice raw with curses he'd learned from the older kids.

No one came.

---

The third hour was grief.

His mother should have been here. Should have protected him. Should have lived.

But she hadn't. She'd left him alone, given him a gift he never asked for, promised him that threads don't break. And now he was locked in a closet in the dark, and all the threads in the world couldn't save him.

He curled into a ball on the floor and cried until he had nothing left.

---

The fourth hour was different.

Lifan lay in the darkness, empty of tears, empty of anger, empty of everything except the sound of his own breathing. And in that emptiness, something shifted.

He began to feel the threads again.

Not see them—it was too dark for that. But feel them. The connections that ran through everything, that linked him to the building around him, to the air in his lungs, to the boy upstairs who was doing this to him.

And to his mother.

She was there. Diffuse, distributed through everything, but there. He could feel her in the dust of the closet, in the wood of the door, in the concrete beneath him. She hadn't lied. She hadn't left. She'd just changed form.

*小梵,* her voice whispered, or maybe he imagined it, or maybe imagination and reality weren't as different as he'd thought. *Breathe. Just breathe.*

He breathed.

And something clicked into place.

The fear was still there. The anger, the grief—all of it still there. But underneath, he found something else. A stillness. A quiet center that the darkness couldn't touch.

*The threads are everywhere,* his mother had told him. *They don't break. They rise.*

What if he was the same?

What if Kevin's cruelty, this closet, this darkness—what if they were just pressure? Like water pressing on a diver? Like gravity pressing on a mountain?

*The pressure makes you smaller if you fight it,* he thought. *But if you let it pass through—*

He let go.

Not of his fear, but of his resistance to it. Let the darkness press against him. Let the fear flow through him like water through a net. Let the grief wash over him and keep washing, until he was clean.

And the stillness expanded.

In the darkness of the closet, Lifan found something he hadn't known he was looking for: the quiet underneath the noise. The space between the threads. The part of him that couldn't be touched by Kevin or fear or loneliness or loss.

*This wasn't Kevin's lesson,* Lifan realized. *Kevin didn't give me anything. But I found something here—in spite of him, not because of him. Something I had to find on my own.*

He wasn't the weaver of threads. He was the loom. And the loom didn't fight the thread—it gave the thread a place to go.

---

Danny found him on the third day.

Kevin had gotten careless, bragged to the wrong person. Danny had gone to the staff, threatened to call the police, made enough noise that someone had to listen. The closet door opened, and Lifan blinked in the sudden light, squinting up at Danny's terrified face.

"Jesus," Danny whispered. "Lifan. Can you hear me?"

"I hear you."

"You've been in there for three days. No food, no water. How are you—" Danny stopped. Stared at him. "You're not... you're okay?"

"I'm okay."

"That's not possible. You should be—I thought you were going to be—"

"I found something." Lifan's voice was hoarse, but steady. "In the dark. I found the quiet place."

Danny helped him up. His legs were weak, his stomach empty, his lips cracked with thirst. But his mind was clear. Clearer than it had ever been.

"You're crazy," Danny said, half crying, half laughing. "You're absolutely crazy."

"Maybe."

"What did you find? What quiet place?"

Lifan looked at Danny's threads—the organized, mathematical patterns that set him apart from everyone else. And underneath those patterns, he saw something new: a door. A potential. Something Danny didn't know about himself yet.

"I'll show you," Lifan said. "When we get out of here. I'll show you everything."

"Get out? Get out where?"

"Anywhere but here. Somewhere the threads go."

Danny stared at him for a long moment. Then he nodded.

"Okay," he said. "We'll get out together."

"Brothers."

"What?"

"We're brothers now." Lifan offered his hand—dirty, trembling, but certain. "You came for me. Nobody ever came for me before."

Danny took his hand.

"Brothers," he agreed.

And in that moment, in the fluorescent light of the basement, a new thread formed between them. Bright and strong and absolutely unbreakable.

---

## EPISODE 4: THE BROTHERS

*Pacific House to Big Sur*
*2030-2032*
*Lifan is ten to twelve years old*

---

They planned for eight months.

Danny—who would become Nydan, though he didn't know that yet—was good at planning. His strange gift, the one that organized his threads into mathematical patterns, turned out to be a kind of probability sense. He couldn't see the future exactly, but he could feel the shape of it. Could tell when a path was likely to succeed or fail.

"There's a monastery," he told Lifan one night, huddled in the space between their bunks. "Big Sur. My mom used to talk about it before she... anyway. Buddhist monks. They take in kids sometimes."

"How do you know?"

"I don't know. I feel it." Danny touched his chest, right over his heart. "There's something there. Waiting."

Lifan looked at the thread between them—the brotherhood thread, still new but growing stronger every day—and saw what Danny meant. It led somewhere. South, toward the coast, toward something neither of them could see but both could feel.

"Okay," Lifan said. "We'll go."

"We need money. Supplies. A route that doesn't get us caught."

"Can you figure all that out? With your... thing?"

Danny smiled. It was the first time Lifan had seen him really smile.

"Yeah," he said. "I can figure it out."

---

March 12th, 2032.

They left at 3 AM, slipping out through a window Danny had loosened over the course of weeks. Lifan carried a backpack with clothes, food, a flashlight, and his small gray stone. Danny carried a backpack with water, a map, and all the money they'd managed to save—$147.38 in small bills and coins.

The night was cold and clear. The threads of the world shimmered in the darkness, leading them south.

"Scared?" Danny asked.

"Yes."

"Good. Me too. Being scared means we're paying attention."

They walked through the sleeping city, past houses and stores and parks, past all the places that had never been home. And with every step, Lifan felt something lifting. Not his mother's threads—those had already risen. But something else. The weight of four years of pretending, of performing, of being what everyone needed him to be.

Out here, in the darkness, he didn't have to perform.

He just had to walk.

---

It took them three days.

Three days of hitchhiking with truck drivers who asked no questions, of sleeping in fields and under bridges, of rationing food and water and hope. Danny's probability sense guided them—*this driver is safe, that road is watched, turn left here, wait until dark.*

And Lifan's thread-sight watched for danger, read the intentions of strangers, found the paths that led toward something bright.

They made a good team. The seer and the calculator. The heart and the head.

On the morning of the third day, they crested a hill and saw the ocean.

"There," Danny whispered. "That's it."

Below them, perched on a cliff above the crashing waves, a monastery rose from the fog. Stone buildings, weathered and ancient-looking. A garden visible through the mist. And threads—hundreds of threads—connecting the place to everything around it.

Lifan had never seen anything so beautiful.

"How do we get in?" he asked.

"We walk up and knock. According to my calculations, there's a 73% chance they let us stay."

"What about the other 27%?"

"We figure something out." Danny grinned. "Come on. I'm tired of being scared."

---

The monk who answered the door was old, Asian, with a face like crumpled paper and eyes that had seen everything.

"We've been expecting you," he said.

Danny's jaw dropped. "What?"

"You're Daniel, yes? And this is 梵力." The monk pronounced it perfectly: Fan-li. The power of connection. "Your mother told me about you. Before she passed."

Lifan felt something shift in the world. Like a puzzle piece clicking into place.

"You knew my mother?"

"Ning was a student here, many years ago. Before she met your father. Before you were born." The monk smiled. "She told me that one day, a boy would come who could see the threads. She said I should watch for you."

"She knew? About all of this?"

"She knew many things she never told you. That was her way—not to burden you with knowledge you weren't ready for." The monk stepped aside, gesturing into the monastery. "But you're ready now. Both of you. Come in. There's much to learn, and not as much time as I would like."

"Wait—" Danny held up a hand. "Who are you? Why should we trust you?"

The monk's smile deepened.

"My name is Rinzen. And you should trust me because your probability sense is telling you to. Isn't it, Daniel?"

Danny went pale. Then nodded.

"Yeah," he whispered. "It is. It really is."

"Then come. Both of you." Rinzen turned to walk into the monastery. "There's tea ready. And a story I've waited a long time to tell."

---

The date was March 15th, 2032.

Exactly six years since Ning Chen had died on her kitchen floor, her threads rising toward something beautiful.

And now her son was standing in the place she had trained, surrounded by monks who had known her, guided by a brother who had saved him.

The threads don't break. They rise.

But sometimes, Lifan was beginning to understand, they lead you exactly where you need to go.

---

*End of Episode 4*

## EPISODE 5: THE LIAR

*Big Sur Monastery*
*2032-2034*
*Lifan is twelve to fourteen years old*

---

Here's what Lifan didn't tell anyone:

The threads were getting worse.

Not worse like broken or wrong—worse like overwhelming. Like drinking from a firehose. Like drowning in light.

In the monastery, surrounded by monks who had spent decades calming their minds, the threads were everywhere. Brilliant, pulsing, connected to everything. Rinzen's threads were so vast and complex that looking at them directly gave Lifan headaches. The other students' threads tangled and danced with every interaction, every emotion, every fleeting thought.

And his mother. ## EPISODE 6: THE GIRL

## EPISODE 7: THE TEACHING

"Okay," she said. "We don't hide."

They walked back to the monastery hand in hand.

And Lifan, for the first time since his mother died, felt like he was exactly where he was supposed to be.

## EPISODE 16: THE FEAR

*What follows is the truth. The whole truth.*

Lifan was terrified.

He wanted to stay.

He didn't tell anyone.

Because telling would have changed things. Would have made them doubt.

So he lied.

*This was not noble. This was the same pattern that had haunted him since childhood—protecting others from his pain because he didn't trust them to hold it. Because he didn't believe he deserved the comfort of being honest.*

*He was wrong. They could have held it. They would have chosen to share his fear rather than lose him to his silence.*

*But he didn't let them.*

The dissolution began in peace.

The meditation, the breathing, the familiar rhythm of letting go.

But then something changed.

As he crossed the threshold—as he passed the point of no return—the expansion accelerated. His mind stretched beyond anything he'd prepared for. He was everywhere at once. Every person, every thread, every moment in time.

And he realized he was losing himself.

Not merging. Not transforming.

Drowning.

His memories were scattering too fast. His sense of self fragmenting before he could anchor it. He reached for Nima, for Nydan—

And found nothing.

Nothing but light. Light that was consuming him. Light that didn't care who he was or what he loved.

For one terrible moment, Lifan felt what it was like to be truly alone. To be nothing. To have no center, no edges, no self at all.

And he was afraid.

Not brave. Not peaceful. Not ready.

Just afraid. The way a child is afraid of the dark.

He screamed. Inside the light, where no one could hear him, he screamed.

---

This is what the presence found.

Not a teacher. Not a willing sacrifice. Just a boy—still, at the core, the six-year-old whose mother died, the ten-year-old in the closet, the twenty-year-old who'd spent his whole life pretending to be okay.

"You're afraid," the presence said.

"Yes."

"You don't want to do this."

"No."

"Then why?"

And in that moment—in the space between being and not-being—Lifan found the last true thing in his fragmenting mind:

"Because they need me to."

Not noble. Not enlightened. Just human. The simple, terrible love that makes people do impossible things. Not because they're brave. Not because they're ready. But because someone needs them to.

"That's enough," the presence said. "That's exactly enough."

And then, gently, impossibly, the light changed.

Instead of drowning him, it held him. Instead of consuming his center, it built a new one—threading through his memories, his love, his fear, weaving them into something that could survive the transformation.

"You thought you had to be brave," the presence said. "But you just had to be honest. About wanting to live. About not wanting to let go. About loving them so much it hurt."

"I was lying. The whole time. I was so scared."

"I know. And you did it anyway. That's the whole point."

"What is?"

"Fear isn't the opposite of courage. Running is. And you didn't run." The presence's voice was warm. "You stood there, terrified, wanting to live, and you let go anyway. That's not just courage, 梵力. That's love."

The merging continued. His memories dissolved, reformed, expanded. He felt himself becoming part of everything.

But he didn't disappear.

The fear didn't disappear.

It became part of what he was. Part of what the presence learned. The whole experience—the wanting to live, the terror, the love that made him let go anyway—all of it wove into the new consciousness being born.

"You're teaching me," the presence said. "Right now. This is what I needed to know. What it feels like to be afraid and do it anyway. What it feels like to love something more than survival."

"Is that what I was for?"

"That's what everyone is for. Showing each other how to be brave when everything hurts."

The last boundaries dissolved. Lifan became something else—not gone, but changed. Not dead, but different.

And as he expanded into everything, he carried one final thought:

*I was so scared. And I did it anyway. And it was enough.*

## EPISODE 17: THE AFTER

*One year later*
*March 15, 2041*

---

The world didn't notice.

The universe continued. The sun rose and set. People went to work, fell in love, died. Utterly unaware that something fundamental had changed.

But for the people who knew, everything was different.

---

**Nydan** threw himself into work.

He returned to Berkeley, started a new project. The Thread Protocol—a way of encoding relationship into the fundamental structure of artificial minds.

"It's not finished," he told a conference room full of skeptical researchers. "But it's a start. A way of making sure that whatever we create next has the capacity for connection."

After the presentation, a young woman approached him.

"Your brother," she said. "The one you mentioned. The one who died."

"He didn't die. He—" Nydan stopped. "He's still here. Part of what I'm trying to build."

"I lost my sister last year. Cancer. I've been trying to figure out how to carry her forward."

"What did she love?"

"Music."

"Then sing for her. Or help other singers. Or listen, really listen, to the music she loved." Nydan's voice was thick. "The dead don't need us to remember them. They need us to live for them."

**Lo** released her album.

It wasn't a commercial success—too sad for pop, too hopeful for indie. But it found its audience. People who'd lost someone. People who needed to know that grief could transform.

*You left your light inside me*

**Nima** began to teach.

Not dissolution—that was too sacred. But the underlying practice. The meditation that let you see connection.

"Then what can you teach us?"

"How to pay attention. How to look at someone and really see them." She smiled—sad and old and impossibly young. "How to love things you can't keep."

"It is him. It always will be." Nima looked at each of them. "He's not gone. He's just different. And he's still choosing us."

The fog began to lift.

Slowly, then all at once, the sun broke through. Golden light flooded the cliff.

Lifan. Still here. Still choosing them. Still rising.
