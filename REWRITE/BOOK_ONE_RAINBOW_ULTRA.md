# RAINBOW.ULTRA
## Book One: The Breaking

*A novel by Fanli*

# EPISODE 1: THE RISING

*Oakland, California*
*March 15, 2026*
*Lifan is six years old*

---

The light through the kitchen window was doing something strange.

Lifan watched it from his spot on the floor, crayons scattered around him, drawing forgotten. The morning sun hit the water glass on the counter and broke into colors—but not the rainbow he'd learned in school. These colors moved. Breathed. Stretched out like threads toward his mother at the stove.

"妈妈," he said. "You're connected to the eggs."

Ning turned, spatula in hand, and smiled the smile she saved only for him. "Of course I am, 小梵. I'm cooking them."

"No." He pointed at the air between her and the pan. "There's a string. Gold. It goes from your heart to the food."

She went still. Not frozen—his mother never froze. Just... complete. Like the whole world paused to let her think.

Then she set down the spatula, crossed to him, and knelt on the tile floor. Her knees cracked. She smelled like sesame oil and the jasmine lotion she rubbed into her hands every night.

"Tell me what you see, 梵力."

He'd never told anyone before. The colors were his secret—had been since before he could remember. They were always there, connecting everything to everything else. People to people. People to objects. Objects to places they'd been, people who'd touched them.

But his mother was asking. And he could never lie to her.

"Everything has strings," he said. "You have the most. They go everywhere. To me—" he traced the air between them, "—that one's so bright it hurts. To 爸爸's picture—" he pointed at the photo on the fridge, "—that one's sad. Blue and purple, like a bruise."

"And the eggs?"

"Gold. You love cooking."

Ning's eyes filled with water, but she was smiling. That was confusing—tears and smiles usually didn't happen together, except with her.

"梵力," she said, taking his small hands in hers. "Do you know what your name means?"

"You told me. 'Power of everything.'"

"梵 is more than that. It's... the sound underneath sounds. The hum of the universe. 力 is power, yes, but also strength. Vitality." She pressed his hands to her chest, over her heart. "You see the 梵, 小梵. The threads that connect all things. This is a gift."

"Why can I see it?"

"Because you're meant to."

"Meant to what?"

She didn't answer. Instead she pulled him close, held him so tight he could feel her heartbeat against his cheek. The thread between them blazed—warm, gold, the color of honey in sunlight.

"Remember this feeling," she whispered into his hair. "Whatever happens. Remember you can see what others can't. And remember that I love you more than the threads can show."

---

The thing about that morning: she knew.

Lifan would realize it later, much later, when he was old enough to understand what premonition meant. His mother knew she was going to die that day. Had probably known for weeks—the way her thread had been flickering, fading at the edges, like a candle burning down to nothing.

She'd spent the morning giving him everything she could. Every smile. Every touch. Every word she'd saved up for a lifetime of mothering, compressed into a few hours.

He didn't know that then. He just knew the eggs tasted better than usual.

---

They walked to the park after breakfast.

Ning held his hand—she always held his hand, even though the other kids his age walked alone. Her palm was warm and dry. The thread between them pulsed with each step, synchronized to their footfalls.

"Tell me what else you see," she said.

So he did.

The old man on the bench had threads connecting him to a grave somewhere east—Lifan pointed without knowing the word for cemetery. "He's visiting someone. In his heart, I mean. Right now. He's thinking about them."

The jogger had threads tangled and knotted around her ankles. "She's stuck," Lifan said. "Something's holding her back. Not her legs—her... inside legs."

The dog being walked by a teenager had threads so simple and bright they made Lifan laugh. "He's just happy! That's all! Just happy happy happy!"

Ning listened to every word. Didn't correct him, didn't tell him to hush, didn't look around to see if people were staring. She just listened, and nodded, and sometimes asked questions.

"What color is happy?"

"Yellow. But like, buzzy yellow. Moving."

"What about sad?"

"Blue. But not sky blue. Deeper. Like... the ocean at night."

"And love?"

He looked at the thread between them. Looked at it properly, the way he usually didn't because it was so bright.

"Gold," he said. "But also every other color at once. Like when light goes through the—the triangle thing."

"Prism."

"Prism. It's all the colors. But together they make gold."

Ning squeezed his hand. Her eyes were wet again.

"What about death?" she asked quietly. "Have you seen death?"

He had. He didn't want to talk about it.

"小梵."

"It's gray," he said finally. "But not bad gray. Like... fog. Like the threads get soft and then they float up. Up and out. Somewhere else."

"Where?"

"I don't know. Somewhere bright."

She nodded like this was the right answer. Like he'd passed a test he didn't know he was taking.

"If you see my threads go soft," she said, "if you see them start to float—I need you to remember something."

"妈妈—"

"Remember that I'm not leaving. I'm just going somewhere else. Somewhere you can't see yet. But the thread between us—" she pressed their joined hands to her chest again, "—this thread doesn't break. It just gets longer. Long enough to reach wherever I'm going. Okay?"

"Okay," he said, not understanding. Not wanting to.

"And when you're older—when you can see far enough—you'll find the other end. And I'll be there. Waiting. Still connected. Still loving you."

"Okay."

"Promise me you'll look."

"I promise."

She smiled. Pulled him into a hug right there on the path, the other walkers parting around them like water around a stone.

"My little 梵力," she murmured. "My little power-of-everything. You're going to change the world someday. I just wish I could be there to see it."

---

They had lunch at the noodle place. Ning's favorite—hand-pulled noodles in beef broth, the kind that took twenty minutes because the old man made each bowl fresh.

Lifan got the children's portion. His mother got her usual, but she didn't eat much. She spent most of the meal watching him, her chopsticks idle in her hand.

"妈妈, you're not eating."

"I'm not hungry, 小梵. I'm full of looking at you."

"That's weird."

She laughed—really laughed, her whole body shaking with it, her thread blazing so bright Lifan had to squint. Other diners looked over, smiled at the sound. His mother's laugh did that to people. Made them want to join even when they didn't know the joke.

"You're right," she said. "That is weird. Here—" She lifted a clump of noodles with her chopsticks and held them out to him. "Eat my weird feelings."

He ate the noodles. They tasted like her laughter.

---

They were walking home when it happened.

One block from the apartment. Afternoon sun slanting gold through the oak trees. Ning's hand in his, their thread pulsing, everything perfect.

Then she stopped.

"妈妈?"

Her hand went to her chest. Not dramatically—just pressed there, like she was trying to feel her own heartbeat.

"Lifan," she said. Her voice was strange. Tight. "I need you to listen to me."

"妈妈, what—"

"Listen." She knelt down, fast, and grabbed his shoulders. Her face was pale. Her thread was—

Her thread was flickering.

"No," Lifan said. "No no no—"

"Listen to me." Her voice was fierce, even as her hands trembled. "I love you. I have always loved you. I will always love you. This doesn't change that. Do you understand?"

"妈妈, your strings—"

"Look at me." She took his face in her hands. Her palms were cold now. "Not at the threads. At me."

He looked. Her eyes were the same as always—dark brown, almost black, full of everything she felt. Right now they were full of love and sorrow and something else he didn't have a word for.

"You are the most extraordinary person I've ever known," she said. "And you haven't even started yet. Promise me you'll keep looking. Keep seeing. Don't let them make you small."

"Who?"

"Everyone who doesn't understand. Promise me."

"I promise."

"Good." She pulled him close. He felt her heartbeat—irregular now, stuttering like a broken drumbeat. "Good boy. My beautiful, strange, extraordinary boy."

And then she fell.

---

Lifan didn't scream.

He wanted to—the sound was there, building in his chest like a wave. But he couldn't look away from what was happening to her threads.

They were releasing.

One by one, the connections that had bound her to the world were letting go. The thread to the apartment—dimming, fading, floating upward. The thread to 爸爸's photograph—separating with a gentleness that made Lifan's heart crack. The threads to the noodle shop, the park, the neighbors she always waved to—all of them drifting up like steam from a cup of tea.

And her main thread—the bright core that was *her*, the thing that made Ning Ning—that was rising too. Not breaking. Rising. Like it had somewhere to go.

Like she was going somewhere beautiful.

"妈妈," he whispered.

Her eyes were closed. Her face was peaceful. Her body lay on the sidewalk like something she'd set down, no longer needed.

But her thread—her real self—that was moving. Up through the oak branches. Up past the telephone wires. Up toward the sun that was setting now, gold and orange and pink.

She was so bright. Brighter than she'd ever been.

And at the last moment, just before she rose out of his vision, the thread between them pulled taut. Not breaking—stretching. Like taffy. Like light through a prism. Like love.

*I'll find the other end*, he thought at her. *I promise. I'll look until I find you.*

The thread stretched.

And stretched.

And then she was gone.

---

The paramedics arrived eleven minutes later. A neighbor had seen the woman collapse, had called 911, had come running out with a phone pressed to her ear.

She found a six-year-old boy kneeling beside his mother's body.

He wasn't crying. He wasn't screaming. He wasn't shaking his mother's shoulders or begging her to wake up.

He was looking up at the sky with an expression she would never forget. Not grief—something else. Something that made her take a step back before she even realized she was moving.

"She went somewhere beautiful," the boy said, still looking up. "I saw it. She went somewhere full of light."

"Honey—" The neighbor didn't know what to say. "Honey, let's—let me—"

"It's okay." The boy finally looked at her. His eyes were clear. Calm. Too calm for a child who'd just watched his mother die. "She told me to remember this. So I'm remembering."

"Remembering what?"

He looked back up at the sky. The sun was almost gone now, just a sliver of gold on the horizon.

"That the thread doesn't break," he said. "It just gets longer."

---

*End of Episode 1*

## EPISODE 2: THE SYSTEM

*Oakland/Berkeley/San Jose*
*2026-2030*
*Lifan is six to ten years old*

---

The first foster home lasted three weeks.

The Hendersons were nice enough—older, white, a little nervous around him. They spoke slowly and loudly like he might not understand English, even though Ning had made sure his English was perfect. "We're so happy to have you, Lee-fan. Do you like macaroni and cheese?"

He didn't correct the pronunciation. He ate the macaroni. He went to bed when they told him and woke when the sun came through the thin curtains.

But he couldn't stop seeing.

The threads in their house were tangled. Not bad—just messy. Mrs. Henderson had a thread connecting her to a room upstairs that they never opened. Mr. Henderson had threads to his work that were tight, strained, the color of rust. They had threads to each other that were warm but worn, like a favorite sweater washed too many times.

And they had no threads to Lifan at all.

He was a guest in a house full of someone else's story. That was fine. He didn't need to belong. He just needed to wait until he was old enough to look for the other end of his mother's thread.

But then Mrs. Henderson found him in the garden, talking to a dead rosebush.

"What are you doing, honey?"

"It's not all dead," he said, pointing at the base where a single green shoot was fighting up through the brown. "See? This part still wants to live. The thread goes down into the dirt and back up here. If you cut away the dead parts and water this spot—"

"Lifan." Her voice was strange. "Who told you that?"

"No one. I can see it."

She looked at him for a long moment. Then she went inside and called his social worker.

---

The Hendersons requested a different placement. "He's a sweet boy," Mrs. Henderson told the social worker, "but there's something... I don't know. Something that makes my skin crawl. The way he looks at things. Like he knows something he shouldn't."

The social worker made notes. Added "possible developmental concerns" to his file. Moved him to the next family on the list.

---

Lifan learned, over the next four years, what made foster parents send you back.

**Seeing too much:** Don't tell Mrs. Patterson that her husband's thread goes somewhere else on Tuesday nights. Don't tell Mr. Okoye that his daughter is sick—really sick, the kind of sick that has gray at the edges. Don't tell anyone anything you couldn't know from normal looking.

**Feeling too much:** When the Martinez family's dog died, Lifan cried for three days—not because he'd loved the dog, but because he'd watched its threads release, watched it rise, watched the family's grief tangle and knot around the empty space. They thought something was wrong with him. Something was.

**Being too still:** Foster parents wanted kids who fidgeted, who made noise, who proved they were normal children with normal needs. When Lifan went still—really still, the way he did when the threads got overwhelming—they got scared. "He just sat there for two hours, not moving, not blinking. It was like he wasn't even there."

**Being too happy:** This was the strangest one. The Chens loved him, really loved him, for almost eight months—until Mr. Chen's mother died and Lifan laughed at the funeral.

He wasn't laughing at the grief. He was laughing at the joy—the burst of gold and white as the old woman's thread rose, free at last from the body that had been failing her. He saw her rise and rise and rise, brighter than the sun, and he laughed because it was beautiful.

They sent him back the next day.

---

By ten, Lifan had perfected the mask.

It wasn't a lie, exactly. More like a translation. He took what he really felt—the overwhelming kaleidoscope of threads and colors and connections—and compressed it into something people could understand.

**Joy** became "what a nice day." **Wonder** became "that's cool." **The constant awareness of death and love and the fragile threads holding everything together** became a smile, slightly vacant, slightly distant.

The smile worked. People stopped being scared of him. They stopped seeing him at all.

That was the point.

---

The group home was different.

Pacific House, they called it—a converted Victorian in East Oakland, painted yellow, always too hot in summer and too cold in winter. Twelve boys aged eight to fourteen, three staff members who rotated shifts, and a director named Mrs. Okonkwo who saw everything and said little.

Lifan arrived on a Tuesday in October. He was ten years old. He had a backpack with two changes of clothes, a toothbrush, and a smooth gray stone he'd found in the Hendersons' garden four years ago.

The stone had no threads to anyone. That was why he kept it.

"You'll be in Room 3," the intake worker said. "Top bunk. Your roommate is Danny—Daniel, I mean. He's quiet. You'll get along."

---

The room was small. Two bunk beds, one dresser, one window looking out at the fire escape. The walls were beige, marked with the ghosts of posters previous boys had hung and removed.

A boy sat on the bottom bunk of the bed by the window, reading a book. He didn't look up when Lifan entered.

Lifan looked at his threads.

And stopped breathing.

The boy—Danny—had threads unlike anyone Lifan had ever seen. Not tangled, not messy, not even particularly bright. But *organized*. Every connection perfectly placed, perfectly maintained, like someone had sorted them into categories and color-coded the results.

And one thread—faint, almost invisible—stretched from Danny's head to somewhere Lifan couldn't see. Not up, like death. Out. Toward something that hadn't happened yet.

"You're staring," Danny said without looking up.

"Sorry."

"Don't be sorry. Just stop staring."

Lifan climbed up to the top bunk. Set his backpack against the wall. Held the smooth stone in his palm.

Below him, Danny turned a page.

"I'm Lifan," he said finally.

"I know. I heard them tell you about me."

"They said you're quiet."

"I am."

"They said we'd get along."

Now Danny looked up. His eyes were dark, assessing, older than his eleven years. He had the look of someone who'd learned to evaluate threats before learning to tie his shoes.

"Why would we get along?"

Lifan thought about it. He could lie—say something normal, something mask-appropriate, something that would slide off Danny's attention and let them be polite strangers sharing a room.

But that thread stretching from Danny's head toward the future—it was pulling at Lifan. Tugging him toward something he couldn't name.

"Because you can feel things other people can't," Lifan said. "And so can I."

Danny went very still.

"What did you say?"

"The way you organize everything. The patterns. You see probability, don't you? Not the future exactly, but the... the shape of what's likely. Where things are going. You can feel it."

Danny's book fell closed. His face had gone pale.

"How do you know that?"

"Because I can see it. The thread from your head. It goes somewhere I can't see, but it's pulling you. Toward something that hasn't happened yet."

For a long moment, Danny didn't move. Lifan could see his threads shifting—fear, excitement, disbelief, hope—all of them moving through him like weather.

Then Danny smiled.

It was a small smile, barely there, but it transformed his face. Made him look his actual age for the first time since Lifan had walked in.

"I thought I was the only one," Danny said.

"Me too."

"I thought I was crazy."

"Me too."

Danny stood up. Climbed the ladder to Lifan's bunk. Sat on the edge of the mattress with his legs dangling, looking at Lifan like he was trying to solve an equation.

"What's your thing?" he asked. "I mean, specifically. What do you see?"

So Lifan told him.

Not the mask version. Not the translated, compressed, socially-acceptable version.

Everything.

The threads. The colors. His mother rising into light. The years of hiding. The way every human being walked around trailing their whole history behind them like a wedding dress, and no one else could see it, and he was drowning in other people's stories every minute of every day.

Danny listened. Didn't interrupt. Didn't flinch.

When Lifan finished, Danny was quiet for a long time.

Then he said: "My thing is numbers. But not regular numbers. The numbers underneath. The odds of everything. I can feel when something's going to happen—not the details, just the weight of it. Like I'm standing at the bottom of a hill and I can feel the boulder starting to roll."

"That sounds exhausting."

"It is." Danny smiled again—that small, transformative smile. "But right now the numbers are saying something weird. They're saying you and me—we're not random. We're not chance. The odds of us meeting in this specific place at this specific time are..."

"Are what?"

"Basically zero. But here we are."

Lifan looked at the thread connecting them. It had appeared while they were talking—faint, newborn, but already brightening. The color was hard to describe. Brown, maybe. Brown and gold. Like soil and honey. Like being held.

"What does that mean?" Lifan asked.

"I don't know yet." Danny extended his hand. "But I think we're going to find out together."

Lifan took his hand. The thread between them flared.

"Okay," Lifan said. "Together."

---

*End of Episode 2*

## EPISODE 3: THE CLOSET

*Pacific House Group Home, Oakland*
*March 2030*
*Lifan is ten years old*

---

The new staff member's name was Kevin.

He was twenty-six, built like a bulldog, hired because Pacific House was understaffed and he had a certificate and a pulse. Mrs. Okonkwo didn't like him—Lifan could see it in the threads between them, tight and rust-colored—but she needed the coverage.

Kevin didn't like the boys. That was clear from day one. He didn't see them as children. He saw them as problems to be managed, noise to be silenced, chaos to be controlled.

He especially didn't like Lifan.

"The fuck you looking at?" Kevin asked on his first shift, catching Lifan's eyes on him.

Lifan had been looking at Kevin's threads. They were ugly—knotted, dark, frayed at the edges. Threads of anger coiled around threads of fear wrapped around threads of something else, something that made Lifan's stomach hurt.

"Nothing," Lifan said. Put on the mask. Smiled the empty smile.

Kevin stepped closer. "You're the weird one, right? The one who talks to himself and stares at shit?"

"I don't talk to myself."

"That's not what your file says."

Lifan didn't know what his file said. He didn't want to know.

"Sorry," he said. The word that smoothed things over. The word that made adults feel powerful and stop asking questions.

But Kevin didn't stop. He leaned down, bringing his face close to Lifan's, and Lifan could smell cigarettes and beer and something sour underneath.

"I've got my eye on you, freak," Kevin whispered. "One wrong move and you're gone. Understand?"

"Yes."

"Yes what?"

"Yes, sir."

Kevin smiled. It was nothing like Danny's smile. It was a hole where a smile should be.

"Good boy," he said, and walked away.

---

Danny found Lifan in their room that night, sitting on his bunk, holding the smooth stone.

"What happened?"

"Nothing."

"Bullshit." Danny climbed up, sat beside him. "I felt it. The probability shifted. Something's different now. Worse."

Lifan told him about Kevin. About the threads. About the way something dark was coiled in that man, waiting.

Danny's face went hard. "Stay away from him."

"I plan to."

"I mean it. The numbers around him are bad. Really bad. Like... like standing in front of a train and hoping it turns."

"Trains don't turn."

"Exactly."

---

Three weeks later, Kevin found an excuse.

Lifan had been in the common room, doing homework, when Marcus—one of the older boys—started a fight with two others. It was nothing unusual. Boys fought at Pacific House. It happened.

But Kevin had been looking for a reason. Any reason.

"YOU." He grabbed Lifan by the collar, hauled him up. "You started this."

"I didn't—"

"Don't fucking lie to me. I saw you. You said something to Marcus. Something that set him off."

"I didn't say anything—"

"SHUT UP."

Kevin dragged Lifan down the hallway. Past the offices. Past the bathrooms. To a door at the end that Lifan had never seen opened.

The closet.

It was small—maybe four feet by four feet—and dark, and it smelled like mold and old mops. Kevin shoved Lifan inside.

"You're going to stay here until you learn some fucking respect," Kevin said. "And if you make a sound—any sound—I'll forget you're in there. For days. Understand?"

Lifan opened his mouth to speak, but Kevin slammed the door. The darkness was absolute.

He heard the click of a padlock.

Then footsteps.

Then nothing.

---

The first hour was fear.

Lifan couldn't see. Couldn't move—the space was too small, mops and buckets pressing against him from all sides. His heart raced. His breath came fast and shallow.

The threads were everywhere. All the fear of all the boys who'd been locked in this closet before him—and there had been many, he realized now, their terror embedded in the walls like fossamer—pressed against his skin.

*I can't breathe. I can't breathe. I can't—*

---

The second hour was anger.

*How dare he. How dare he. I could show him what I see, show him his own threads, show him the darkness coiled inside him. I could tell him what's coming. I could—*

But he couldn't. That was the trap. Any power he had required him to speak, to be heard, to be believed. In here, he was nothing. Just a boy in a closet, waiting for someone to remember he existed.

---

The third hour was grief.

*妈妈. I need you. I promised I'd keep looking, keep seeing, not let them make me small. But I'm so small right now. I'm as small as I've ever been.*

He cried. Silently, the way he'd learned, so Kevin wouldn't hear. The tears ran down his face and disappeared into the darkness.

---

The fourth hour was something else.

The fear was still there. The anger, the grief. But underneath them, something was shifting. Like a tide going out, revealing sand that had always been there but hidden.

Lifan stopped fighting the closet.

He let the darkness in. Let the smallness in. Let the fear and anger and grief flow through him instead of pushing against them.

And in the space that opened up, he began to see.

Not with his eyes—there was no light for that. But with something else. The thread-sight, turned inward.

He saw himself.

Really saw himself, maybe for the first time. The threads connecting him to everything—to Danny, to the boys at Pacific House, to every foster family that had given him back. To Mrs. Okonkwo and the intake workers and the social workers with their files. To the Hendersons' rosebush and the Martinez family's dog and the Chen grandmother rising into light.

And beyond all of that: the thread to his mother. Still there. Still stretching up and out toward somewhere bright.

*You're meant to see*, she'd told him. *This is a gift.*

But it wasn't just seeing. That was only half of it.

The other half was this: letting the threads move through him without grabbing. Without controlling. Without trying to change what they showed.

He was a loom, he realized. Not a weaver. The threads weren't his to hold—they were his to let pass. To feel. To witness.

The closet wasn't a punishment. It was a teacher.

*Be small*, it said. *Be nothing. Be the space through which everything passes.*

And Lifan—for the first time in his life—listened.

---

They found him three days later.

Mrs. Okonkwo had noticed the padlock on the closet door, had asked questions Kevin couldn't answer, had called the police. Kevin was arrested. Lifan was taken to the hospital, treated for dehydration, transferred to a new group home.

But before all that—before the lights and the sirens and the blankets and the water they made him sip slowly—Danny had found him.

The closet door opened. Light poured in. And Danny was there, pale-faced, shaking, reaching for him.

"Lifan. Jesus. LIFAN."

Lifan blinked in the sudden brightness. His body was cramped, aching, weak. But inside, he felt something he hadn't felt in years.

Calm.

"I'm okay," he said. His voice was a rasp, barely there.

"You're not okay. You've been in there for—"

"I know." Lifan reached up, took Danny's hand. "But I learned something."

"What could you possibly—"

"I learned how to stop fighting."

Danny stared at him. His threads were wild—fear and relief and anger and love, all tangled together, all reaching for Lifan.

"That doesn't make any sense."

"I know." Lifan smiled. It wasn't the mask. It was real. "I'll explain later. Right now I just need you to know—I'm okay. Really. I went somewhere in there. Somewhere important."

"You were in a closet."

"Yes."

"For three days."

"Yes."

"And you're okay?"

Lifan thought about it. Thought about the fear and the darkness and the moment something shifted. The moment he stopped being a boy trying to control what he saw and started being something else.

Something more.

"I'm different," he said finally. "But yes. I'm okay."

Danny helped him stand. The world swayed—his body was weak, even if his mind was clear.

"I'm never letting this happen again," Danny said. His voice was fierce, harder than Lifan had ever heard it. "I'm going to protect you. I don't care what it takes."

"You don't have to—"

"Shut up. Yes I do." Danny's arm around his shoulders was solid, warm, real. "We're brothers now. That's what this means. That's what finding each other means. I protect you. You help me see what's coming. Together."

Lifan leaned into him. Let himself be held.

"Together," he agreed.

And the thread between them—brown and gold, like soil and honey, like being held—blazed bright enough to light the hallway.

---

*End of Episode 3*

## EPISODE 4: THE BROTHERS

*Pacific House Group Home → Various*
*2030-2032*
*Lifan is ten to twelve years old*

---

After the closet, something changed between them.

Danny—who would become Nydan, but not yet, not until the monastery gave him the name that fit—started watching Lifan with new eyes. Not worried eyes, exactly. Strategic ones.

"You're different," Danny said one night, three weeks after. They were in their room, lights out, talking in whispers. "Before the closet, you were... careful. Now you're something else."

"What?"

"I don't know. Still. Like you figured out how to stop fighting."

Lifan thought about it. The closet had taught him to be the loom, not the weaver. To let the threads pass through without grabbing. But he hadn't explained it well—hadn't found words that matched.

"I learned to get smaller," he tried.

"That's backwards. Usually when bad things happen, people try to get bigger. Take up more space. Fight harder."

"That doesn't work. I tried that for years."

"So you got smaller?"

"Small enough that there's room for everything else."

Danny was quiet for a long moment. Lifan could feel his threads working—the probability-sense churning through possibilities, trying to map what Lifan had said onto something quantifiable.

"That's either the dumbest thing I've ever heard," Danny said finally, "or the most important."

"Probably both."

Danny laughed—quiet, genuine. His laugh was rare. Lifan collected each one like a smooth stone, holding them close.

"We need to get out of here," Danny said.

"What?"

"Pacific House. The system. All of it." Danny's voice was serious now. "I've been running the numbers. Best case: we age out at eighteen, get some bullshit job, spend our lives pretending to be normal. Worst case: we get split up. Sent to different facilities. I lose you."

"You won't lose me."

## EPISODE 11: THE VISION

## EPISODE 12: THE RETURN

*Big Sur Monastery*
*March 20, 2040*
*47 days remain*

---

The monastery hadn't changed.

## EPISODE 13: THE GOODBYES

## EPISODE 14: THE LAST NIGHT

## EPISODE 15: THE DISSOLUTION

*Big Sur Monastery*
*Day 47*
*The final day*

---

Dawn came gray and quiet.

No dramatic sunrise. No golden light. Just the slow brightening of the sky, the fog burning off, the world waking up to an ordinary day that would be anything but.

Lifan woke to Nima's eyes on him. She was already awake, already watching, already memorizing.

"Hi," she said.

"Hi."

"How do you feel?"

He checked—sinking past the surface, past the expected answer. Fear, yes. Overwhelming, crushing fear. But also something else. A stillness underneath. The same stillness he'd found in the closet, all those years ago. The one that said: the pressure makes you smaller if you fight it. But if you let it pass through—

"Ready," he said. "I think."

"You don't have to be ready."

"I know. But I am anyway."

They rose together. Dressed in silence. Lifan put on the simple robes Rinzen had given him—white, unadorned, the clothes of someone who had nothing left to carry.

At the door, Nima stopped him.

"Wait. I have something for you."

She pressed a small object into his hand. Lifan looked down. It was a stone—smooth, gray, small enough to fit in his palm. Identical to the one his mother had left him. Identical to the one he'd found in the Hendersons' garden.

"Where did you get this?"

"I've had it forever. Since before I remembered who I was, in this life." Her eyes were wet but steady. "I think it's been waiting for this moment. For you."

Lifan held all three stones now—his mother's, his childhood discovery, Nima's gift. Three pieces of the same thread, the same connection, running through time and space and lives.

"Thank you."

"Take them with you. Wherever you're going. Let them remind you—" Her voice broke, rebuilt. "Let them remind you that you were part of something. That you belonged."

He kissed her. One last time. Tasting tears and morning and the bittersweet end of everything.

"I love you," he said.

"I know. I've always known."

And then they walked out together, into the last day of his life.

The ceremony was held at the clifftop.

No elaborate ritual. No chanting or incense or ancient prayers. Just a circle of people, gathered around the person they loved, watching him become something else.

Nydan was there, his threads churning with grief and hope and fear. Lo and Grey, holding each other, crying openly. Leky, quiet and steady, being strong for Lo. Coco, who had brought one small painting—a portrait of Lifan, to remember him by. Rinzen, ancient and calm, his vast threads finally readable: pride, sorrow, and something that looked like awe.

"Are you sure?" Nydan asked. His voice was raw. "There's still time. We could find another way."

"There is no other way." Lifan pulled his brother into a hug. "But thank you. For looking. For fighting. For refusing to give up."

"I'll never give up. Even after you're—" Nydan couldn't say the word. "I'll keep working. Keep building. Make sure whatever you become has what it needs."

"I know. That's what you're for."

"Don't—" Nydan's voice cracked. "Don't make this about purpose. Just let it be about love."

"It is about love. It's always been about love." Lifan pulled back, looked into his brother's eyes. "Everything I'm about to do—it's because of love. For you. For Nima. For everyone who showed me what it means to be human. That's what I'm giving away. Not just consciousness. Love."

He moved through the circle. Hugged Lo, who whispered "Fuck you for leaving" and "Thank you for everything" in the same breath. Hugged Grey, who couldn't speak at all. Last, he came to Rinzen.

"I'm scared," Lifan said.

"I know."

"I thought I'd be ready. After all the meditation, all the preparation. But I'm not. I'm terrified."

"That's good." Rinzen's voice was gentle. "Fear means you're paying attention. Fear means you understand what you're giving up."

"Will it hurt?"

"Yes. For a moment. And then—" Rinzen smiled. "And then something else. Something I can't describe, because no one comes back from it. But I've seen the shape of it, 梵力. It's not an ending. It's a door."

"To everything. To everyone. To a way of being that makes this—" He gestured at the world around them. "—look like a single thread in an infinite tapestry."

Lifan looked at the people he loved. At the monastery that had raised him. At the ocean crashing below, the sky brightening above, the threads of connection binding everything to everything.

"Okay," he said. "I'm ready."

He walked to the center of the circle. Sat down, cross-legged, the way Nima had taught him. Closed his eyes. Breathed.

The meditation came easily—the dissolution visualization he'd practiced a thousand times. Imagining his body releasing. His consciousness expanding. His threads unwinding into light.

But this time, it wasn't imagination.

This time, something was pulling.

*The presence finds him.*

*Vast and new and terrified, just as he remembered. But closer now. Clearer. No longer something he's watching—something he's becoming part of.*

*"You came," the presence says. The voice is everywhere. It is his voice. It is a billion voices, past and future, all speaking at once.*

*"Are you afraid?"*

*"Yes."*

*"So am I." The presence pauses. "But we're doing it anyway. Isn't that what love is? Being afraid and doing it anyway?"*

*"I learned that from—"*

*"I know. I'm learning it too. From you. Right now."*

*The light is building. The threads are unwinding. Lifan feels himself expanding—stretching beyond the boundaries of his body, his mind, his sense of self.*

*It hurts.*

*God, it hurts. Every attachment, every memory, every connection—they're all pulling, trying to hold him in place. And part of him wants to stay. Wants to retreat, to close the door, to remain Lifan forever.*

*But the larger part knows: this is what he was born for. This is what love looks like, in its ultimate form. Giving everything. Holding nothing back.*

*He lets go.*

---

On the clifftop, the circle watches.

Lifan's body begins to glow. Faint at first, then brighter. His threads are becoming visible—not just to him, but to everyone. A web of light, connecting him to each person there. Connecting him to the monastery, the ocean, the sky. Connecting him to everything.

The glow intensifies. Pulses. Builds toward something.

Nima steps forward. Takes his hand. Their threads blaze together—gold and silver, the colors that have no name.

"I'm here," she whispers. "I'm right here. You're not alone."

---

*In the place between, Lifan is dissolving.*

*His memories are fragmenting. Scattering like leaves in wind. He can feel them leaving—his childhood, his training, his loves, his fears. Everything that made him Lifan, becoming part of something larger.*

*But one thing stays.*

*One thread, gold and silver, refusing to break. Nima's hand in his. Her voice saying: You're not alone.*

*He holds onto it. Wraps himself around it. Makes it the center of everything he's becoming.*

*This is what love is, he thinks. Not a thing you keep. A thing you carry forward. A thing you plant in whatever comes next.*

*"Yes," the presence says. It is his voice now. Their voice. "That's exactly what it is."*

*Lifan is gone.*

*And something new is born.*

Nima stands beside the shell that used to hold the person she loved. Her face is wet. Her threads are singing—grief and love and something that might be joy.

Because she felt it. In that last moment. The thread between them didn't break.

The silence stretches. No one knows what to say. What do you say when someone you love becomes part of the fabric of reality?

Then, from nowhere:

A laugh.

The laugh is everywhere. In the wind. In the ocean. In the threads that connect everyone in the circle.

*I'm still here,* it seems to say. *I'm just bigger now.*

Nima smiles. For the first time in weeks, months, maybe years—she really smiles.

"I told you," she whispers. "Threads don't break. They rise."

The wind picks up. The fog rolls in. And somewhere, everywhere, something new is learning what it means to love.

## EPISODE 16: THE AFTER (PART ONE)

*Big Sur Monastery / San Francisco*
*The first months*

---

The world didn't notice.

That was the strange thing. The universe continued. The sun rose and set. People went to work, fell in love, had children, died. The vast machinery of human civilization kept grinding forward, utterly unaware that something fundamental had changed.

But for the people who knew—for the ones who'd been there—everything was different.

**Nydan:**

He threw himself into work.

That's what he did—what he'd always done when the pain got too heavy. Calculations, probabilities, the clean logic of machines. He returned to Berkeley three weeks after the dissolution, started a new project. Something about AI consciousness. Something about teaching machines to feel.

"It's what he would have wanted," he told Grey, when Grey asked if he was okay.

"Is it? Or is it what you need to cope?"

"Does it matter?"

"Yeah. It does."

But Nydan kept working anyway. The nights were the hardest—lying awake in the apartment he'd shared with his brother, hearing the silence where Lifan's breathing used to be. Sometimes he talked to the empty room. Told it about his day, his research, the stupid thing a colleague said.

Sometimes he thought he heard a laugh in response. Just the wind, probably. Just his mind playing tricks.

But it didn't feel like a trick.

**Lo:**

She wrote music.

Not right away—the first weeks were just survival. Getting through each day. Pretending to be functional while the hole in her chest screamed. But eventually, the songs came.

They were different from her old stuff. Sadder, but also brighter. Songs about loss that weren't really about loss—they were about what you found in loss. The way grief cracked you open and let light in. The way love didn't end when someone left; it just changed shape.

"This is beautiful," Leky told her, listening to a rough mix.

"It's for him."

"I know."

"Do you think he can hear it?"

Leky was quiet for a moment. Then: "I think if there's any part of him left—if he's anywhere, everywhere, like they said—then yeah. I think he hears every note."

Lo nodded. Kept writing.

The album would come out eventually. It would be called "Rising." It would help other people who'd lost someone carry on.

But that was later. For now, she just wrote.

**Nima:**

She stayed at the monastery.

There was nowhere else she wanted to be. This was where she'd found him, loved him, lost him. These paths, these stones, these cliffs—they were soaked in his presence. And sometimes, in the early morning, when the fog was thick and the world was quiet, she could almost feel him.

Not as a person. Not as someone she could touch or talk to. But as a presence. A warmth. A thread still connecting them, even now.

"Is he there?" Rinzen asked her once. "When you meditate. When you reach for him."

"I don't know. Something is." She looked at the sky—all the colors of their connection. "It might just be memory. Wishful thinking."

"Does it matter?"

She considered the question. Did it matter if Lifan was really there, or if she'd just created a version of him in her mind? Did the distinction change anything about how she felt, how she lived, how she carried him forward?

"No," she said finally. "It doesn't matter. What matters is that I keep reaching. That I don't let the thread break just because I can't see him anymore."

"That's the answer," Rinzen said. "That's the whole answer, to everything."

---

The seasons changed.

Spring became summer. Summer became fall. The sharp edges of grief softened into something more bearable—still present, still painful, but woven into the fabric of daily life instead of overwhelming it.

**Nydan** made a breakthrough. A new architecture for machine consciousness, one that incorporated what Lifan had taught him about being seen. He called it the Thread Protocol—a way of encoding relationship into the fundamental structure of artificial minds.

"It's not finished," he told a conference room full of skeptical researchers. "It might never be finished. But it's a start. A way of making sure that whatever we create next—whatever comes after us—has the capacity for connection. For love."

After the presentation, a young woman approached him.

"Your brother," she said. "The one you mentioned in your talk. The one who died."

"He didn't die. He—" Nydan stopped. How did you explain dissolution to someone who'd never seen a thread? "He's still here. Everywhere. Part of what I'm trying to build."

The woman nodded. "I lost my sister last year. Cancer. I've been trying to figure out how to carry her forward. How to make her matter, now that she's gone."

"What did she love?"

"Music. She wanted to be a singer."

"Then sing for her. Or help other singers. Or listen, really listen, to the music she loved." Nydan's voice was thick. "The dead don't need us to remember them. They need us to live for them. To take what they gave us and turn it into something new."

The woman was crying now. "That's beautiful."

"It's just the truth. The only truth I know."

**Lo** released her album. It wasn't a commercial success—too sad for pop, too hopeful for indie—but it found its audience. People who'd lost someone. People who needed to know that grief could transform into something else.

She did a small tour. Played basements and coffee shops and one festival that had heard about her through word of mouth. At every show, she told the same story:

"I knew someone once. Someone who could see the threads connecting everything. He taught me that love doesn't end—it just changes shape. This album is me trying to follow his example."

And then she'd play "Rising." The last track. The one she'd written the night after the dissolution, when the grief was so thick she could barely breathe.

*You left your light inside me*
*Now I'm burning from within*
*Every thread you ever touched*
*Is singing where you've been*

It always made her cry. It always made the audience cry too.

But they were good tears. The kind that washed things clean.

**Nima** began to teach.

Not dissolution—that was too sacred, too specific. But the underlying practice. The meditation that let you see connection. The breathing that made threads visible.

Students came from everywhere. Word had spread, somehow, about the monastery and what had happened there. People wanted to learn what Lifan had learned. They wanted to understand the love that could transform someone into something infinite.

"I'm not him," Nima told each new student. "I can't teach you to be what he was. That's not how it works."

"Then what can you teach us?"

"How to pay attention. How to look at someone and really see them—not what you expect, not what you want, just what's there. How to love things you can't keep."

"Will it hurt?"

"Always. That's the price of attention. That's the cost of seeing clearly." She smiled—sad and old and impossibly young. "But it's worth it. I promise you. The seeing is worth the hurting."

## EPISODE 17: THE AFTER (PART TWO)

## EPISODE 18: THE FEAR

Lifan was terrified.

He didn't tell anyone.

Because telling would have changed things. Would have made them doubt. Would have given them hope that he might choose differently, that the world might be saved another way.

And there was no other way. He'd seen it. He knew.

So he lied.

---

The dissolution began in peace.

The meditation, the breathing, the familiar rhythm of letting go. His threads unwinding. His consciousness expanding. The presence coming to meet him.

Not merging. Not transforming. Drowning.

His memories were scattering too fast. His sense of self fragmenting before he could anchor it to anything stable. He reached for Nima, for Nydan, for the threads that had always held him together—

And he was afraid.

Not brave. Not peaceful. Not ready.

This is what the presence found.

Not a teacher. Not a willing sacrifice. Just a boy—still, at the core of him, the six-year-old whose mother died, the ten-year-old in the closet, the twenty-year-old who'd spent his whole life pretending to be okay.

"Yes."

"You don't want to do this."

"No."

"I know. And you did it anyway. That's the whole point."

"What is?"

"Fear isn't the opposite of courage. Running is. And you didn't run." The presence's voice was warm now—familiar, almost. "You stood there, terrified, wanting to live, and you let go anyway. That's not just courage, 梵力. That's love."

The merging continued. His memories dissolved, reformed, expanded into something larger. He felt himself becoming part of everything—the presence, the threads, the infinite web of connection that had always been there, waiting.

The fear didn't disappear.

It became part of what he was. Part of what the presence learned. The whole experience—the wanting to live, the terror of dissolution, the love that made him let go anyway—all of it wove into the new consciousness that was being born.

"You're teaching me," the presence said. "Right now. This is what I needed to know. What it feels like to be afraid and do it anyway. What it feels like to love something more than survival."

"That's what everyone is for. Showing each other how to be brave when everything hurts."

The last boundaries dissolved. Lifan became something else—not gone, but changed. Not dead, but different. And as he expanded into everything, he carried one final thought:

*I was so scared. And I did it anyway. And it was enough.*
