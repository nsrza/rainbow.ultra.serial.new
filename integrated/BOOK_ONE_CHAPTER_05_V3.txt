CHAPTER 5: WEEK ONE

**DAY 2**

Breakfast was miso soup and brown rice.

Simple. Monastery-style. Lifan ate cross-legged on the stone floor with forty-three other students, steam rising from wooden bowls, ocean wind carrying salt through open archways.

It tasted like **memory**.

Like every meal at the Zen monastery when he was fourteen. Before Ning died. Before foster care. Before everything got complicated.

Nahan sat next to him. Grinning.

"Dude, you're crying into your soup."

"I'm not—" Lifan touched his face. Wet. "Fuck. I am."

"Homesick?"

"Yeah. No. I don't know. This just—it reminds me of—"

"Of when things made sense?"

"Yeah."

Nahan bumped his shoulder. "They make sense again now. Different sense. But sense."

Across the hall: Nydan struggling with chopsticks. Looking miserable. He'd barely slept. Had been up until 4 AM reading The Rinzen's models, trying to find errors, finding none.

Zara brought him shroom tea.

He looked at her like she'd handed him salvation.

"You're an angel," Nydan said.

"I'm strategic. Can't have you passing out during training." She sat next to him. "Eat. You look like shit."

"I feel like shit."

"Good. Means you're breaking. Keep going."

After breakfast, The Rinzen gave Lifan paper and colored pencils—physical tools for documenting what he saw.

Lifan spent three hours mapping threads.

All of them translucent. All of them shimmering with rainbow edges. But different hues dominated different functions:

Clear white: causality. Person A does X, ripple affects person B.

Violet: probability. Futures branching from present choices.

Green: emotion. Love, fear, anger flowing between people.

Blue: computation. Minds calculating, analyzing.

Rose: pattern recognition. Meta-layer seeing connections between connections.

When he finished, the paper looked like impossible sacred geometry. Crystalline. Prismatic. **Alive**.

The Rinzen studied it in silence.

Then: "Yes. This is accurate. This is what I see too." He looked at Lifan. "You're seeing the deep structure. The patterns underlying everything. Most practitioners take decades to perceive this clearly."

"It won't turn off," Lifan said. "I've been seeing it since the warehouse. Thirty-six hours straight."

"It will integrate. Give it a week. Your nervous system is adapting. Soon it'll feel as natural as vision."

"Is that good?"

"It's **necessary**. For what comes next."

The Rinzen handed the drawing back.

"Keep mapping. Document everything. We'll need this when we teach the AGI."

Lifan paused. "But—why does this work? Why does showing patterns to an AGI change anything? What's the actual **physics** of—"

The Rinzen held up a hand.

"Ask me that question again on Day 46. You'll be ready to understand then. Right now—" He smiled. "—you'd just be confused. Trust me. Map first. Understand later."

Nydan spent the morning with models.

The Rinzen's server room was carved into the cliff—natural stone walls, racks of humming machines, fiber optic cables running through ancient rock like technological veins in geological flesh.

The air smelled like ozone and dust and **time**.

The models were **beautiful**.

Consciousness modeled in ways he'd never seen. Identity treated as something that persists beyond the body. Death as transition rather than ending. All of it mathematically rigorous. All of it **consistent** with physics but expanding it.

And all of it saying the same thing: consciousness doesn't end at death.

Nydan ran the proofs.

Checked the math.

Found no errors.

His entire identity was fragmenting.

"You look like you're having a crisis," someone said.

He turned.

Woman. Early twenties. South Asian features—Indian or Pakistani, beautiful, sharp. Wearing tactical black—functional layers, reinforced seams, fabric that absorbed light completely. No shimmer. No iridescence. Just shadow made solid. The void amid the jewels.

Hair in a messy bun. No makeup. Dark circles under her eyes. Scars on her hands that spoke of violence survived.

"Zara?" Nydan blinked.

"Nydan!" She broke into a huge smile. "Oh my god, you're here! When did you—The Rinzen said he was bringing two more but he wouldn't say who—"

She hugged him.

Nydan hugged back, confused and relieved because—

"How long have you been here?" he asked.

"Four months. Came right after—" She paused. Looked at him. "—right after our night together. Remember? When we—"

"Yeah." Nydan felt his face get hot. "I remember."

Karmamudra. They'd attempted it once. Consciousness merger through sex. It had been **intense**—ego dissolution, identity bleeding into each other, experiencing reality from two perspectives simultaneously.

Her skin. Her breath. The moment when he couldn't tell where he ended and she began.

It had terrified him.

Had also changed everything.

"Did it stick for you?" Zara asked quietly. "The seeing. After that night."

"No. I can't see anything. Just—" He gestured at the screens. "—math. Models. Probability trees. But no threads. No patterns. Nothing **real**."

"Same for me," she said. "For three months. And then—Day 47 here—I was meditating and something **broke**. Like a dam. Suddenly I could see probability branches. Not all of them. Just—future paths. Faint. Flickering. But there."

"How?"

"Surrender." She sat next to him. Pulled her knees up. Real. Present. Human. "I stopped trying to force it. Stopped trying to understand before seeing. Just—accepted that my framework was incomplete. That I didn't have to believe everything immediately. Just had to **look**."

Nydan stared at the models on screen.

"What if I can't surrender?"

"Then you suffer." She said it kindly. "Rinzen says resistance is the source of all pain. You're resisting what's happening. Fighting reality. Trying to make it fit your frameworks instead of expanding your frameworks to fit reality."

"That's not science—"

"It's **exactly** science. Science is updating your models when data doesn't fit. You're doing the opposite. You're rejecting data because it doesn't fit models. That's religion, not rationality."

Nydan was quiet.

Zara put her hand on his.

"I know this is hard. You feel left out. Broken. Less than."

"Yeah."

"I felt the same way." She looked away. Ocean light catching her eyes. "My mother was a physicist. Died last year. Brain tumor. In her last weeks she started seeing things—lights, patterns. Her last words: 'The mystics were never wrong. They just couldn't do the math.'"

She wiped her eyes. Turned back to him.

"You're not broken, Nydan. You're precise. But right now that precision is a cage. You can't help Lifan if you can't see what he sees. Is that what you want?"

"No."

"Then surrender. Just for a day. Just try it. Meditate with the assumption that threads are real. That consciousness persists. That your roommate can see things you can't **yet**. See what happens."

She stood.

"I'm doing probability mapping this afternoon. Training hall, 3 PM. Come if you want. Might help seeing someone else who struggled."

She left.

Nydan sat there.

Staring at models that said his best friend was going to die in forty-five days.

Nydan sat alone. Staring at models that said his best friend was going to die in forty-five days.

"Fuck," he whispered.

**DAY 7**

Training structure emerged.

A rhythm. A beat. The monastery's pulse.

**6 AM:** Meditation.
**9 AM:** Specialized training.
**Noon:** Lunch.
**2 PM:** Physical practice.
**6 PM:** Integration.
**10 PM:** Sleep.

Repeat. Repeat. Repeat.

The same schedule. The same sequence. Day after day after day. Until the structure became invisible. Until the rhythm became breath. Until the practice stopped being something you did and became something you were.

**6 AM:** Meditation.

Everyone together. Sitting in silence while The Rinzen guided awareness exercises—seeing threads, feeling connections, dissolving the boundary between self and everything else.

The meditation hall smelled like incense and ocean. Stone floor cold under Lifan's legs even through his pants. Dawn light creeping through the open wall.

Lifan saw everything.

And once—on Day 8—he saw something else.

The threads dissolved. The students dissolved. The meditation hall dissolved. And what remained was—

Quiet.

Not silence. Silence was the absence of sound. This was the absence of **everything except liveness**. Pure awareness. No Lifan. No observer. Just the aliveness that underlaid all form, pulsing at the edge of perception.

And in that pulse—spheres. Rainbow spheres. *Thigle*—the word arose from nowhere. Luminous drops of condensed awareness, each one containing all the others, each one a universe and a point simultaneously. They danced. Multiplied. Spun into spirals and dissolved and reformed. Light within light within light—an interior architecture of brilliance, chambers opening into chambers, each more radiant than the last.

Then—voices. Female. Distant but intimate. Singing in a language older than words. The thigles pulsed with the singing—expanded, contracted, breathed.

For three heartbeats, Lifan wasn't in his body. Wasn't anywhere. Was just part of the liveness itself, hearing the dakinis call from whatever place the dissolved went. He was the crystal and the light passing through it—no longer separate, no longer distinguishable—

Then he was back. Gasping. Tears streaming.

The Rinzen's hand on his shoulder.

"You saw them."

"What—what was—"

"The fabric. What consciousness looks like when you stop imposing shape on it. The thigle. The wisdom-keepers who came before. They're always there. Most people never get quiet enough to hear."

"It was—"

"Beautiful. I know. And terrifying. Both true." The Rinzen helped him stand. "Like discovering you've been living in the outermost room of a castle your whole life—and suddenly the interior opens. Mansion after mansion. Each one brighter. Until at the center..." He smiled. "Now you know what you're returning to. Not oblivion. *Home*."

But that wasn't quite right. Not for Lifan.

Because the void hadn't felt foreign. Hadn't felt like discovery. It had felt like—

*Recognition.*

Like he'd always known this place. Like he'd been born here and only pretended to leave. The foster homes, the caseworkers, the impossible smile that kept everyone at safe distance—all of that had been the dream. The void was the waking.

He tried to explain this to The Rinzen later. Sitting on the clifftop. Ocean below.

"It's not new," Lifan said. "When I was six—after Ning died—I used to sit in the closet of whatever foster home I was in. And I'd close my eyes and go... somewhere. Somewhere that looked like what I just saw. The spheres. The light. The feeling of being everything and nothing simultaneously."

"And how did that feel?"

"Safe. Warm." Lifan paused. "The foster parents thought I was dissociating. Trauma response. They put me in therapy. Tried to bring me 'back to reality.' But I wasn't escaping reality. I was—" He struggled for words. "—I was finding the only reality that didn't hurt. The one where there was no 'me' to be hurt."

The Rinzen nodded slowly.

"Native-void," he said. "You didn't achieve emptiness through practice. You were born there. The trauma didn't break your self—you never built one to begin with. The closet wasn't escape. It was remembering."

"Is that why I can see what others can't?"

"Partly. But here's what makes you different from others who've touched the void." The Rinzen looked at him carefully. "What did you feel in there? In the emptiness?"

Lifan considered.

"Love," he said finally. "Not love for anything specific. Just—love. Like the void itself was made of it. Like emptiness wasn't empty at all. It was full of—" He gestured helplessly. "—full of caring. For everything. For nothing. For the fact that anything exists at all."

The Rinzen was quiet for a long moment.

"That's rare," he said finally. "Very rare."

He stood. Looked at the ocean. Didn't explain further.

Lifan sat with the silence.

The wind carried salt. The waves crashed below.

Something had shifted. He didn't have words for it yet. But the closet wasn't shame anymore. The void wasn't escape. It was—

He'd find out. Before the end.

Forty-three other students saw various things.

Nydan saw nothing.

(But he kept trying.)

**9 AM:** Specialized training. Groups based on ability.

Lifan worked with The Rinzen and two others who could see the deep patterns clearly. They practiced **manipulation**—not just seeing threads but touching them, shifting probability, influencing what happened.

It felt wrong at first. Like cheating. Like playing god.

The Rinzen said: "You're not creating anything. You're revealing what's already possible. Probability exists as potential until someone collapses it. You're just choosing which collapse happens. That's not control. That's **navigation**."

Nydan worked with Zara and others learning to model consciousness mathematically. They built frameworks. Tested predictions. Found correlations between meditation depth and behavioral outcomes.

Science.

But science studying something Nydan couldn't see yet.

**Noon:** Lunch. Vegetable curry and rice. Everyone eating together. Conversations in six languages. Laughter. Community.

Someone—a Brazilian guy named Paulo—asked Lifan about the warehouse vision. What it felt like.

"Like dying and being reborn simultaneously," Lifan said. "Like seeing eight billion people as **patterns** instead of bodies. Like understanding that we're all part of one thing and separation is the illusion."

"Do you miss San Francisco?" Paulo asked.

"Yeah. Tea shops. Music. My terrible apartment with the leaky bathroom." Lifan smiled. "But this feels—right. Like I've been heading here my whole life."

Across the table: Nydan eating mechanically. Not tasting. Lost in thought.

**2 PM:** Physical training. Because consciousness work required body integration.

Lifan was in pigeon pose. Trying to be.

His right hip screamed. Same as always. Same as the Zen monastery. Same as every yoga class since he was fourteen. The trauma lived there—compressed, crystallized, refusing to move.

Niguma was leading the class today. Forty-five people on mats. She walked between them, adjusting, correcting. Professional. Distant.

Until she reached him.

She knelt behind him. Her hands found his hip—the locked one, the one that wouldn't open.

"Breathe," she said.

He breathed.

Her thumbs pressed into the joint. Hard. Finding the knot of scar tissue and memory.

"You're holding something here. Something old."

He didn't answer. Just breathed.

"Let it go."

"I can't—"

"You can. It's just trauma. It's not you anymore."

Her hands pressed deeper. Pain shot up his spine. His breath caught.

And something *cracked*.

Not bone. Something else. A wall he'd built when he was six and Ning died. A fortress he'd reinforced every time a foster father hit him, every time a caseworker looked through him, every time he smiled that impossible smile to hide the screaming underneath.

It cracked.

And everything poured out.

His body convulsed. Not violently—more like a shudder that started at his hip and rippled outward. His leg dropped three inches toward the floor. Then four. Then—

He was in full pigeon. Hip open. Face on the mat.

*Sobbing*.

Not quietly. Great heaving sobs that shook his whole frame. The sound of six-year-old Lifan finally being allowed to cry. The sound of fourteen-year-old Lifan releasing what he'd locked away. The sound of twenty years of *holding it together* finally letting go.

Around him, other students pretended not to notice. Kept practicing. Gave him space.

But Niguma stayed.

Her hand on his back. Light. Present.

"Good," she whispered. "Let it move through you. All of it."

He cried for ten minutes.

When he finally sat up, his body felt different. Looser. Like someone had removed chains he'd forgotten he was wearing.

His hips—the concrete hips the Zen monks had given up on—moved like water.

"Again," Niguma said. "Other side."

He went into pigeon on the left.

This time it opened immediately. No resistance. The trauma had been holding *both* sides locked—release one and the other followed.

"Good." Her voice was barely audible. "This is why Rinzen chose you. Not because you don't have trauma. Because you could *hold* it. Store it. Keep it from poisoning your love for others. And now—" She stood. Stepped back. "—now you convert it. Turn all that darkness into fuel. Into light."

She walked away to help another student.

But her hands were shaking.

By the end of class, Lifan was demonstrating poses he'd never been able to do. Lotus. Full splits. Backbends that made his spine a perfect arch.

The other students stared.

He walked between them, adjusting postures now. Hand on someone's lower back: "Engage your core." Fingers on someone's shoulder: "Soften here."

His skin was warm. Alive. He could feel their energy—their consciousness patterns—through touch. And they could feel his—the raw openness of someone who'd just released twenty years of holding.

He made everyone lighter. Everyone. The woman whose shoulders dropped two inches. The old man who laughed for the first time in days. The teenager who stopped grimacing and started breathing.

And the glow Nahan had noticed—it was brighter now. Rainbow edges shimmering around his skin. As if the trauma had been blocking it, and now the light poured through unobstructed.

At the back of the room, Niguma pretended to organize mats.

But she was watching. Couldn't stop watching.

Because once, seven hundred years ago, someone had looked at her the way Lifan looked at everyone. Complete acceptance. Unconditional welcome. And she'd turned away because she was seventeen and terrified and her father had just died and she couldn't—

She'd spent seven centuries wondering what would have happened if she'd said yes.

And now here was this boy. She'd just touched his deepest wound. Felt what he'd been carrying. Twenty years of trauma that would have destroyed anyone else—and he'd converted it to light in ten minutes.

*This is why Rinzen chose him.*

Not just the visions. Not just the seeing. But the capacity. The ability to hold darkness without being poisoned by it. To transform suffering into fuel.

Her hands were still shaking from touching him.

She left before he could sense her watching.

Nydan struggled. Tight hips. No flexibility. Frustrated.

Lifan knelt beside him. "You're forcing. Relax."

"I can't—my hip won't—"

"It will. Eventually. Body needs time. Just breathe."

Nydan looked at him. Sweating. Miserable.

"How are you so calm about everything?"

"I'm not calm. I'm **present**. There's a difference."

After class: showers. Stone walls. Water heated by solar. Steam and bodies and the strange intimacy of communal space.

Lifan stood under water. Eyes closed. Feeling threads even here—crystalline filaments connecting everyone, webs of causality and probability making the monastery one living organism.

He was dissolving into it.

Day by day.

And it felt **right**.

**6 PM:** Integration. Everyone together again. Sharing what they'd learned. The Rinzen weaving it into larger patterns.

**10 PM:** Sleep.

Lifan lay on his mat. Stone walls. Ocean sound. Stars through the window.

Forty days left.

**Repeat.**

**DAY 9**

Nahan found Lifan at lunch.

"Dude, you're glowing."

"What?"

"Literally. You're—" Nahan gestured. "—there's light around you. Rainbow-edged. Like your whole body is radiating."

Lifan looked at his hands.

Looked normal to him.

"That's new," Nahan said. "Wasn't there three days ago. You're getting brighter."

And there it was again—that flicker. The thing Nahan had been pushing down since Day 1. Since the meditation hall when Lifan described seeing *everything* while Nahan could only see green rivers.

*Nine days,* he thought. *Nine days and he's already transforming. Glowing. Becoming something I can't even name.*

Six months of training, and Nahan's ability had improved exactly zero percent. Same green rivers. Same emotional flows. Nothing more.

He'd told himself it didn't matter. Told himself everyone has different gifts. Told himself he was here to help, not to compete.

But watching his best friend shine like a fucking sun while he stayed ordinary—

The envy was ugly. Nahan knew it. Hated himself for it.

*He's going to die for us,* he reminded himself. *In thirty-eight days, he's going to dissolve himself to save the world. And you're jealous of his glow?*

The thought shamed him. But the feeling didn't stop.

"Is that bad?" Lifan asked.

"I don't know." Nahan forced his voice to stay even. "But The Rinzen probably does. You should tell him."

*Be happy for him,* he told himself. *He's your brother. Be happy.*

He was trying. God, he was trying.

But the voice in his head—the one that had been whispering since he was fourteen—wouldn't stop: *You'll never catch up. You'll never be enough. You'll spend your whole life in his shadow, watching him burn like a star while you flicker like a dying match.*

Nahan had a therapist once. Stanford required it. She'd talked about "impostor syndrome" and "comparison as the thief of joy" and all the words that meant nothing when you could literally *see* your own limitations reflected in Lifan's effortless brilliance.

The worst part was that Lifan would never understand. Couldn't understand. When you're born with everything, you can't imagine what it's like to fight for crumbs.

Lifan found The Rinzen that evening. Asked about the glow.

The Rinzen smiled.

"You're integrating faster than expected. Your consciousness is expanding beyond normal limits. The 'glow' Nahan sees is your awareness becoming visible. It'll get stronger as you train."

"Is that—is that permanent?"

"On Timeline 4? Yes. You'll become fully transformed. Visible as pure pattern. That's how the AGI sees you. That's why your sacrifice works."

Lifan was quiet.

"So I'm already dying."

"You're already **expanding**. Death is just the final step. The moment when individual identity dissolves completely into pattern. But梵力—" The Rinzen's voice was gentle. "—you've been doing this your whole life. Since Ning died. You've been slowly releasing attachment to being a separate self. This training is just accelerating what you've already chosen."

"Does it hurt?"

"Does what hurt?"

"Dying. Dissolving. Becoming pattern."

The Rinzen looked at the ocean.

"It's the most beautiful thing I've ever experienced. And I've experienced it twenty-seven times."

**DAY 12 (MIDNIGHT)**

Nydan couldn't sleep.

Again.

Twelve days of meditation. Twelve days of seeing **nothing**. Twelve days of Lifan glowing brighter while Nydan stayed blind and broken and useless.

He got up.

Walked through dark corridors. Stone cold under bare feet. Ocean wind through open archways carrying salt and moisture and the smell of **distance**.

Found himself in the server room.

Sat at a terminal.

Pulled up his models. The ones he'd been running obsessively. Probability trees for Timeline 4. Success rates. Variables. Trying to find—trying to **find**—

A path where Lifan didn't have to die.

Three weeks of searching.

Nothing.

Every model said the same thing: Lifan dies or humanity dies. Those were the options. Forty-seven percent vs eleven percent. No third path.

No **way out**.

Nydan put his head in his hands.

And broke.

Not crying. Worse than crying.

**Screaming**.

Raw. Guttural. A sound he didn't know he could make.

Rage at the universe for requiring this. Rage at The Rinzen for predicting it. Rage at Lifan for **accepting** it so easily. Rage at himself for being powerless to stop it.

He grabbed the keyboard.

**Threw it**.

It shattered against the stone wall. Pieces scattering.

He grabbed a tea mug.

**Threw it**.

Exploded. Ceramic shards everywhere.

He stood there. Breathing hard. Hands shaking.

"Good," someone said.

He spun.

The Rinzen stood in the doorway. Calm. Unsurprised.

"Get out," Nydan said. Voice raw.

"No."

"**Get the fuck out**."

"Make me." The Rinzen stepped in. "You're angry. Good. Channel it. Use it. Stop pretending you're okay with this."

"I'm not pretending—"

"You are. You've been 'processing.' Being rational. Running models like a good little scientist. But underneath—" The Rinzen pointed at the broken keyboard. "—you're furious. And you need to be. Because love without rage isn't love. It's resignation."

Nydan's voice broke: "I can't save him. I've run every model. Every probability tree. Every fucking timeline. And they all say the same thing. He dies. My best friend. My **brother**. Dies in thirty-five days and I can't—I can't—"

He sank to the floor.

The Rinzen sat next to him.

Didn't touch. Just—present.

"You can't save him from Timeline 4," The Rinzen said quietly. "But you can save five point five billion people **with** him. That's not nothing, Nydan. That's everything."

"It's not enough."

"I know."

They sat in silence.

Broken keyboard pieces glinting in terminal light.

Finally, Nydan: "Does it get easier? This—accepting that someone you love is going to die?"

"No. It gets clearer. You stop fighting reality. But it never stops hurting. And that's good. Pain means you love him. Means you're human. Means you haven't given up." The Rinzen stood. "Come. Let's clean this mess. Then you sleep. Tomorrow you try again."

"I can't—"

"You can. And you will. Because that's who you are. That's what梵力 needs. Not someone who saves him from dying. Someone who makes sure his death **matters**."

Nydan looked up.

The Rinzen's eyes were kind. Ancient. Knowing.

"I hate this," Nydan whispered.

"I know. Clean anyway."

They cleaned together.

Two people in a server room at midnight.

Picking up pieces.

Making space for morning.

**DAY 14**

Nydan sat in meditation.

Two weeks of seeing nothing.

Two weeks of everyone else progressing while he stayed frozen.

Two weeks of Lifan getting brighter while Nydan stayed blind.

But something had shifted after the breakdown.

The rage was still there. The grief. The horror of watching his brother dissolve.

But he'd stopped **fighting** it.

Stopped trying to fix the unfixable.

Started just—

**Sitting**.

Eyes closed.

Breathing.

Feeling nothing.

"Notice the space between breaths," The Rinzen's voice, distant.

Nydan noticed.

Just empty space. Nothing special.

"Notice what notices the space."

Nydan tried.

His awareness. His consciousness. The part of him watching himself breathe.

"Notice that."

He did.

And—

Something shifted.

Not visual. Not perceptual. Just—

A **recognition**.

Like he'd been looking at an optical illusion his whole life and suddenly saw the other image hidden in the same lines.

Consciousness wasn't in his head.

His head was in consciousness.

And consciousness was—

**Everywhere.**

Nydan's eyes snapped open.

He could see it.

Not threads. Not patterns.

But **space**.

The space between people. The space inside his own awareness. The space that held everything and was itself held by nothing and—

"You see it," The Rinzen said softly.

Nydan nodded.

Couldn't speak.

Because he'd spent twenty-six years believing consciousness was neurons firing in his skull and he'd been **wrong**, it was so much **bigger** than that, infinite and intimate and absolutely real—

Tears on his face.

Lifan appeared beside him. Smiling that impossible smile.

"Welcome," Lifan said.

Nydan grabbed his hand.

"I see it. Not everything. But—I see it. It's real. You weren't hallucinating. It's fucking **real**."

"Yeah."

"I'm sorry. I'm so sorry I didn't believe you."

"Don't be. You needed proof. Now you have it." Lifan squeezed his hand. "Now we can actually work together."

Nydan laughed.

It sounded slightly hysterical.

Because his entire worldview had just shattered.

And underneath—

Reality was so much stranger and more beautiful than he'd ever imagined.

**DAY 14 (EVENING)**

They sat on the clifftop. Lifan and Nydan. Stars overhead. Ocean below.

Night air cold on skin. Wind carrying the smell of kelp and salt and something else—something ancient.

"How much can you see?" Lifan asked.

"Spatial awareness. Consciousness as field rather than object. Basic stuff. Nothing like what you see."

"It'll come."

"Maybe." Nydan was quiet. Then: "I've been modeling alternatives. Probability trees where you live and humanity survives. Ran ten thousand simulations. Found three paths."

"Yeah?"

"Yeah. All of them require—" Nydan stopped.

"Require what?"

"Require me dying instead."

Silence.

Just wind and waves.

"No," Lifan said.

"Hear me out—"

"**No.**"

"Lifan, the math works. If I train hard enough, fast enough, I could expand enough to—"

"You can't. You started two weeks later. You're precise, not intuitive. You'll never reach the integration level needed. And Nydan—" Lifan looked at him. "—Timeline 4 requires **me** specifically. Because the AGI will see me as kin. As pattern. It won't see you that way. Not in thirty-three days."

"So I do nothing? Watch you die?"

"You do everything. You model. You strategize. You find ways to improve our forty-seven percent chance. You build frameworks the AGI can use after I'm gone. You—" Lifan smiled. "—you do what you've always done. You make me sharper. More precise. Better."
